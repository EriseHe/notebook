> Chapter 7.3 is the point where electrodynamics becomes a complete field theory.
> Before Maxwell, we had Gauss’s law, $\nabla \cdot B = 0$, Faraday’s law, and the old Ampère law. The problem was that the old Ampère law fails for time-dependent situations, especially when charge accumulates, such as in a charging capacitor. Maxwell fixed this by adding the displacement current term. The result is the full set of Maxwell’s equations.

### 7.3.1 Electrodynamics before Maxwell

The pre-Maxwell equations were:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot E &= \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}
&&\text{(Gauss's law)},\\
\nabla \cdot B &= 0,\\
\nabla \times E &= -\frac{\partial B}{\partial t}
&&\text{(Faraday's law)},\\
\nabla \times B &= \mu_0 J
&&\text{(old Ampère law)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

#### Why this is inconsistent

Take the divergence of the curl equations. For Faraday:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times E)
&= \nabla \cdot \left(-\frac{\partial B}{\partial t}\right)\\
0 &= -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \cdot B),
\end{aligned}
$$

which is fine because $\nabla \cdot B = 0$. But for old Ampère:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times B)
&= \mu_0 \nabla \cdot J,\\
0 &= \mu_0 \nabla \cdot J.
\end{aligned}
$$
So this would force
$$
\nabla \cdot J = 0,
$$
which is only true for steady currents. Hence the old Ampère law cannot be correct in general time-dependent electrodynamics.

#### Capacitor paradox

For a charging capacitor, if we apply Ampère’s law in integral form,
$$
\oint B \cdot dl = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}},
$$
then the answer depends on which surface spans the same loop:

- a flat surface cutting the wire gives $I_{\text{enc}} = I$,
- a bulging surface between capacitor plates gives $I_{\text{enc}} = 0$.

So the old Ampère law makes $I_{\text{enc}}$ surface-dependent, which is unacceptable.

### 7.3.2 How Maxwell fixed Ampère’s law

Use charge conservation:
$$
\nabla \cdot J = -\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}.
$$
Then use Gauss’s law:
$$
\rho = \epsilon_0 \nabla \cdot E.
$$
So
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot J
&= -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\epsilon_0 \nabla \cdot E)\\
&= -\nabla \cdot \left(\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}\right).
\end{aligned}
$$
This suggests correcting Ampère’s law by replacing $J$ with
$$
J + \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}
$$
Therefore the corrected equation is
$$
\nabla \times B
=
\mu_0 J + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}.
$$
This extra term is called the **displacement current density**:
$$
J_d \equiv \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}.
$$
#### Integral form

The corrected Ampère-Maxwell law is
$$
\oint B \cdot dl
=
\mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}
+
\mu_0 \epsilon_0 \int \frac{\partial E}{\partial t} \cdot da.
$$
This resolves the charging capacitor paradox:

- on the wire-cutting surface, the conduction current contributes;
- on the balloon surface between the plates, the displacement current contributes.

Both surfaces give the same magnetic field.

#### Key idea

Faraday says:
$$
\text{changing } B \Rightarrow \text{induces } E.
$$

Maxwell’s correction says:
$$
\text{changing } E \Rightarrow \text{induces } B.
$$

This symmetry is one of the deep structural ideas of electrodynamics.


> [!remark]
> In some time-dependent situations, a nonzero conduction current can be exactly canceled by displacement current, so the magnetic field can still be zero by symmetry.

### 7.3.3 Maxwell’s equations

The finished Maxwell equations are:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot E &= \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0},
\\
\nabla \cdot B &= 0,
\\
\nabla \times E &= -\frac{\partial B}{\partial t},
\\
\nabla \times B &= \mu_0 J + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{Maxwell}
$$

Together with the Lorentz force law,
$$
F = q(E + v \times B),
$$
they summarize classical electrodynamics.

#### Continuity equation from Maxwell

Applying $\nabla \cdot$ to Ampère-Maxwell gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
0
&= \mu_0 \nabla \cdot J + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \cdot E)\\
&= \mu_0 \nabla \cdot J + \mu_0 \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t},
\end{aligned}
$$
hence
$$
\nabla \cdot J = -\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}.
$$

So charge conservation is built into Maxwell’s equations.

#### Alternative source-emphasizing form

It is often conceptually cleaner to write all field terms on the left and all source terms on the right:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot E &= \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0},
\\
\nabla \cdot B &= 0,
\\
\nabla \times E + \frac{\partial B}{\partial t} &= 0,
\\
\nabla \times B - \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t} &= \mu_0 J.
\end{aligned}
$$

This emphasizes that the ultimate sources are $\rho$ and $J$.

## 7.3.4 Magnetic charge

In vacuum, where $\rho = 0$ and $J = 0$, Maxwell’s equations look very symmetric:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot E &= 0,
&
\nabla \times E &= -\frac{\partial B}{\partial t},
\\
\nabla \cdot B &= 0,
&
\nabla \times B &= \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

This suggests asking: what if magnetic charge existed? Then one would write
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot E &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_0}\rho_e,
\\
\nabla \cdot B &= \mu_0 \rho_m,
\\
\nabla \times E &= -\mu_0 J_m - \frac{\partial B}{\partial t},
\\
\nabla \times B &= \mu_0 J_e + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Then both electric and magnetic charges would satisfy continuity equations:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot J_e &= -\frac{\partial \rho_e}{\partial t},
\\
\nabla \cdot J_m &= -\frac{\partial \rho_m}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!remark] Physical conclusion
> This would be mathematically elegant, but experimentally no magnetic monopoles have been found. So, as far as classical electrodynamics goes,
>$$
\rho_m = 0, \qquad J_m = 0.
>$$
> That is why electric fields have monopole sources, but magnetic fields do not.

### 7.3.5 Maxwell’s equations in matter

Inside matter, it is useful to separate **free** charge/current from **bound** charge/current.

#### Bound sources

Electric polarization $P$ produces bound charge density
$$
\rho_b = -\nabla \cdot P.
$$

Magnetization $M$ produces bound current density
$$
J_b = \nabla \times M.
$$

If the polarization changes with time, there is also a **polarization current**
$$
J_p = \frac{\partial P}{\partial t}.
$$

This is important: $J_p$ is not the same thing as $J_b$.

- $J_b$ comes from magnetization,
- $J_p$ comes from changing electric polarization.

#### Total charge and current

Thus,
$$
\rho = \rho_f + \rho_b = \rho_f - \nabla \cdot P,
$$
and
$$
J = J_f + J_b + J_p
= J_f + \nabla \times M + \frac{\partial P}{\partial t}.
$$

#### Define $D$ and $H$

Introduce the auxiliary fields
$$
D \equiv \epsilon_0 E + P,
$$
and
$$
H \equiv \frac{1}{\mu_0}B - M.
$$

Then Maxwell’s equations become

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot D &= \rho_f,
\\
\nabla \cdot B &= 0,
\\
\nabla \times E &= -\frac{\partial B}{\partial t},
\\
\nabla \times H &= J_f + \frac{\partial D}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

These are not more fundamental than the vacuum-style equations; they are just more convenient in matter because they isolate the free sources.

#### Linear media

For linear materials,
$$
P = \epsilon_0 \chi_e E,
\qquad
M = \chi_m H.
$$

Therefore,
$$
D = \epsilon E,
\qquad
H = \frac{1}{\mu} B,
$$
where
$$
\epsilon = \epsilon_0(1+\chi_e),
\qquad
\mu = \mu_0(1+\chi_m).
$$

In this context, one also writes the displacement current as
$$
J_d \equiv \frac{\partial D}{\partial t}.
$$


## 7.3.6 Boundary conditions

At an interface between two media, or across a surface carrying free surface charge density $\sigma_f$ or free surface current density $K_f$, the fields may jump.

Notation:

- $\perp$ means component normal to the boundary,
- $\parallel$ means component tangent to the boundary,
- “above” and “below” refer to the two sides of the interface,
- $\hat n$ is the unit normal pointing from below to above.

#### General boundary conditions

From Maxwell’s equations in matter:
$$
\begin{align}
D_{\perp}^{\text{above}} - D_{\perp}^{\text{below}}  & = \sigma_f \\

B_{\perp}^{\text{above}} - B_{\perp}^{\text{below}}  & = 0\\

E_{\parallel}^{\text{above}} - E_{\parallel}^{\text{below}}  & = 0 \\

H_{\parallel}^{\text{above}} - H_{\parallel}^{\text{below}}  & = K_f \times \hat n.
\end{align}
$$
These are the basic jump conditions.

### In linear media

Using $D=\epsilon E$ and $H=B/\mu$, these become

$$
\begin{align}
\epsilon_{\text{above}} E_{\perp}^{\text{above}}
-
\epsilon_{\text{below}} E_{\perp}^{\text{below}}
 & =
\sigma_f, \\
B_{\perp}^{\text{above}} - B_{\perp}^{\text{below}} &  = 0, \\
E_{\parallel}^{\text{above}} - E_{\parallel}^{\text{below}}  & = 0, \\
\frac{1}{\mu_{\text{above}}}B_{\parallel}^{\text{above}}
-
\frac{1}{\mu_{\text{below}}}B_{\parallel}^{\text{below}}
 & =
K_f \times \hat n.
\end{align}
$$

#### If there is no free surface charge or current

If $\sigma_f = 0$ and $K_f = 0$, then

$$
\epsilon_{\text{above}} E_{\perp}^{\text{above}}
=
\epsilon_{\text{below}} E_{\perp}^{\text{below}},
$$

$$
B_{\perp}^{\text{above}} = B_{\perp}^{\text{below}},
$$

$$
E_{\parallel}^{\text{above}} = E_{\parallel}^{\text{below}},
$$

$$
\frac{1}{\mu_{\text{above}}}B_{\parallel}^{\text{above}}
=
\frac{1}{\mu_{\text{below}}}B_{\parallel}^{\text{below}}.
$$

These are the boundary conditions used later for reflection and refraction.

## What to remember most

### 1. The old Ampère law fails in time-dependent situations
The inconsistency is
$$
\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times B) = 0
\quad \text{but} \quad
\nabla \cdot J \neq 0
$$
in general.

### 2. Maxwell’s correction is
$$
\mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}.
$$

### 3. The full Maxwell system is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot E &= \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0},
\\
\nabla \cdot B &= 0,
\\
\nabla \times E &= -\frac{\partial B}{\partial t},
\\
\nabla \times B &= \mu_0 J + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

### 4. In matter, use
$$
D = \epsilon_0 E + P,
\qquad
H = \frac{1}{\mu_0}B - M.
$$

### 5. In matter, Maxwell becomes
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot D &= \rho_f,
\\
\nabla \cdot B &= 0,
\\
\nabla \times E &= -\frac{\partial B}{\partial t},
\\
\nabla \times H &= J_f + \frac{\partial D}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

### 6. Boundary conditions are indispensable
They tell you exactly which components jump and by how much at interfaces.

## One-line intuition

Electrostatics and magnetostatics are not separate worlds anymore. In full electrodynamics,

$$
\text{changing } B \leftrightarrow \text{induces } E,
\qquad
\text{changing } E \leftrightarrow \text{induces } B,
$$

and Maxwell’s equations are the compact mathematical statement of that mutual coupling.
