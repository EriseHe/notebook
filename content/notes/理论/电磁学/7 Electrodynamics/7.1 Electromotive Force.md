## Big picture

Chapter 7.1 answers one practical question:

> What actually drives charges around a circuit?

The structure is:

1. **Local material response**: how current density $\mathbf J$ responds to force per unit charge $\mathbf f$
2. **Circuit-level driving**: how a source produces an emf $\mathcal E$
3. **Motion in a magnetic field**: how moving conductors generate emf even when the “source” is magnetic

For exams, the main skill is to know **which formula belongs to which physical mechanism**.

---

## 7.1.1 Ohm's Law

### Core formulas

#### Most general local form in this section
$$
\mathbf J=\sigma \mathbf f
$$

Meaning:
- $\mathbf J$ = current density
- $\sigma$ = conductivity
- $\mathbf f$ = **force per unit charge**

This is the real starting point.

---

#### Electromagnetic driving force
$$
\mathbf J=\sigma(\mathbf E+\mathbf v\times \mathbf B)
$$

Use this when the current is driven by electromagnetic forces and the charges may be moving through a magnetic field.

---

#### Ordinary Ohm's law
$$
\mathbf J=\sigma \mathbf E
$$

Use this when the magnetic term is negligible, which is the standard case in ordinary conductors/circuits.

This is the formula most problems mean by “Ohm’s law,” but the book emphasizes that it is really a **special case** of the more general relation above.

---

#### Resistivity
$$
\rho=\frac{1}{\sigma}
$$

Be careful:
- here $\rho$ means **resistivity**
- not charge density

---

#### Circuit form of Ohm's law
$$
V=IR
$$

This is the global/circuit version.

Meaning:
- $V$ = potential difference between electrodes/ends
- $I$ = total current
- $R$ = resistance

---

#### Current from current density
$$
I=\int \mathbf J\cdot d\mathbf a
$$

Use this whenever the current density is known, or when symmetry makes $\mathbf J$ easy to integrate.

---

#### Resistance of a uniform cylindrical resistor
For a resistor of length $L$, cross-sectional area $A$, conductivity $\sigma$:
$$
I=\sigma \frac{A}{L}V
$$
so
$$
R=\frac{L}{\sigma A}
$$

This is the standard “wire resistor” formula.

Use when:
- material is homogeneous
- cross section is constant
- field/current is uniform along the wire

---

#### Resistance of coaxial cylindrical geometry
For two coaxial cylinders of radii $a,b$, length $L$, conductivity $\sigma$:
$$
I=\frac{2\pi \sigma L}{\ln(b/a)}V
$$
so
$$
R=\frac{\ln(b/a)}{2\pi \sigma L}
$$

Use when current flows **radially** through the material between coaxial conductors.

This is an exam favorite because it tests whether you recognize that the area for current flow changes with radius.

---

#### Steady current in uniform ohmic medium
For steady currents and uniform conductivity,
$$
\nabla\cdot \mathbf E=\frac{1}{\sigma}\nabla\cdot \mathbf J=0
$$

So inside a homogeneous ohmic material carrying steady current:
- $\nabla\cdot \mathbf E=0$
- charge density in the bulk is zero
- any excess charge lives on the surface

Hence the potential satisfies Laplace's equation:
$$
\nabla^2 V=0
$$

This is extremely important for solving conductor/current problems with boundary conditions.

---

#### Joule heating law
$$
P=VI=I^2R
$$

Also,
$$
P=\frac{V^2}{R}
$$

Use this whenever the problem asks about:
- heat dissipated
- power delivered to a resistor
- energy loss in a resistive element

---

### What Ohm's law means physically

A constant electric field does **not** make the current grow forever because charges collide repeatedly inside the material.

So:
- microscopic motion is accelerated between collisions
- macroscopic result is a steady **drift velocity**
- therefore $\mathbf J$ can be proportional to $\mathbf E$

The book’s rough Drude-style estimate gives
$$
\mathbf J
=
\left(
\frac{nf\lambda q^2}{2mv_{\text{thermal}}}
\right)\mathbf E
$$

Do **not** usually use this formula computationally unless explicitly asked.
What it is good for:
- explaining why conductivity increases with charge-carrier density
- explaining why conductivity typically decreases as temperature rises

---

### What to do on an exam

If you see a conduction problem, ask:

#### Case 1: Is this a local-material law problem?
Then start from
$$
\mathbf J=\sigma \mathbf E
$$
or
$$
\mathbf J=\sigma(\mathbf E+\mathbf v\times \mathbf B)
$$

#### Case 2: Is this a geometry-to-resistance problem?
Then:
1. find $\mathbf E$ or $V$
2. get $\mathbf J=\sigma \mathbf E$
3. compute
   $$
   I=\int \mathbf J\cdot d\mathbf a
   $$
4. identify
   $$
   R=\frac{V}{I}
   $$

#### Case 3: Is this a steady-current boundary value problem?
Then use:
- $\nabla\cdot \mathbf J=0$
- uniform $\sigma \Rightarrow \nabla\cdot \mathbf E=0$
- $\mathbf E=-\nabla V$
- therefore
  $$
  \nabla^2 V=0
  $$

This lets you use all the Chapter 3 Laplace-equation machinery.

---

### Example logic you should remember

#### Example 7.1: uniform cylindrical resistor
Because the ends are equipotentials and the geometry is uniform, the field inside is uniform:
$$
\mathbf E=-\frac{V_0}{L}\hat{\mathbf z}
$$
and so
$$
I=\sigma \frac{A}{L}V
$$

#### Example 7.2: coaxial cylinders
Use radial electrostatic field and integrate current across a cylindrical surface:
$$
I=\int \mathbf J\cdot d\mathbf a
$$
Then relate $\lambda$ to $V$ using the potential difference.

#### Example 7.3: proving the field is uniform
Technique:
1. use
   $$
   \nabla^2 V=0
   $$
2. impose boundary conditions on the ends and side wall
3. guess the simple linear solution
4. invoke uniqueness theorem

That is a very exam-worthy solution style.

## 7.1.2 Electromotive Force

### Core idea

In a real circuit, current is uniform all the way around, even though the obvious “source” may only be inside the battery.

Why?

Because charge pileups create an electrostatic field that rapidly adjusts the flow until the current becomes uniform.

So there are **two forces per unit charge** in the circuit:
$$
\mathbf f=\mathbf f_s+\mathbf E
$$

where:
- $\mathbf f_s$ = source force per unit charge
- $\mathbf E$ = electrostatic force per unit charge from redistributed charge

---

### Definition of emf
$$
\mathcal E \equiv \oint \mathbf f\cdot d\mathbf l
=
\oint \mathbf f_s\cdot d\mathbf l
$$

Why can we replace $\mathbf f$ by $\mathbf f_s$?

Because for electrostatic fields,
$$
\oint \mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf l=0
$$

So only the non-electrostatic source part contributes to the loop integral.

---

### What emf means

$\mathcal E$ is **not** a force.

It is:
- line integral of force per unit charge around the circuit
- often interpreted as work done per unit charge by the source

So dimensions:
$$
[\mathcal E]=\text{volts}
$$

not newtons.

---

### Ideal source relation

Inside an ideal source, such as an ideal battery, the net force on charges is zero, so
$$
\mathbf E=-\mathbf f_s
$$

Then the terminal voltage is
$$
V=-\int_a^b \mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf l
=
\int_a^b \mathbf f_s\cdot d\mathbf l
=
\oint \mathbf f_s\cdot d\mathbf l
=
\mathcal E
$$

So for an **ideal battery**:
$$
V=\mathcal E
$$

That is the clean relation to remember.

---

### Meaning in circuit language

A battery does two things:
1. inside the battery, non-electrostatic forces push charges “uphill” in potential
2. outside the battery, the electrostatic field drives current through the rest of the circuit

So:
- inside source: $\mathbf f_s$ is essential
- outside source: $\mathbf E$ usually does the pushing

---

### Exam traps

#### Trap 1: emf is not the same thing as electric field
$\mathcal E$ is a loop integral.
$\mathbf E$ is a field.

Do not confuse
$$
\mathcal E
\quad \text{with} \quad
\mathbf E
$$

---

#### Trap 2: current uniformity comes from small surface charge rearrangements
If a problem asks
> Why is the current the same everywhere in the loop?

the answer is:
- transient charge buildup creates an electrostatic field
- that field redistributes the driving so the current equalizes

---

#### Trap 3: a purely electrostatic arrangement does not produce emf around a closed loop
Because
$$
\oint \mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf l=0
$$

So if a loop is partly inside a capacitor field and partly outside, the net emf around the whole closed loop is still
$$
\mathcal E=0
$$

This is exactly the kind of “trick question” the book warns about.

---

### How to use emf on an exam

If the problem asks for emf from a battery/source:
1. identify the non-electrostatic driving region
2. compute
   $$
   \mathcal E=\oint \mathbf f_s\cdot d\mathbf l
   $$
3. if resistance is given, then use circuit Ohm’s law
   $$
   I=\frac{\mathcal E}{R}
   $$

For an ideal battery, you can usually jump directly to:
$$
V=\mathcal E
$$

## 7.1.3 Motional Emf

### Core idea

A conductor moving through a magnetic field can develop an emf.

This is the generator idea.

The charges in the moving conductor feel magnetic force, which separates charge and drives current.

---

### Basic motional-emf formula
$$
\mathcal E=\oint \mathbf f_{\text{mag}}\cdot d\mathbf l
$$

where $\mathbf f_{\text{mag}}$ is the magnetic force **per unit charge** on the mobile charges.

In simple rigid-wire problems,
$$
\mathbf f_{\text{mag}}=\mathbf v\times \mathbf B
$$
for the moving segment.

More generally, if the charge has actual velocity $\mathbf w$,
$$
\mathbf f_{\text{mag}}=\mathbf w\times \mathbf B
$$

---

### Standard rectangular-loop result
For a bar/segment of length $h$ moving with speed $v$ perpendicular to a uniform $B$:
$$
\mathcal E=vBh
$$

Use this immediately when:
- one segment of length $h$ cuts magnetic field lines
- motion is perpendicular to both the wire segment and $\mathbf B$

---

### Magnetic flux
$$
\Phi\equiv \int \mathbf B\cdot d\mathbf a
$$

---

### Flux rule for motional emf
$$
\mathcal E=-\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

This is the major shortcut formula.

Use it when:
- there is a **single, well-defined loop**
- the loop moves, rotates, stretches, or distorts continuously
- you can compute the changing magnetic flux more easily than the magnetic force integral

---

### Sign convention

There are two sign choices:
- direction around loop in $\oint \cdots d\mathbf l$
- orientation of $d\mathbf a$ in the flux

They must be connected by the right-hand rule:

- fingers curl in positive loop direction
- thumb gives positive area direction

If you compute
$$
\mathcal E<0
$$
it means the current flows opposite to your chosen positive loop direction.

---

### Extremely important conceptual point

The magnetic force sets up the emf, but

$$
\text{magnetic forces do no work}
$$

So who supplies the energy?

The **external agent** pulling the loop.

That is why generators require mechanical work input.

---

### Work/emf subtlety

The book stresses a subtle but important distinction:

#### To compute emf
you integrate around the loop **at one instant of time**
$$
\mathcal E=\oint \mathbf f_{\text{mag}}\cdot d\mathbf l
$$

#### To compute actual work done on a charge
you follow the charge around its true path

These are not the same path.

Yet in the rectangular-loop example, the work per unit charge done by the external pulling force turns out to equal the emf.

This is exam-worthy as a conceptual explanation.

---

### Standard rectangular-loop formulas

If the loop width inside the field is $x$, height is $h$, and field is uniform $B$, then
$$
\Phi=Bhx
$$

As the loop moves out of the field,
$$
\frac{d\Phi}{dt}=-Bhv
$$

So
$$
\mathcal E=-\frac{d\Phi}{dt}=vBh
$$

If the total circuit resistance is $R$, then
$$
I=\frac{\mathcal E}{R}=\frac{vBh}{R}
$$

---

### Faraday disk / Faraday dynamo

For a rotating conducting disk of radius $a$, angular speed $\omega$, in uniform vertical field $B$:
$$
v=\omega s
$$
so
$$
\mathbf f_{\text{mag}}=\mathbf v\times \mathbf B=\omega sB\,\hat{\mathbf s}
$$

Then
$$
\mathcal E=\int_0^a f_{\text{mag}}\,ds
=
\omega B\int_0^a s\,ds
=
\frac{\omega B a^2}{2}
$$

Hence
$$
I=\frac{\mathcal E}{R}
=
\frac{\omega B a^2}{2R}
$$

This example matters because:

- it **does** have motional emf
- but the flux rule is not convenient/directly applicable, since current spreads over the disk and there is no single simple wire path

So this is a classic example of:

> use direct motional-emf integral, not flux rule

---

### When the flux rule is safe

Use
$$
\mathcal E=-\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$
when the current path is a single loop and remains well defined.

---

### When the flux rule is dangerous

Be cautious if there are:
- switches
- sliding contacts
- multiple possible current paths
- extended conductors where current spreads through a 2D region
- strange geometries like the Faraday disk

The book explicitly warns that flux can change without there being a motional emf, if no conductor is actually moving through the magnetic field in the required way.

## One-page exam checklist

### If you see a resistor/material problem
Start from
$$
\mathbf J=\sigma\mathbf E
$$
then use
$$
I=\int \mathbf J\cdot d\mathbf a
$$
and
$$
V=IR
$$

---

### If you see a “why is current the same everywhere?” question

Use
$$
\mathcal E=\oint \mathbf f_s\cdot d\mathbf l

Use

$$

## High-yield distinctions to memorize

### 1. Local vs circuit Ohm's law
Local:
$$
\mathbf J=\sigma\mathbf E
$$
Circuit:
$$
V=IR
$$

### 2. emf vs voltage
- **Voltage** = potential difference between two points
- **emf** = loop integral of source force per unit charge

For an ideal battery they are equal:
$$
V=\mathcal E
$$

### 3. Electrostatic emf vs motional emf
- Electrostatic field alone satisfies
  $$
  \oint \mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf l=0
  $$
- Motional emf comes from moving conductors in magnetic field:
  $$
  \mathcal E=\oint \mathbf f_{\text{mag}}\cdot d\mathbf l
  $$

### 4. Magnetic force vs energy transfer
- magnetic force establishes charge separation/current direction
- external mechanical agent supplies the energy
