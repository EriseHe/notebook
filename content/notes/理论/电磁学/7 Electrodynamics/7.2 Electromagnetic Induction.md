## Big picture

Chapter 7.2 is about one theme:

> a changing magnetic flux produces an emf

But on exams, there are actually **three different kinds of questions** hidden under that sentence:

1. **Find the induced emf/current in a loop**
2. **Find the induced electric field in space**
3. **Find inductance or magnetic energy**

The main difficulty is not the algebra.  
It is identifying **which object you are solving for**:

- emf $\mathcal E$
- electric field $\mathbf E$
- inductance $L$ or $M$
- magnetic energy $W$

So the first thing to ask is:

### What is the unknown?

- If the problem asks for **emf**, **induced current**, or **direction of current**, start with flux
- If the problem asks for the **electric field itself in space**, start with the integral form of Faraday's law
- If the problem gives **current changing in one loop/coil** and asks about another loop, think **mutual inductance**
- If the problem gives a circuit/coil and asks for **stored energy** or **work to establish current**, think **inductance + magnetic energy**

---

## 7.2.1 Faraday's Law

### Core formulas

$$
\mathcal E = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

$$
\Phi = \int \mathbf B \cdot d\mathbf a
$$

For a stationary loop in a time-dependent field,
$$
\mathcal E = \oint \mathbf E \cdot d\mathbf l = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

And in differential form,
$$
\nabla \times \mathbf E = -\frac{\partial \mathbf B}{\partial t}
$$

---

### What this means

If the magnetic flux through a loop changes, there is an induced emf around the loop.

The flux can change because:

1. the loop moves
2. the magnet/source moves
3. the magnetic field strength changes
4. the loop area changes
5. the loop orientation changes

Numerically, all of these lead to
$$
\mathcal E = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

But Griffiths distinguishes two mechanisms:

- **motional emf**: charges in a moving conductor feel magnetic force
- **Faraday-induced electric field**: a changing $\mathbf B$ creates an electric field

So on an exam:

### If the conductor is physically moving through a static $B$
that is conceptually **motional emf** from §7.1.3

### If the loop is stationary but $B(t)$ changes
that is **Faraday's law proper** in §7.2.1

The formula for emf is the same, but the mechanism is different.

---

### Exact exam triggers: when to use
$$
\mathcal E = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

Use it immediately when the problem gives:

- a **loop** or **coil**
- a magnetic field $B$ that depends on **time**, **position**, or both
- a loop whose **area** or **orientation** changes with time
- words like **induced emf**, **induced current**, **flux through the loop**, **magnet entering/leaving a ring**
- a graph of $\Phi(t)$ or $B(t)$ and asks for $\mathcal E(t)$

---

### Exact data patterns to look for

#### Pattern A: uniform $B(t)$ through a loop of fixed area
If the problem gives:
- loop area $A$
- uniform field normal to the loop
- $B(t)$

then
$$
\Phi = BA
$$
and
$$
\mathcal E = -A\frac{dB}{dt}
$$

If the field makes angle $\theta$ with the area vector,
$$
\Phi = BA\cos\theta
$$

---

#### Pattern B: only part of the loop is inside the field region
If the problem gives:
- a loop partly overlapping a field region
- dimensions of overlap
- motion at speed $v$

then do **not** use the full loop area

Use only the area where $B \neq 0$:
$$
\Phi = B(\text{overlap area})
$$

Then differentiate with respect to time.

This is exactly the right approach for “magnet entering/leaving loop” or “loop sliding out of field” problems.

---

#### Pattern C: graph problem
If the problem gives a graph of $\Phi(t)$, then
$$
\mathcal E(t) = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

So:

- flat $\Phi(t)$ graph $\Rightarrow \mathcal E = 0$
- positive slope $\Rightarrow \mathcal E < 0$
- negative slope $\Rightarrow \mathcal E > 0$

So the emf graph is the **negative slope** of the flux graph.

This is exactly the technique in the moving-magnet example.

---

#### Pattern D: asks for induced current
If resistance $R$ is given, then after finding emf use
$$
I = \frac{\mathcal E}{R}
$$

So if the problem gives:
- $B(t)$
- loop geometry
- resistance $R$

the order is:
$$
\Phi \longrightarrow \mathcal E = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt} \longrightarrow I=\frac{\mathcal E}{R}
$$

---

### Direction: how to use Lenz's law correctly

Lenz's law says:

> the induced current flows so that its magnetic field opposes the change in flux

Use this 4-step method:

#### Step 1
Choose an area direction $\hat{\mathbf n}$

#### Step 2
Compute whether the flux $\Phi$ is increasing or decreasing

#### Step 3
Use
$$
\mathcal E = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$
to determine the sign

#### Step 4
Convert that sign to current direction using the right-hand rule

---

### Fast direction rules

#### If upward flux is increasing
induced current tries to create **downward** field

#### If upward flux is decreasing
induced current tries to create **upward** field

Then use right-hand rule:

- counterclockwise current $\Rightarrow$ upward field
- clockwise current $\Rightarrow$ downward field

---

### What not to do

Do **not** use
$$
\mathcal E = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$
blindly unless you know exactly what flux surface you are using and which part of the field contributes.

The most common exam mistake is using the wrong area.

---

## 7.2.2 The Induced Electric Field

### Core formulas

$$
\oint \mathbf E \cdot d\mathbf l = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf E = -\frac{\partial \mathbf B}{\partial t}
$$

For a pure Faraday field,
$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf E = 0
\qquad
\nabla \times \mathbf E = -\frac{\partial \mathbf B}{\partial t}
$$

Griffiths gives the analog of Biot–Savart:
$$
\mathbf E = -\frac{1}{4\pi}\int \frac{(\partial \mathbf B/\partial t)\times \hat{\boldsymbol{\mathcal R}}}{\mathcal R^2}\,d\tau
= -\frac{1}{4\pi}\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int \frac{\mathbf B\times \hat{\boldsymbol{\mathcal R}}}{\mathcal R^2}\,d\tau
$$

---

### What this means

A changing magnetic field creates a **circulating electric field**, even in empty space.

This is not electrostatics.

So unlike electrostatics,
$$
\oint \mathbf E \cdot d\mathbf l \neq 0
$$

That is the conceptual point you must remember.

---

### Exact exam triggers: when to solve for $\mathbf E$ instead of $\mathcal E$

Use induced-field methods when the problem asks for:

- the **electric field in space**
- force on a **stationary charge** due to changing $B$
- torque on a charged ring/wheel due to changing $B$
- the electric field at radius $s$, $r$, etc.
- field lines/direction of induced electric field
- “what is $\mathbf E(\mathbf r,t)$?” rather than “what is the emf?”

If the question asks for the field **at points in space**, then the unknown is $\mathbf E$, not just $\mathcal E$.

---

### Exact symmetry clue to look for

If the changing magnetic field has **cylindrical symmetry**, such as:

- uniform $B(t)\hat{\mathbf z}$ inside a circular region
- long solenoid with changing current
- anything axisymmetric about the $z$-axis

then the induced electric field will usually be azimuthal:
$$
\mathbf E = E_\phi(s)\,\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}
$$

Then choose a circular loop of radius $s$ and use
$$
\oint \mathbf E \cdot d\mathbf l = E(2\pi s)
$$

This is one of the most important exam patterns in the chapter.

---

### Canonical result: changing uniform $B$ in a circular region

If a uniform field $B(t)\hat{\mathbf z}$ fills a circular region of radius $a$, then by symmetry the induced field is azimuthal.

#### For $s<a$
the loop of radius $s$ encloses only the smaller circle:
$$
\Phi = \pi s^2 B(t)
$$
so
$$
E(2\pi s) = -\frac{d}{dt}(\pi s^2 B)
$$
hence
$$
\boxed{
\mathbf E = -\frac{s}{2}\frac{dB}{dt}\,\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}
\qquad (s<a)
}
$$

#### For $s>a$
the loop encloses all the magnetic field region:
$$
\Phi = \pi a^2 B(t)
$$
so
$$
E(2\pi s) = -\frac{d}{dt}(\pi a^2 B)
$$
hence
$$
\boxed{
\mathbf E = -\frac{a^2}{2s}\frac{dB}{dt}\,\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}
\qquad (s>a)
}
$$

This second formula is the natural outer-region continuation of the textbook’s inner-region example.

---

### How to know whether to use $s<a$ or $s>a$

Look at the **radius of your chosen loop** compared with the **radius of the magnetic-field region**.

- if your loop lies **inside** the field region: use enclosed area $\pi s^2$
- if your loop lies **outside**: use enclosed area $\pi a^2$

So the area in Faraday's law is always the area that actually contains magnetic flux.

---

### If the problem asks for force or torque after finding $\mathbf E$

Once the induced field is known, use ordinary electric-force formulas:

$$
d\mathbf F = dq\,\mathbf E
$$

$$
d\boldsymbol\tau = \mathbf r \times d\mathbf F
$$

This is exactly the strategy in the charged-wheel example.

So if the problem gives:
- charge density on a ring or rim
- changing magnetic field
- asks whether it rotates / asks for torque

then:
$$
\text{changing }B \longrightarrow \mathbf E_{\text{induced}} \longrightarrow \mathbf F=q\mathbf E \longrightarrow \boldsymbol\tau
$$

---

### Physical picture to memorize

Electrostatic fields begin on $+$ charge and end on $-$ charge.

Faraday-induced fields are different:
- they form **closed loops**
- they circulate around the region where $B$ is changing

That is why they can have nonzero circulation.

## 7.2.3 Inductance

## Big picture

Inductance is what happens when changing current creates changing flux, which then creates emf.

There are two versions:

- **mutual inductance**: current in loop 1 affects loop 2
- **self-inductance**: current in a loop affects itself

---

### Mutual inductance formulas

If current $I_1$ in loop 1 produces flux $\Phi_2$ through loop 2,
$$
\Phi_2 = M_{21} I_1
$$

Similarly,
$$
\Phi_1 = M_{12} I_2
$$

And Griffiths emphasizes the symmetry:
$$
M_{21}=M_{12}
$$

So we usually just write
$$
M
$$

---

### Induced emf from mutual inductance
$$
\mathcal E_2 = -\frac{d\Phi_2}{dt} = -M\frac{dI_1}{dt}
$$

This is the key usable formula.

---

### Self-inductance formulas

For a single loop or coil,
$$
\Phi = LI
$$

and therefore
$$
\mathcal E = -L\frac{dI}{dt}
$$

This is the **back emf** of an inductor.

---

### Exact exam triggers: when to use mutual inductance

Use mutual inductance when the problem gives:

- **two loops**, **two coils**, **two solenoids**, **a toroid and a wire**, etc.
- current in one changes with time
- asks for emf in the other
- uses words like **coupled coils**, **induced emf in second loop**, **flux through outer loop due to inner loop**

Then the workflow is:

1. find flux through loop 2 due to current in loop 1
2. identify
   $$
   M=\frac{\Phi_2}{I_1}
   $$
3. then use
   $$
   \mathcal E_2 = -M\frac{dI_1}{dt}
   $$

---

### Exact exam triggers: when to use self-inductance

Use self-inductance when the problem gives:

- **one coil/loop/solenoid/toroid**
- current in that same object changes
- asks for back emf
- asks for stored energy
- asks for inductance $L$

Then use
$$
\Phi = LI
$$
or
$$
\mathcal E=-L\frac{dI}{dt}
$$

---

### Important sign meaning

In
$$
\mathcal E = -L\frac{dI}{dt}
$$
the minus sign means:

> the induced emf opposes the change in current

So:

- if current is increasing, back emf opposes the increase
- if current is decreasing, back emf tries to keep it going

---

### Exact geometry clues

#### If the problem gives
- number of turns $N$
- turn density $n$
- area of turns
- field inside solenoid/toroid from Chapter 5

then the usual method is:

1. find magnetic field $B$ from Chapter 5
2. compute flux through **one** turn
3. multiply by number of turns if needed
4. use
   $$
   \Phi=LI
   $$
   or
   $$
   \Phi=MI
   $$

---

### Standard nested-solenoid result

For a short solenoid of length $l$, radius $a$, turn density $n_1$, inside a very long solenoid of turn density $n_2$,
$$
M=\mu_0\pi a^2 n_1 n_2 l
$$

This is a very useful pattern if the geometry matches “small solenoid inside long solenoid.”

---

### Standard toroid self-inductance

For a toroidal coil with rectangular cross section, inner radius $a$, outer radius $b$, height $h$, and total turns $N$,
$$
L=\frac{\mu_0 N^2 h}{2\pi}\ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)
$$

Use this when the problem literally gives toroid dimensions $a,b,h,N$.

---

### Fast method vs slow method

#### Direct inductance method
Use
$$
\Phi = LI
$$
when:
- current follows a clear wire path
- flux through the circuit is easy to define

#### Energy method
Use
$$
W=\frac12 LI^2
$$
to define $L$ when:
- current is spread over a surface or volume
- “which flux belongs to which path?” becomes messy
- geometry is like a coax cable or distributed current system

This is explicitly highlighted in the chapter.

---

## 7.2.4 Energy in Magnetic Fields

### Core formulas

For an inductor,
$$
W=\frac12 LI^2
$$

This is the recoverable energy needed to establish the current.

---

### More general current-distribution form
$$
W=\frac12 \int (\mathbf A\cdot \mathbf J)\,d\tau
$$

---

### Magnetic-field form
$$
W=\frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int B^2\,d\tau
$$

So the magnetic energy density is
$$
u_B=\frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}
$$

This is the formula you will use most often if the magnetic field is known in space.

---

### Exact exam triggers: when to use
$$
W=\frac12 LI^2
$$

Use this when the problem gives:

- inductance $L$
- current $I$
- asks for stored energy
- asks for work needed to build current
- asks for energy recovered when current is turned off

This is the fastest formula.

---

### Exact exam triggers: when to use
$$
u_B=\frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}
$$

Use this when the problem gives:

- magnetic field as a function of position
- a spatial region where the field exists
- asks for total stored energy
- geometry like solenoid, toroid, coax cable

Then do:
$$
W=\int u_B\,d\tau
=
\frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int B^2\,d\tau
$$

This is often easier than computing $L$ first.

---

### Canonical example: coax cable

If current $I$ flows down the inner cylinder (radius $a$) and returns on the outer cylinder (radius $b$), then between the cylinders
$$
B=\frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi s}\,\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}
$$

So
$$
u_B=\frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}
=\frac{\mu_0 I^2}{8\pi^2 s^2}
$$

and for a length $l$,
$$
W=\frac{\mu_0 I^2 l}{4\pi}\ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)
$$

Comparing with
$$
W=\frac12 LI^2
$$
gives
$$
L=\frac{\mu_0 l}{2\pi}\ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)
$$

This example is important because it shows when the **energy method** is cleaner than the direct flux method.

---

### Physical meaning

Magnetic fields do no work on charges directly.

So why does it take energy to establish a current?

Because while the current is building up, the magnetic field is changing, and that changing $B$ induces an electric field. That induced electric field is what you must work against.

So the energy is associated with the process of building the field, and in the end it is stored as magnetic-field energy.

---

## Concrete exam decision tree

## If the problem gives a loop and asks for induced emf/current

Look for:
- $B(t)$
- changing overlap area
- changing angle
- magnet moving in/out
- loop moving in field

Then use:
$$
\mathcal E=-\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

If $R$ is given:
$$
I=\frac{\mathcal E}{R}
$$

If direction is asked:
use Lenz's law

---

## If the problem gives a changing magnetic field and asks for the electric field in space

Look for:
- “find $\mathbf E$”
- stationary charges/wires
- circular symmetry
- “what is the field at radius $s$?”

Then use:
$$
\oint \mathbf E\cdot d\mathbf l = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

and choose a loop matching the symmetry

Usually for cylindrical symmetry:
$$
E(2\pi s) = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}
$$

---

## If the problem gives two loops/coils and changing current in one

Look for:
- $I_1(t)$ given
- asks emf in second loop
- asks mutual inductance

Then use:
$$
\Phi_2 = MI_1,
\qquad
\mathcal E_2 = -M\frac{dI_1}{dt}
$$

---

## If the problem gives one coil and changing current in that same coil

Look for:
- back emf
- self inductance
- stored energy

Then use:
$$
\Phi=LI,
\qquad
\mathcal E=-L\frac{dI}{dt},
\qquad
W=\frac12 LI^2
$$

---

## If the problem gives magnetic field in a region and asks stored energy

Look for:
- known $B(\mathbf r)$
- shape/volume given

Then use:
$$
u_B=\frac{B^2}{2\mu_0},
\qquad
W=\frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int B^2\,d\tau
$$

---

## Most common mistakes

### 1. Confusing emf with electric field
- emf is a loop integral
- electric field is a local vector field

### 2. Using total loop area instead of flux area
Only the part of the surface where $B$ passes through contributes to $\Phi$

### 3. Forgetting Lenz's minus sign
The induced effect opposes the change in flux/current

### 4. Using $\mathcal E=-d\Phi/dt$ when the problem actually asks for $\mathbf E$
If the unknown is a field in space, solve for $\mathbf E$ first

### 5. Using $\Phi=LI$ in a distributed-current geometry where flux is ambiguous
Then use the energy method instead
