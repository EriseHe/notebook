## 5.4.1 The Vector Potential

Just as the condition $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = \mathbf{0}$ in electrostatics permitted the introduction of a scalar potential ($V$), the fundamental fact that $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ in magnetostatics invites the introduction of a **vector potential** $\mathbf{A}$.

> [!definition] Magnetic Vector Potential ($\mathbf{A}$)
> Since the divergence of a curl is identically zero, the condition $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ is automatically satisfied if we express the magnetic field as:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}}$$



### 1. Gauge Freedom and the Coulomb Gauge
The vector potential is not unique; you can add the gradient of any scalar function $\lambda$ to $\mathbf{A}$ without altering $\mathbf{B}$, because $\nabla \times (\nabla \lambda) = \mathbf{0}$. This flexibility allows us to impose a specific constraint to simplify our equations.

**Derivation of the Coulomb Gauge Necessity:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{A} &= \mathbf{A}_0 + \nabla \lambda && \text{\scriptsize adding a gradient to an initial potential } \mathbf{A}_0 \\
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} &= \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}_0 + \nabla^2 \lambda && \text{\scriptsize taking the divergence of both sides} \\
0 &= \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}_0 + \nabla^2 \lambda && \text{\scriptsize requiring the new potential to be divergenceless} \\
\nabla^2 \lambda &= -\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}_0 && \text{\scriptsize solving this Poisson equation for } \lambda \text{ ensures the gauge condition}
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!definition] The Coulomb Gauge
> We ordinarily exploit our gauge freedom to stipulate that $\mathbf{A}$ is divergenceless:
> $$\boxed{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0}$$

---

### 2. The Poisson Equation for A
When the potential formulation is substituted into Ampère's law ($\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$), we obtain a second-order differential equation for $\mathbf{A}$.

**Derivation of the Vector Poisson Equation:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) &= \mu_0 \mathbf{J} && \text{\scriptsize substituting } \mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A} \text{ into Ampère's law} \\
\nabla(\underbrace{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}}_{0}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{A} &= \mu_0 \mathbf{J} && \text{\scriptsize applying vector identity and the Coulomb gauge} \\
-\nabla^2 \mathbf{A} &= \mu_0 \mathbf{J} && \text{\scriptsize the divergence term vanishes by choice of gauge}
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!definition] Poisson Equation for Magnetostatics
> In the Coulomb gauge, each Cartesian component of $\mathbf{A}$ satisfies Poisson's equation:
> $$\boxed{\nabla^2 \mathbf{A} = -\mu_0 \mathbf{J}}$$

---

### 3. Integral Solutions for A
Assuming the current density $\mathbf{J}$ goes to zero at infinity, the solution to the vector Poisson equation is mathematically analogous to the solution for the electric potential $V$.

> [!definition] Integral Form of the Vector Potential
> For a volume current distribution, the potential at field point $\mathbf{r}$ is:
> $$\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')}{\boldsymbol{r}} d\tau'$$
> 
> For line and surface currents, the "dictionary" translation yields:
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> \mathbf{A} &= \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \int \frac{1}{\boldsymbol{r}} d\mathbf{l}' && \text{\scriptsize for line currents} \\
> \mathbf{A} &= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{K}}{\boldsymbol{r}} da' && \text{\scriptsize for surface currents}
> \end{aligned}
> $$



### 4. Summary of Physical Content
* **Directionality**: Ordinarily, the direction of $\mathbf{A}$ mimics the direction of the current $\mathbf{J}$. For a straight wire, $\mathbf{A}$ is parallel to the wire; for a loop, $\mathbf{A}$ is azimuthal.
* **Theoretical Importance**: While $\mathbf{A}$ was traditionally viewed as a mathematical convenience, it has deep physical significance in quantum mechanics and as "potential momentum per unit charge."
* **Scalar Potential ($U$)**: Note that $\mathbf{B} = -\nabla U$ only works in current-free regions and is generally incompatible with Ampère's law.

## 5.4.2 Boundary Conditions

Just as the electric field suffers a discontinuity at a surface charge ($\sigma$), the magnetic field is discontinuous at a surface current ($\mathbf{K}$). We use the integral forms of the divergence and curl equations to find exactly how the field changes across this boundary.

### 1. The Normal Component (Continuous)
Applying $\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = 0$ to a wafer-thin pillbox straddling the surface current:


$$
\begin{aligned}
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a} &= B_{\text{above}}^{\perp} A - B_{\text{below}}^{\perp} A = 0 \\
B_{\text{above}}^{\perp} &= B_{\text{below}}^{\perp}
\end{aligned}
$$
**Conclusion:** The normal component of $\mathbf{B}$ is continuous across any surface.

### 2. The Tangential Component (Discontinuous)
Applying Ampère's law ($\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}$) to a narrow rectangular loop running perpendicular to the current:


$$
\begin{aligned}
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} &= \left( B_{\text{above}}^{\parallel} - B_{\text{below}}^{\parallel} \right) l && \text{\scriptsize integrating along the top and bottom edges} \\
\mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} &= \mu_0 K l && \text{\scriptsize enclosed current depends on surface density } K \\
B_{\text{above}}^{\parallel} - B_{\text{below}}^{\parallel} &= \mu_0 K && \text{\scriptsize equating the two expressions}
\end{aligned}
$$
**Conclusion:** The tangential component of $\mathbf{B}$ is discontinuous by an amount proportional to the surface current density. 

> [!definition] General Boundary Condition for B
> These results can be elegantly summarized in a single vector formula:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{B}_{\text{above}} - \mathbf{B}_{\text{below}} = \mu_0 (\mathbf{K} \times \hat{\mathbf{n}})}$$
> where $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is a unit vector perpendicular to the surface, pointing "upward."

### 3. Boundary Conditions for the Vector Potential (A)
Like the scalar potential $V$, the vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ is continuous across any boundary:
$$\mathbf{A}_{\text{above}} = \mathbf{A}_{\text{below}}$$
However, its *derivative* inherits the discontinuity of $\mathbf{B}$:
$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{A}_{\text{above}}}{\partial n} - \frac{\partial \mathbf{A}_{\text{below}}}{\partial n} = -\mu_0 \mathbf{K}$$

*(Note: Griffiths provides a triangular diagram—Fig 5.51—summarizing the mathematical links between $\mathbf{J}$, $\mathbf{B}$, and $\mathbf{A}$, completing the magnetostatic equivalent of the electrostatics triangle.)*

## 5.4.3 Multipole Expansion of the Vector Potential

When dealing with a localized current distribution and observing from far away, we can approximate the vector potential using a multipole expansion. This relies on the same $1/\boldsymbol{r}$ expansion used in electrostatics.


$$
\frac{1}{\boldsymbol{r}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r^2 + (r')^2 - 2rr'\cos\alpha}} = \frac{1}{r} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left( \frac{r'}{r} \right)^n P_n(\cos\alpha)
$$

Substituting this into the integral for $\mathbf{A}$ (for a line current):
$$
\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{r^{n+1}} \oint (r')^n P_n(\cos\alpha) d\mathbf{l}'
$$

### 1. The Monopole Term (Always Zero)
The first term ($n=0$) corresponds to the magnetic monopole:
$$
\mathbf{A}_{\text{mono}}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi r} \oint d\mathbf{l}'
$$
Because the integral of a closed displacement vector $\oint d\mathbf{l}'$ is mathematically exactly zero, **the magnetic monopole term is always zero**. This reflects the physical assumption ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$) that isolated magnetic charges do not exist.

### 2. The Dipole Term (The Dominant Contribution)
In the absence of a monopole, the dipole term ($n=1$) is the dominant contribution to the field at large distances:
$$
\mathbf{A}_{\text{dip}}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi r^2} \oint r' \cos\alpha d\mathbf{l}' = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi r^2} \oint (\hat{\mathbf{r}} \cdot \mathbf{r}') d\mathbf{l}'
$$

Using a vector calculus identity, this integral evaluates to $-\hat{\mathbf{r}} \times \int d\mathbf{a}'$. This motivates the definition of the **Magnetic Dipole Moment**.


> [!definition] Magnetic Dipole Moment ($\mathbf{m}$)
> For a steady current loop, the magnetic dipole moment is:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{m} \equiv I \int d\mathbf{a} = I\mathbf{a}}$$
> * **$I$**: The current in the loop.
> * **$\mathbf{a}$**: The "vector area" of the loop. If flat, it's just the area.
> * **Direction**: Given by the right-hand rule (fingers curl with current, thumb points to $\mathbf{m}$).

### 3. Vector Potential and Field of a Perfect Dipole
Using the dipole moment, the vector potential at a distance $r$ simplifies beautifully:
$$
\boxed{\mathbf{A}_{\text{dip}}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{\mathbf{m} \times \hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2}}
$$

If we align the dipole moment $\mathbf{m}$ with the $z$-axis, we can take the curl of $\mathbf{A}$ ($\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$) to find the explicit magnetic field:

> [!remark] Magnetic Field of a Dipole
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{B}_{\text{dip}}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0 m}{4\pi r^3} (2\cos\theta \hat{\mathbf{r}} + \sin\theta \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})}$$
> **Observation**: This structure is *mathematically identical* to the field of an electric dipole. However, up close, a physical magnetic dipole (a current loop) looks very different from a physical electric dipole (two separated charges).
