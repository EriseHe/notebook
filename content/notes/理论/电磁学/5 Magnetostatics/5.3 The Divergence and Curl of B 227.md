## 5.3.1 Straight-Line Currents

Unlike electrostatic fields (which originate from sources), magnetic fields for straight wires **circle around** the source. This circular geometry implies a nonzero curl, a stark contrast to the curl-free nature of $\mathbf{E}$ in electrostatics.

### 1. The Line Integral of B
For an infinite straight wire, the field at distance $s$ is $\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi s} \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$. If we integrate this field around a circular path of radius $s$ centered on the wire:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} &= \oint \left( \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi s} \right) dl && \text{\scriptsize since } \mathbf{B} \text{ is parallel to } d\mathbf{l} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi s} \oint dl && \text{\scriptsize pulling constants out of the integral} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi s} (2\pi s) && \text{\scriptsize circumference of the circle} \\
&= \mu_0 I && \text{\scriptsize result is independent of radius } s
\end{aligned}
$$


### 2. Generalization to Arbitrary Loops
The result $\mu_0 I$ is not restricted to circles. Because $B$ decreases at the same rate the circumference increases ($1/s$ vs. $s$), any loop that encloses the wire once will yield the same result.

**Derivation in Cylindrical Coordinates:**
For a general loop, $d\mathbf{l} = ds \hat{\mathbf{s}} + s d\phi \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} + dz \hat{\mathbf{z}}$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} &= \int \left( \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi s} \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \right) \cdot (ds \hat{\mathbf{s}} + s d\phi \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} + dz \hat{\mathbf{z}}) && \scriptsize \text{ only } \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \text{ survives} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi} \oint \frac{1}{s} s d\phi &&\scriptsize s \text{ cancels out} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi} \int_{0}^{2\pi} d\phi && \text{\scriptsize assuming loop encircles wire once} \\
&= \mu_0 I && \text{\scriptsize (Eq. 5.44)}
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!important] Loop Topology
> * **Enclosed Wire**: If the loop encircles the wire, the integral is $\mu_0 I$.
> * **Not Enclosed**: If the loop does not encircle the wire, $\phi$ goes from $\phi_1$ to $\phi_2$ and back again, resulting in $\oint d\phi = 0$. (See Fig. 5.27)

---

### 3. Ampere's Law in Integral Form
If we have a bundle of many wires, the principle of superposition dictates that each wire passing through the loop contributes $\mu_0 I_i$.

> [!definition] Ampere's Law (Integral Form)
> The line integral of $\mathbf{B}$ around any closed curve is equal to $\mu_0$ times the total current enclosed by the curve:
> $$\boxed{\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}}$$
> where $I_{\text{enc}} = \int \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{a}$ for a volume current density $\mathbf{J}$.


---

### 4. Ampere's Law in Differential Form
By applying **Stokes' Theorem** to the integral form, we can relate the curl of the magnetic field to the current density at a specific point.

**Derivation of the Curl of B:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} &= \int (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot d\mathbf{a} && \text{\scriptsize by Stokes' Theorem} \\
\mu_0 \int \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{a} &= \int (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot d\mathbf{a} && \text{\scriptsize substituting } I_{\text{enc}} \text{ from Eq. 5.45}
\end{aligned}
$$

Since this must hold for **any** loop (and thus any surface), the integrands must be equal:

> [!definition] Ampere's Law (Differential Form)
> The curl of the magnetic field is proportional to the local current density:
> $$\boxed{\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}}$$

---

### 5. Limitation of this Derivation
Griffiths notes that this specific derivation is "seriously flawed" because it relies on the symmetry of **infinite straight wires**. While Eq. 5.46 is generally true for all steady currents, we have not yet proven it for curved wires or more complex configurations. The next section will provide the formal proof using the Biot-Savart law.

## 5.3.2 The Divergence and Curl of B

To prove the general properties of $\mathbf{B}$, we start with the Biot-Savart law for a volume current distribution $\mathbf{J}$:
$$\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} d\tau'$$

> [!important] Coordinate Distinction
> It is crucial to distinguish between the two sets of coordinates:
> * **Source Point ($\mathbf{r}'$):** Where the current is located. The integration is over primed variables $(x', y', z')$.
> * **Field Point ($\mathbf{r}$):** Where we calculate the field. The operators $\nabla$ and $\nabla \times$ act on unprimed variables $(x, y, z)$.
> * **Separation Vector:** $\boldsymbol{r} = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'$.

---

### 1. The Divergence of B
We apply the divergence operator to the Biot-Savart integral. Since the integral is over primed coordinates and the derivative is with respect to unprimed coordinates, we can move the $\nabla \cdot$ inside the integral.

**Derivation of Divergence:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} &= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \nabla \cdot \left( \mathbf{J} \times \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} \right) d\tau' && \text{\scriptsize applying divergence to Eq. 5.47} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \left[ \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} \cdot (\underbrace{\nabla \times \mathbf{J}}_{0}) - \mathbf{J} \cdot (\underbrace{\nabla \times \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2}}_{0}) \right] d\tau' && \text{\scriptsize invoking Product Rule 6} \\
&= 0 && \text{\scriptsize both terms vanish}
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!definition] Divergence of the Magnetic Field
> The divergence of any magnetic field is always zero:
> $$\boxed{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0}$$
> This implies there are no "magnetic charges" (monopoles); magnetic field lines never begin or end.


---

### 2. The Curl of B
Next, we apply the curl operator. This requires using Product Rule 8 for the curl of a cross product.

**Derivation of Curl:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \times \mathbf{B} &= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \nabla \times \left( \mathbf{J} \times \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} \right) d\tau' && \text{\scriptsize applying curl to Eq. 5.47} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \left[ \mathbf{J} \left( \nabla \cdot \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} \right) - (\mathbf{J} \cdot \nabla) \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} \right] d\tau' && \text{\scriptsize invoking Product Rule 8 (terms with } \nabla \mathbf{J} \text{ drop)} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \left[ 4\pi \delta^3(\boldsymbol{r}) \right] d\tau' - 0 && \text{\scriptsize using } \nabla \cdot (\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}/\boldsymbol{r}^2) = 4\pi\delta^3(\boldsymbol{r}) \\
&= \mu_0 \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}) && \text{\scriptsize the delta function picks out the value at } \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}'
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!definition] Ampere's Law (Differential Form)
> The curl of the magnetic field is proportional to the current density:
> $$\boxed{\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}}$$


---

### 3. Technical Detail: Why the Second Term Vanishes
The proof of the curl requires showing that the integral of the second term in Product Rule 8 vanishes:
$$\int (\mathbf{J} \cdot \nabla) \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} d\tau' = 0$$

**Logic of the Vanishing Integral:**
1. **Switch Derivatives:** Since $\boldsymbol{r}$ depends only on the difference $(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')$, we can replace $\nabla$ with $-\nabla'$.
2. **Integration by Parts:** We use Product Rule 5 to shift the $\nabla'$ from the $\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}/\boldsymbol{r}^2$ term onto the $\mathbf{J}$ term.
3. **Magnetostatic Condition:** For steady currents, $\nabla' \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$.
4. **Boundary Condition:** Applying the Divergence Theorem, the remaining term becomes a surface integral:
$$\oint_{S} \left[ \frac{(x-x')}{\boldsymbol{r}^3} \right] \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{a}'$$
If we choose a volume large enough to contain all current, then $\mathbf{J} = 0$ on the boundary $S$, causing the integral to vanish.

---

### Summary of Field Equations (Magnetostatics)
| Property       | Differential Form                             | Integral Form                                               |
| :------------- | :-------------------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Divergence** | $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$                 | $\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = 0$                    |
| **Curl**       | $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$ | $\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}$ |
## 5.3.3 Ampère’s Law

While the Biot-Savart law is the fundamental tool for calculating magnetic fields, it is often mathematically intensive. For systems with specific geometric symmetries, we utilize **Ampère’s Law**, which is the magnetostatic equivalent of Gauss's Law in electrostatics.

> [!definition] Ampère’s Law (Integral Form)
> The line integral of the magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ around any closed loop is equal to $\mu_0$ times the total current enclosed by that loop ($I_{\text{enc}}$):
> $$\boxed{\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}}$$

### 1. Derivation via Stokes’ Theorem
The transition from the differential form (curl) to the integral form is achieved through the fundamental theorem for curls.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot d\mathbf{a} &= \mu_0 \int \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{a} && \text{\scriptsize integrating the curl equation over a surface } S \\
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} &= \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} && \text{\scriptsize applying Stokes' theorem and the definition of } I_{\text{enc}}
\end{aligned}
$$



### 2. Sign Convention (Right-Hand Rule)
To determine which current direction is "positive" for $I_{\text{enc}}$:
* **Fingers**: Curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction you chose to integrate around the loop.
* **Thumb**: Your thumb points in the direction of **positive current**.

### 3. Applications and Symmetry
Ampère's law is always *true* for steady currents, but it is only *useful* for calculating $\mathbf{B}$ when the symmetry allows you to pull $B$ out of the integral. 

| Symmetry Type | Standard Amperian Loop |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Infinite Straight Line** | A circle centered on the wire. |
| **Infinite Plane** | A rectangular "Pillbox" loop. |
| **Infinite Solenoid** | A rectangular loop partially inside the solenoid. |
| **Toroid** | A circle inside the "donut." |

## 5.3.4 Comparison of Magnetostatics and Electrostatics

We can now summarize the governing equations for stationary charges and steady currents, collectively known as **Maxwell’s Equations for Statics**.

### 1. Maxwell's Equations for Statics
| Field Property | Electrostatics                                              | Magnetostatics                                               |
| :------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Divergence** | $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho / \epsilon_0$ (Gauss's Law) | $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ (No name)                      |
| **Curl**       | $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = 0$ (No name)                    | $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$ (Ampère's Law) |

> [!definition] The Lorentz Force Law
> Together with the force law, these equations describe the entirety of static electromagnetic interactions:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{F} = Q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})}$$

### 2. Physical Interpretations
* **The Strength Paradox**: Electric forces are intrinsically much stronger than magnetic forces. We notice magnetism because the electric fields of positive and negative charges in a **neutral wire** cancel out, allowing the magnetic force to emerge.
* **No Magnetic Monopoles**: The fact that $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ means there are no "magnetic charges." Magnetic field lines do not begin or end; they generally form **closed loops**.



### 3. The Nature of Field Lines
Griffiths warns that field lines are merely visual "cartoons." 
* **Electrostatics**: $\mathbf{E}$ lines diverge from positive charges.
* **Magnetostatics**: $\mathbf{B}$ lines curl around currents.
* **Complex Cases**: In systems with low symmetry, a field line may wind endlessly (like a helix) without ever closing on itself. The real physical quantities are the **vector fields** themselves at each point in space.
