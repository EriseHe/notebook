## 5.1.1 Magnetic Fields

The basic problem of classical electrodynamics is calculating the force that a collection of "source" charges ($q_1, q_2, \dots$) exerts on a "test" charge $Q$. While **Electrostatics** deals with source charges at rest, **Magnetostatics** addresses the forces between charges in motion.

> [!definition] **The Origin of Magnetic Fields**
> A stationary charge produces only an electric field $\mathbf{E}$ in the space around it. However, a **moving charge** generates, in addition, a **magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$**.

### The Demonstration: Parallel and Antiparallel Currents
To illustrate the unique nature of magnetic forces, consider two parallel wires hanging from a ceiling. The force between them depends on the relative direction of the currents:

| Current Configuration   | Physical Result                      | Interaction Type |
| :---------------------- | :----------------------------------- | :--------------- |
| **Opposite Directions** | The wires jump apart (Fig. 5.1a).    | **Repulsion**    |
| **Same Direction**      | The wires move together (Fig. 5.1b). | **Attraction**   |

> [!Remark] **Crucial Concept: Electrical Neutrality**
> This force is **not electrostatic** in nature. The wires are electrically neutral because there are just as many stationary positive charges (atomic nuclei) as there are moving negative charges (electrons) in any given segment. A stationary test charge $Q$ held near these wires would feel **no force**. The magnetic force only acts on charges that are also in motion.

### The Geometry of the B-Field

Unlike the electric field of a point charge (which points radially toward or away from the source), the magnetic field of a wire **circles around** it (Fig. 5.2).
![|673x717](https://cdn.mathpix.com/snip/images/v7YTUfURRa8YV5ML7gdsjqp8L5JuuVY0o4UOWW1-O14.original.fullsize.png)


**The Right-Hand Rule (RHR) for Fields:**
To determine the direction of the field:
1. Point your right **thumb** in the direction of the current $I$.
2. Your **fingers** will curl around the wire in the direction of the magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$.

### The Direction of Magnetic Force
The direction of the resulting force $\mathbf{F}$ is often counterintuitive. For two parallel wires carrying current in the same direction (upward):
1. **Field Generation**: Wire 1 creates a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ that points **into the page** at the location of Wire 2.
2. **Force Result**: Despite the current being upward and the field being "into" the page, the resulting force $\mathbf{F}$ on Wire 2 points **to the left** (toward Wire 1), as seen in Fig. 5.3.

> [!remark] **Detecting Magnetic Fields**
> In practice, magnetic fields are easily detected using a compass. The needle aligns itself with the local magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$. While it ordinarily points North due to the Earth's field, laboratory fields are often strong enough to dominate the compass response.

## 5.1.2 Magnetic Forces

In this section, we transition from the qualitative observations of wires and compasses to the formal mathematical "law of the land" for magnetic interactions. While the electric force is a simple push or pull, the magnetic force is inherently linked to **velocity** and **three-dimensional geometry**.

### The Lorentz Force Law

The magnetic force $\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}}$ on a charge $Q$, moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, is defined by the vector cross product.


> [!definition] Lorentz Force Law
> The **total force** on a charge $Q$ in the presence of both electric and magnetic fields is:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{F} = Q[\mathbf{E} + (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})]}$$

* **The Cross Product**: The force is always perpendicular to both the velocity $\mathbf{v}$ and the magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$.
* **Stationary Charges**: If $\mathbf{v} = 0$, the magnetic part of the force vanishes. Magnetic fields "ignore" stationary charges.
* **Sign of Charge**: For a negative charge (like an electron), the force direction is exactly opposite to the result of the standard Right-Hand Rule (use the back of your hand).

### Magnetic Forces Do No Work

This is the most famous conceptual "trap" in magnetostatics. Because the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the direction of motion, it can change a particle's **direction**, but never its **speed** or **kinetic energy**.


>[!definition] Magnetic Work
>Magnetic forces may alter the direction in which a particle moves, but they cannot speed it up or slow it down. The work done is:
> $$\boxed{dW_{\text{mag}} = 0}$$

**Mathematical Proof:**
1. Work is defined as $dW = \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{l}$.
2. Displacement $d\mathbf{l}$ can be written as $\mathbf{v} dt$.
3. Substitute the magnetic force: $dW_{\text{mag}} = Q(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot \mathbf{v} dt$.
4. Since $(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})$ is perpendicular to $\mathbf{v}$ by definition of the cross product, their dot product is **zero**.

## 5.1.3 Currents

### 1. Definition of Current ($I$)
Current is defined as the **charge per unit time** passing a given point in a wire.

* **Units**: Measured in Amperes (A), where $1\text{ A} = 1\text{ C/s}$.
* **Sign Convention**: Positive charges moving to the right count the same as negative charges moving to the left. In practice, electrons (negative) move opposite to the direction of the "electric current" $I$.
* **Line Charge Current**: If a line charge with density $\lambda$ travels down a wire at speed $v$, the current $I$ is:

> [!definition] Electric Current ($I$)
> The current $I$ in a wire is the charge per unit time passing a given point. It is measured in Amperes (A).$$1\text{ A} = 1\text{ C/s}$$
> For a line charge density $\lambda$ moving at velocity $\mathbf{v}$, the current is:$$\mathbf{I} = \lambda \mathbf{v}$$


### 2. Magnetic Force on a Line Current

To find the force on a segment of wire, we integrate the Lorentz force over all the charge elements in that segment.

**Derivation of the Force on a Wire:**
$$\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}} &= \int (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) dq && \text{\scriptsize Lorentz force law} \\
&= \int (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) \lambda dl && \scriptsize  dq = \lambda dl \text{ for line charges} \\
&= \int (\underbrace{\lambda \mathbf{v}}_{\mathbf{I}} \times \mathbf{B}) dl &&  \\
&= \int I (d\mathbf{l} \times \mathbf{B}) && \scriptsize\text{ since } \mathbf{I} dl = I d\mathbf{l} \text{  (Eq. 5.16)} \\
&= I \int (d\mathbf{l} \times \mathbf{B}) &&
\end{aligned}$$

> [!definition] Force on a Wire
> For a wire carrying a constant current $I$, the total magnetic force is:$$\boxed{\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}} = I \int (d\mathbf{l} \times \mathbf{B})}$$

### 3. Surface Current Density ($\mathbf{K}$)
When charge flows over a surface (like a sheet of metal), we use the **surface current density $\mathbf{K}$**.

* **Definition**: The current per unit width-perpendicular-to-flow ($dl_{\perp}$).
$$\mathbf{K} \equiv \frac{d\mathbf{I}}{dl_{\perp}}$$
* **Relation to Velocity**: If $\sigma$ is the surface charge density, then $\boxed{\mathbf{K} = \sigma \mathbf{v}}$.


**Magnetic Force on a Surface Current:**
$$\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}} &= \int (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) \sigma da && \text{\scriptsize force on charge element } \sigma da \\
&= \int (\underbrace{\sigma \mathbf{v}}_{\mathbf{K}} \times \mathbf{B}) da  \\
&= \int (\mathbf{K} \times \mathbf{B}) da && \text{\scriptsize final integral over the surface area (Eq. 5.24)}
\end{aligned}$$

---

### 4. Volume Current Density ($\mathbf{J}$)
When charge is distributed throughout a 3D region, we use the **volume current density $\mathbf{J}$**.

* **Definition**: The current per unit area-perpendicular-to-flow ($da_{\perp}$).
$$\mathbf{J} \equiv \frac{d\mathbf{I}}{da_{\perp}}$$
* **Relation to Velocity**: If $\rho$ is the volume charge density, then $\boxed{\mathbf{J} = \rho \mathbf{v}}$.


**Magnetic Force on a Volume Current:**
$$\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}} &= \int (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) \rho d\tau && \text{\scriptsize force on charge element } \rho d\tau \\
&= \int (\underbrace{\rho \mathbf{v}}_{\mathbf{J}} \times \mathbf{B}) d\tau  \\
&= \int (\mathbf{J} \times \mathbf{B}) d\tau && \text{\scriptsize final integral over the volume (Eq. 5.27)}
\end{aligned}$$


### 5. The Continuity Equation
This equation is the mathematical statement of **Local Charge Conservation**. It states that if charge is leaving a volume (outward flow of $\mathbf{J}$), the charge density $\rho$ inside must be decreasing.

**Derivation of the Continuity Equation:**
$$\begin{aligned}
\oint_{\mathcal{S}} \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{a} &= \int_{\mathcal{V}} (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}) d\tau && \text{\scriptsize applying the Divergence Theorem} \\
&= -\frac{d}{dt} Q_{\text{enc}} && \text{\scriptsize conservation: flow out = decrease in charge} \\
&= -\frac{d}{dt} \int_{\mathcal{V}} \rho d\tau && \text{\scriptsize expressing enclosed charge via density } \rho \\
\int_{\mathcal{V}} (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}) d\tau &= \int_{\mathcal{V}} \left( -\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} \right) d\tau && \text{\scriptsize equating the two volume integrals}
\end{aligned}$$

Since this must hold for any volume, we get the **Continuity Equation (Eq. 5.29)**:
$$\boxed{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = -\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}}$$
* **In Magnetostatics**: Charges do not pile up anywhere ($\partial \rho / \partial t = 0$), so the divergence of current is zero: $\boxed{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0}$.
