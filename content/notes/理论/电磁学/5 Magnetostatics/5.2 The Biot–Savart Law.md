This section bridges the gap between individual moving charges and the constant fields we study in **Magnetostatics**. Just as Electrostatics deals with charges that don't move, Magnetostatics deals with currents that don't change.

## 5.2.1 Steady Currents

### 1. Defining the Regime

The transition from electricity to magnetism follows a perfect parallel logic. In both cases, we are looking for "equilibrium" states where the fields themselves do not fluctuate with time.

| Regime | Source | Field Result |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Electrostatics** | Stationary Charges | Constant Electric Field ($\mathbf{E}$) |
| **Magnetostatics** | **Steady Currents** | Constant Magnetic Field ($\mathbf{B}$) |

> [!definition] Steady Current
> A **steady current** is a continuous flow of charge that has been going on forever, without change and without charge piling up anywhere. 

### 2. Formal Conditions of Magnetostatics
To keep the fields constant in time, the sources (charge density and current density) must themselves be independent of time at every point in space.

> [!Remark] The Mathematical Conditions
> Formally, magnetostatics is the regime where:
> $$\boxed{\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0} \quad \text{and} \quad \boxed{\frac{\partial \mathbf{J}}{\partial t} = \mathbf{0}}$$
> * **$\partial \rho / \partial t = 0$**: Charge density at any fixed point remains constant.
> * **$\partial \mathbf{J} / \partial t = \mathbf{0}$**: The "flow rate" at any fixed point remains constant.


### 3. The "Point Charge" Headache
Griffiths points out a major difference between how we teach Electrostatics and Magnetostatics:
* **In Electrostatics**: we start with a **point charge at rest**, which produces a static field.
* **In Magnetostatics**: we **cannot** start with a single moving point charge. 

**Why?** Because a moving point charge is the opposite of steady. If a charge is at position $A$ at time $t_1$, and at position $B$ at $t_2$, then $\rho$ at those points is changing violently over time. A single moving point charge does not produce a static magnetic field. Therefore, we must deal with **extended current distributions** (like wires or loops) right from the start.

---

### 4. Conservation of Charge: Continuity Equation

In magnetostatics, the fact that charge does not "pile up" has a specific mathematical consequence for the Divergence of the current density.

**Derivation of the Magnetostatic Continuity Equation:**
$$\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} &= -\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} && \text{\scriptsize starting from the general Continuity Equation} \\
&= 0 && \text{\scriptsize since } \partial \rho / \partial t = 0 \text{ in magnetostatics} 
\end{aligned}$$

> [!definition] Magnetostatic Continuity
> In the steady current regime, the current density is **divergenceless**:
> $$\boxed{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0}$$
> This implies that for a steady current in a wire, the magnitude $I$ must be the same all along the line. 


## 5.2.2 The Magnetic Field of a Steady Current

This section introduces the **Biot-Savart Law**, which serves as the fundamental empirical law of magnetostatics, analogous to Coulomb's law in electrostatics.

### 1. The Biot-Savart Law for Line Currents
The magnetic field produced by a steady line current $I$ follows an inverse-square relationship with the separation distance, with a direction governed by the cross product.

> [!definition] Biot-Savart Law (Line Current)
> The magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ at a point $\mathbf{r}$ due to a steady current $I$ is:
> $$\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} I \int \frac{d\mathbf{l}' \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2}$$
> * **$d\mathbf{l}'$**: Element of length along the wire, pointing in the direction of current.
> * **$\boldsymbol{r}$**: The separation vector from the **source** ($d\mathbf{l}'$) to the **field point** ($\mathbf{r}$).
> * **$\mu_0$**: Permeability of free space, defined as $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ N/A}^2$.



### 2. Units and Comparison
The magnetic field is measured in **Teslas (T)**. According to the Lorentz force law, the dimensions are:
$$1 \text{ T} = 1 \text{ N}/(\text{A} \cdot \text{m})$$

| Feature | Coulomb's Law | Biot-Savart Law |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Source** | Stationary charge ($dq$) | Steady current ($I d\mathbf{l}'$) |
| **Dependence** | $1/\boldsymbol{r}^2$ | $1/\boldsymbol{r}^2$ |
| **Direction** | Radial (along $\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}$) | Perpendicular to $d\mathbf{l}'$ and $\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}$ |

### 3. Generalization to Continuous Distributions
Using the "dictionary" from Section 5.1.3 ($I d\mathbf{l}' \sim \mathbf{K} da' \sim \mathbf{J} d\tau'$), we extend the law to surface and volume currents.

**Derivation of Surface and Volume Fields:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) &= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{K}(\mathbf{r}') \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} da' && \text{\scriptsize for surface currents } \mathbf{K} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} d\tau' && \text{\scriptsize for volume currents } \mathbf{J} 
\end{aligned}
$$



### 4. Important Principles and Warnings

> [!Remark] The Point Charge Error
> It is tempting to write the field of a moving point charge as:
> $$\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{q\mathbf{v} \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} \quad \text{(INCORRECT)}$$
> Griffiths emphasizes that this is **simply wrong**. A moving point charge does not constitute a steady current, and the Biot-Savart law applies strictly to the steady-state regime.

**The Superposition Principle:**
Magnetic fields obey the superposition principle. If you have a collection of source currents, the net field is the vector sum of the fields due to each source:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{B}_{\text{net}} &= \mathbf{B}_1 + \mathbf{B}_2 + \dots && \text{\scriptsize summation of individual fields} \\
&= \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{B}_i && \text{\scriptsize discrete sources} \\
&= \int d\mathbf{B} && \text{\scriptsize continuous distribution}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Summary Checklist
* **Inverse-Square**: Field strength drops with $1/\boldsymbol{r}^2$.
* **Geometry**: $d\mathbf{l}' \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}$ ensures the field circles the wire (Right-Hand Rule).
* **Steady state**: The law only works if $\partial \mathbf{J}/\partial t = 0$.
