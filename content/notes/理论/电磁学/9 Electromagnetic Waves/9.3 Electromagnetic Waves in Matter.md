### Big picture

In vacuum, Maxwell’s equations gave us waves traveling at speed $c$.  
In matter, the fields interact with the medium through polarization and magnetization, so the wave still propagates, but with a modified speed

$$
v=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon\mu}}=\frac{c}{n}.
$$

The main goals of this section are:

- derive wave propagation in a linear homogeneous medium,
- use electromagnetic boundary conditions to determine what happens at an interface,
- recover the laws of reflection and refraction,
- derive amplitude and energy relations for reflected and transmitted waves,
- understand the special role of polarization, including Brewster’s angle.

---

### 9.3.1 Propagation in Linear Media

#### Maxwell equations in matter

Inside matter, but in regions with no free charge and no free current, Maxwell’s equations are

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla\cdot \mathbf D &= 0, \\
\nabla\cdot \mathbf B &= 0, \\
\nabla\times \mathbf E &= -\frac{\partial \mathbf B}{\partial t}, \\
\nabla\times \mathbf H &= \frac{\partial \mathbf D}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

If the medium is **linear**,

$$
\mathbf D=\epsilon \mathbf E,
\qquad
\mathbf H=\frac{1}{\mu}\mathbf B,
$$

and **homogeneous** so that $\epsilon$ and $\mu$ are constants, then these become

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla\cdot \mathbf E &= 0, \\
\nabla\cdot \mathbf B &= 0, \\
\nabla\times \mathbf E &= -\frac{\partial \mathbf B}{\partial t}, \\
\nabla\times \mathbf B &= \mu\epsilon \frac{\partial \mathbf E}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

These are exactly the vacuum equations with the replacement

$$
\mu_0\epsilon_0 \longrightarrow \mu\epsilon.
$$

So all the vacuum-wave machinery carries over with this substitution.

---

#### Wave speed and index of refraction

From the same curl-of-curl derivation used in vacuum,

$$
\boxed{
\nabla^2\mathbf E=\mu\epsilon\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf E}{\partial t^2},
\qquad
\nabla^2\mathbf B=\mu\epsilon\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf B}{\partial t^2}}
$$

so the wave speed is

$$
\boxed{
v=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon\mu}}=\frac{c}{n}}
$$

with index of refraction

$$
\boxed{
n\equiv \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon\mu}{\epsilon_0\mu_0}} }.
$$

For most media, $\mu\approx \mu_0$, so

$$
\boxed{n\approx \sqrt{\epsilon_r}}.
$$

That is why light usually travels more slowly in matter than in vacuum.

---

#### Plane-wave relations in matter

All the plane-wave structure from vacuum survives, with $c\to v$, $\epsilon_0\to\epsilon$, $\mu_0\to\mu$.

For a monochromatic plane wave:

- $\omega=kv$,
- the amplitude of $\mathbf B$ is $(1/v)$ times the amplitude of $\mathbf E$,
- $\mathbf E$, $\mathbf B$, and $\hat{\mathbf k}$ are mutually perpendicular.

So for amplitudes,

$$
\boxed{B_0=\frac{E_0}{v}}.
$$

---

#### Energy density, Poynting vector, and intensity

The energy density is

$$
\boxed{
u=\frac12\left(\epsilon E^2+\frac{1}{\mu}B^2\right)}
$$

and the Poynting vector is

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf S=\frac{1}{\mu}(\mathbf E\times \mathbf B)}.
$$

For a monochromatic plane wave, the intensity is

$$
\boxed{
I=\frac12\,\epsilon v E_0^2 }.
$$

So the practical transcription from vacuum is very simple:

$$
\epsilon_0 \to \epsilon,\qquad
\mu_0 \to \mu,\qquad
c \to v.
$$

---

#### Key conceptual point

The mathematics looks simple, but physically this is subtle: the wave polarizes and magnetizes the medium, and the induced microscopic dipoles generate fields that combine with the original wave into one new wave with the same frequency but a different speed.

### 9.3.2 Reflection and Transmission at Normal Incidence

#### Setup

Let the interface be the $xy$-plane.  
A wave in medium 1 travels in the $+z$ direction toward medium 2, polarized in the $x$ direction.

The incident wave is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{\mathbf E}_I(z,t) &= \tilde E_{0I} e^{i(k_1 z-\omega t)}\hat{\mathbf x},\\
\tilde{\mathbf B}_I(z,t) &= \frac{1}{v_1}\tilde E_{0I} e^{i(k_1 z-\omega t)}\hat{\mathbf y}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The reflected wave is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{\mathbf E}_R(z,t) &= \tilde E_{0R} e^{i(-k_1 z-\omega t)}\hat{\mathbf x},\\
\tilde{\mathbf B}_R(z,t) &= -\frac{1}{v_1}\tilde E_{0R} e^{i(-k_1 z-\omega t)}\hat{\mathbf y}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The minus sign in $\tilde{\mathbf B}_R$ is essential: it makes $\mathbf E\times \mathbf B$ point in the reflected propagation direction.

The transmitted wave is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{\mathbf E}_T(z,t) &= \tilde E_{0T} e^{i(k_2 z-\omega t)}\hat{\mathbf x},\\
\tilde{\mathbf B}_T(z,t) &= \frac{1}{v_2}\tilde E_{0T} e^{i(k_2 z-\omega t)}\hat{\mathbf y}.
\end{aligned}
$$

---

#### Boundary conditions

At the interface $z=0$, the fields on the left must match those on the right.

For linear media, the boundary conditions are

$$
\begin{aligned}
\epsilon_1 E_{1}^{\perp} &= \epsilon_2 E_{2}^{\perp}, \\
B_{1}^{\perp} &= B_{2}^{\perp}, \\
E_{1}^{\parallel} &= E_{2}^{\parallel}, \\
\frac{1}{\mu_1}B_{1}^{\parallel} &= \frac{1}{\mu_2}B_{2}^{\parallel}.
\end{aligned}
$$

In the present geometry there are no perpendicular components, so only the tangential conditions matter.

From continuity of tangential $\mathbf E$,

$$
\boxed{\tilde E_{0I}+\tilde E_{0R}=\tilde E_{0T}}
$$

and from continuity of tangential $\mathbf H=\mathbf B/\mu$,

$$
\frac{1}{\mu_1}\left(\frac{1}{v_1}\tilde E_{0I}-\frac{1}{v_1}\tilde E_{0R}\right)
=
\frac{1}{\mu_2}\left(\frac{1}{v_2}\tilde E_{0T}\right).
$$

Define

$$
\boxed{
\beta\equiv \frac{\mu_1 v_1}{\mu_2 v_2}
=\frac{\mu_1 n_2}{\mu_2 n_1}}
$$

so the second condition becomes

$$
\boxed{\tilde E_{0I}-\tilde E_{0R}=\beta \tilde E_{0T}}.
$$

---

#### Solving for amplitudes

Now solve the two equations

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde E_{0I}+\tilde E_{0R} &= \tilde E_{0T},\\
\tilde E_{0I}-\tilde E_{0R} &= \beta \tilde E_{0T}.
\end{aligned}
$$

A clean textbook-style elimination is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde E_{0I}+\tilde E_{0R} &= \tilde E_{0T},\\
\tilde E_{0I}-\tilde E_{0R} &= \beta \tilde E_{0T}
\end{aligned}
$$

Add and subtract appropriately:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2\tilde E_{0I}
&=(1+\beta)\tilde E_{0T}
\\
\Rightarrow\quad
\tilde E_{0T}
&=\frac{2}{1+\beta}\tilde E_{0I},
\end{aligned}
$$

and then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde E_{0R}
&=\tilde E_{0T}-\tilde E_{0I}
\\
&=\left(\frac{2}{1+\beta}-1\right)\tilde E_{0I}
\\
&=\frac{1-\beta}{1+\beta}\tilde E_{0I}.
\end{aligned}
$$

So

$$
\boxed{
\tilde E_{0R}=\frac{1-\beta}{1+\beta}\tilde E_{0I},
\qquad
\tilde E_{0T}=\frac{2}{1+\beta}\tilde E_{0I}}
$$

These are the normal-incidence amplitude relations.

---

#### Common approximation: $\mu_1\approx \mu_2$

For most optical media, $\mu_1\approx\mu_2\approx \mu_0$, so

$$
\beta=\frac{v_1}{v_2}.
$$

Then

$$
\boxed{
\tilde E_{0R}=\frac{v_2-v_1}{v_2+v_1}\tilde E_{0I},
\qquad
\tilde E_{0T}=\frac{2v_2}{v_2+v_1}\tilde E_{0I}}
$$

or, using $v=c/n$,

$$
\boxed{
E_{0R}=\left|\frac{n_1-n_2}{n_1+n_2}\right|E_{0I},
\qquad
E_{0T}=\frac{2n_1}{n_1+n_2}E_{0I}}
$$

with the sign of the reflected amplitude encoding phase reversal:

- if $v_2>v_1$ then reflected wave is in phase,
- if $v_2<v_1$ then reflected wave is $180^\circ$ out of phase.

---

#### Reflection and transmission coefficients

Since

$$
I=\frac12 \epsilon v E_0^2,
$$

the reflection coefficient is

$$
\boxed{
R\equiv \frac{I_R}{I_I}
=
\left(\frac{E_{0R}}{E_{0I}}\right)^2
=
\left(\frac{n_1-n_2}{n_1+n_2}\right)^2}
$$

and the transmission coefficient is

$$
\boxed{
T\equiv \frac{I_T}{I_I}
=
\frac{\epsilon_2 v_2}{\epsilon_1 v_1}
\left(\frac{E_{0T}}{E_{0I}}\right)^2
=
\frac{4n_1n_2}{(n_1+n_2)^2}}
$$

when $\mu_1=\mu_2=\mu_0$.

These satisfy

$$
\boxed{R+T=1},
$$

as they must.

---

#### Example / interpretation

For air to glass,

$$
n_1=1,\qquad n_2=1.5,
$$

so

$$
R=0.04,\qquad T=0.96.
$$

Only about $4\%$ is reflected, and most is transmitted.

---

#### Solution technique to remember

For normal incidence:

1. write incident, reflected, transmitted waves with correct propagation directions,
2. be careful with the sign of $\mathbf B_R$,
3. apply tangential boundary conditions at the interface,
4. solve the resulting two linear equations for $\tilde E_{0R}$ and $\tilde E_{0T}$,
5. convert to intensities using $I=\frac12 \epsilon v E_0^2$.

That is the standard template.

### 9.3.3 Reflection and Transmission at Oblique Incidence

#### General setup

Now let the incident wave strike the interface at angle $\theta_I$.

Write

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{\mathbf E}_I(\mathbf r,t)&=\tilde{\mathbf E}_{0I}e^{i(\mathbf k_I\cdot\mathbf r-\omega t)},\\
\tilde{\mathbf B}_I(\mathbf r,t)&=\frac{1}{v_1}(\hat{\mathbf k}_I\times \tilde{\mathbf E}_I),
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{\mathbf E}_R(\mathbf r,t)&=\tilde{\mathbf E}_{0R}e^{i(\mathbf k_R\cdot\mathbf r-\omega t)},\\
\tilde{\mathbf B}_R(\mathbf r,t)&=\frac{1}{v_1}(\hat{\mathbf k}_R\times \tilde{\mathbf E}_R),
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{\mathbf E}_T(\mathbf r,t)&=\tilde{\mathbf E}_{0T}e^{i(\mathbf k_T\cdot\mathbf r-\omega t)},\\
\tilde{\mathbf B}_T(\mathbf r,t)&=\frac{1}{v_2}(\hat{\mathbf k}_T\times \tilde{\mathbf E}_T).
\end{aligned}
$$

All three waves have the same frequency $\omega$, while the wave numbers satisfy

$$
\boxed{
k_I v_1=k_R v_1=k_T v_2=\omega}
$$

so

$$
\boxed{
k_I=k_R=\frac{v_2}{v_1}k_T=\frac{n_1}{n_2}k_T}.
$$

---

#### Matching the phase along the boundary

At $z=0$, the boundary conditions have the generic form

$$
(\cdots)e^{i(\mathbf k_I\cdot\mathbf r-\omega t)}
+
(\cdots)e^{i(\mathbf k_R\cdot\mathbf r-\omega t)}
=
(\cdots)e^{i(\mathbf k_T\cdot\mathbf r-\omega t)}.
$$

Because this must hold for all points on the interface and for all time, the exponential factors must match when $z=0$:

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf k_I\cdot\mathbf r
=
\mathbf k_R\cdot\mathbf r
=
\mathbf k_T\cdot\mathbf r
\qquad (z=0)}
$$

which implies equality of the components parallel to the interface.

---

#### The three laws of geometrical optics

Choose axes so that the incident wave lies in the $xz$ plane. Then the reflected and transmitted wave vectors must also lie in that plane.

So:

##### First law
The incident, reflected, and transmitted wave vectors all lie in a single plane, the **plane of incidence**, which also contains the normal to the surface.

From matching tangential components,

$$
k_I\sin\theta_I=k_R\sin\theta_R=k_T\sin\theta_T.
$$

Using $k_I=k_R$, we get

##### Second law: reflection
$$
\boxed{\theta_I=\theta_R}
$$

and using $k_I/k_T=n_1/n_2$, we get

##### Third law: refraction (Snell’s law)
$$
\boxed{
\frac{\sin\theta_T}{\sin\theta_I}=\frac{n_1}{n_2}}
$$

or equivalently

$$
\boxed{n_1\sin\theta_I=n_2\sin\theta_T}.
$$

A key point is that these laws follow mostly from phase matching, before using the detailed electromagnetic boundary conditions.

---

#### Boundary conditions for amplitudes

After the exponentials cancel, the boundary conditions become relations among amplitudes:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\epsilon_1(\tilde E_{0I}+\tilde E_{0R})_z &= \epsilon_2(\tilde E_{0T})_z,\\
(\tilde B_{0I}+\tilde B_{0R})_z &= (\tilde B_{0T})_z,\\
(\tilde E_{0I}+\tilde E_{0R})_{x,y} &= (\tilde E_{0T})_{x,y},\\
\frac{1}{\mu_1}(\tilde B_{0I}+\tilde B_{0R})_{x,y}
&=
\frac{1}{\mu_2}(\tilde B_{0T})_{x,y}.
\end{aligned}
$$

At this point polarization matters.

---

#### Case 1: polarization parallel to the plane of incidence

Take the incident electric field in the plane of incidence ($xz$-plane). Then the reflected and transmitted fields are also in that plane.

The boundary conditions reduce to

$$
\epsilon_1(-\tilde E_{0I}\sin\theta_I+\tilde E_{0R}\sin\theta_R)
=
\epsilon_2(-\tilde E_{0T}\sin\theta_T),
$$

$$
\tilde E_{0I}\cos\theta_I+\tilde E_{0R}\cos\theta_R
=
\tilde E_{0T}\cos\theta_T,
$$

$$
\frac{1}{\mu_1 v_1}(\tilde E_{0I}-\tilde E_{0R})
=
\frac{1}{\mu_2 v_2}\tilde E_{0T}.
$$

Using the laws of reflection and refraction, the first and third both reduce to

$$
\boxed{\tilde E_{0I}-\tilde E_{0R}=\beta \tilde E_{0T}}
$$

with

$$
\boxed{\beta=\frac{\mu_1 v_1}{\mu_2 v_2}
=\frac{\mu_1 n_2}{\mu_2 n_1}}
$$

while the second becomes

$$
\boxed{\tilde E_{0I}+\tilde E_{0R}=\alpha \tilde E_{0T}}
$$

where

$$
\boxed{\alpha\equiv \frac{\cos\theta_T}{\cos\theta_I}}.
$$

Now solve exactly as in the normal-incidence case:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde E_{0I}-\tilde E_{0R}&=\beta \tilde E_{0T},\\
\tilde E_{0I}+\tilde E_{0R}&=\alpha \tilde E_{0T}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{
\tilde E_{0R}=\frac{\alpha-\beta}{\alpha+\beta}\tilde E_{0I},
\qquad
\tilde E_{0T}=\frac{2}{\alpha+\beta}\tilde E_{0I}}
$$

These are the **Fresnel equations** for polarization parallel to the plane of incidence.

---

#### Angular dependence and Brewster angle

Here

$$
\alpha=\frac{\cos\theta_T}{\cos\theta_I}
=
\frac{\sqrt{1-\sin^2\theta_T}}{\cos\theta_I}
=
\frac{\sqrt{1-\left[(n_1/n_2)\sin\theta_I\right]^2}}{\cos\theta_I}.
$$

So the reflected and transmitted amplitudes depend on the incident angle.

A remarkable feature is the existence of **Brewster’s angle** $\theta_B$, where the reflected wave disappears:

$$
\tilde E_{0R}=0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\alpha=\beta.
$$

This yields

$$
\boxed{
\sin^2\theta_B=\frac{1-\beta^2}{(n_1/n_2)^2-\beta^2}}
$$

and for the common case $\mu_1\approx \mu_2$,

$$
\beta\approx \frac{n_2}{n_1}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\boxed{\tan\theta_B\approx \frac{n_2}{n_1}}.
$$

At this angle, the reflected beam is extinguished for this polarization.

---

#### Intensities for the parallel-polarization case

The power per unit area of interface is the normal component of the Poynting vector, so

$$
\boxed{
I_I=\frac12 \epsilon_1 v_1 E_{0I}^2 \cos\theta_I}
$$

$$
\boxed{
I_R=\frac12 \epsilon_1 v_1 E_{0R}^2 \cos\theta_R,
\qquad
I_T=\frac12 \epsilon_2 v_2 E_{0T}^2 \cos\theta_T}
$$

The extra cosine factors appear because the wave fronts are tilted relative to the interface.

So the reflection and transmission coefficients are

$$
\boxed{
R=\frac{I_R}{I_I}
=
\left(\frac{E_{0R}}{E_{0I}}\right)^2
=
\left(\frac{\alpha-\beta}{\alpha+\beta}\right)^2}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
T=\frac{I_T}{I_I}
=
\frac{\epsilon_2 v_2}{\epsilon_1 v_1}
\left(\frac{E_{0T}}{E_{0I}}\right)^2
\frac{\cos\theta_T}{\cos\theta_I}
=
\alpha\beta\left(\frac{2}{\alpha+\beta}\right)^2}
$$

with

$$
\boxed{R+T=1}.
$$

At Brewster’s angle,

$$
R=0,\qquad T=1.
$$

---

#### Case 2: polarization perpendicular to the plane of incidence

Griffiths leaves the full derivation to the problem set. The important conceptual takeaway is:

- there is another pair of Fresnel equations for this polarization,
- unlike the parallel-polarization case, there is **no Brewster angle** in the generic case,
- the reflected component is not extinguished.

So polarization is not a minor detail; it changes the boundary-value problem.

---

#### Solution technique to remember for oblique incidence

The standard Griffiths strategy is:

1. write incident, reflected, and transmitted plane waves in complex notation,
2. impose equality of phase along the interface,
3. deduce:
   - coplanarity of $\mathbf k_I,\mathbf k_R,\mathbf k_T$,
   - $\theta_I=\theta_R$,
   - Snell’s law,
4. then impose amplitude boundary conditions,
5. solve separately for each polarization.

This is the cleanest way to organize the algebra.
