### Big picture

The goal of this section is to show that, in empty space, Maxwell’s equations imply self-propagating waves of $\mathbf E$ and $\mathbf B$. The key consequences are:

- both $\mathbf E$ and $\mathbf B$ satisfy the wave equation,
- the propagation speed is
  $$
  c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon_0\mu_0}},
  $$
- electromagnetic waves are **transverse**,
- for a plane monochromatic wave, $\mathbf E$, $\mathbf B$, and the propagation direction are mutually perpendicular,
- the wave carries **energy** and **momentum**.

---

### 9.2.1 The wave equation for $\mathbf E$ and $\mathbf B$

In vacuum, where there is no charge and no current, Maxwell’s equations are

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla\cdot \mathbf E &= 0, \\
\nabla\cdot \mathbf B &= 0, \\
\nabla\times \mathbf E &= -\frac{\partial \mathbf B}{\partial t}, \\
\nabla\times \mathbf B &= \mu_0\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf E}{\partial t}.
\end{aligned}
$$

These are coupled first-order PDEs. To decouple them, take the curl of Faraday’s law and of Ampère–Maxwell’s law.

#### Derivation for $\mathbf E$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla\times(\nabla\times \mathbf E)
&= \nabla(\nabla\cdot \mathbf E)-\nabla^2\mathbf E \\
&= \nabla\times\left(-\frac{\partial \mathbf B}{\partial t}\right) \\
&= -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla\times \mathbf B) \\
&= -\mu_0\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf E}{\partial t^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $\nabla\cdot \mathbf E=0$,

$$
\boxed{\nabla^2\mathbf E=\mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf E}{\partial t^2}}.
$$

#### Derivation for $\mathbf B$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla\times(\nabla\times \mathbf B)
&= \nabla(\nabla\cdot \mathbf B)-\nabla^2\mathbf B \\
&= \nabla\times\left(\mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial \mathbf E}{\partial t}\right) \\
&= \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla\times \mathbf E) \\
&= -\mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf B}{\partial t^2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $\nabla\cdot \mathbf B=0$,

$$
\boxed{\nabla^2\mathbf B=\mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf B}{\partial t^2}}.
$$

Thus each Cartesian component satisfies the 3D wave equation

$$
\nabla^2 f=\frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial t^2},
$$

with wave speed

$$
\boxed{v=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon_0\mu_0}}=3.00\times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}.
$$

So in vacuum,

$$
\boxed{c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon_0\mu_0}}}.
$$

#### Conceptual point

This is the central result: empty space itself supports electromagnetic disturbances that travel at speed $c$. This is why light can be understood as an electromagnetic wave.

### 9.2.2 Monochromatic plane waves

#### Setup

Restrict attention to sinusoidal waves of angular frequency $\omega$. For a wave traveling in the $z$ direction, with no $x$ or $y$ dependence, take

$$
\tilde{\mathbf E}(z,t)=\tilde{\mathbf E}_0 e^{i(kz-\omega t)}, 
\qquad
\tilde{\mathbf B}(z,t)=\tilde{\mathbf B}_0 e^{i(kz-\omega t)},
$$

where $\tilde{\mathbf E}_0$ and $\tilde{\mathbf B}_0$ are complex amplitudes, and

$$
\omega=ck.
$$

These are called **plane waves** because the fields are uniform on each plane perpendicular to the direction of propagation.

---

#### Transversality

From $\nabla\cdot \mathbf E=0$ and $\nabla\cdot \mathbf B=0$,

$$
\boxed{(\tilde{\mathbf E}_0)_z=0,\qquad (\tilde{\mathbf B}_0)_z=0}.
$$

So electromagnetic waves are **transverse**: both fields are perpendicular to the propagation direction.

---

#### Relation between $\mathbf E$ and $\mathbf B$

Use Faraday’s law:

$$
\nabla\times \mathbf E=-\frac{\partial \mathbf B}{\partial t}.
$$

For the plane-wave ansatz, this gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
-k(\tilde E_0)_y &= \omega (\tilde B_0)_x, \\
k(\tilde E_0)_x &= \omega (\tilde B_0)_y.
\end{aligned}
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\boxed{\tilde{\mathbf B}_0=\frac{k}{\omega}\left(\hat{\mathbf z}\times \tilde{\mathbf E}_0\right)}.
$$

Hence:

- $\mathbf E \perp \mathbf B$,
- $\mathbf E \perp \hat{\mathbf z}$,
- $\mathbf B \perp \hat{\mathbf z}$.

Also, the fields are **in phase**, and their amplitudes satisfy

$$
\boxed{B_0=\frac{k}{\omega}E_0=\frac{1}{c}E_0}.
$$

So the electric and magnetic fields are locked together.

---

#### Standard example: propagation in $+\hat{\mathbf z}$

If $\mathbf E$ points in the $x$ direction, then $\mathbf B$ points in the $y$ direction:

$$
\tilde{\mathbf E}(z,t)=\tilde E_0 e^{i(kz-\omega t)}\hat{\mathbf x},
\qquad
\tilde{\mathbf B}(z,t)=\frac{1}{c}\tilde E_0 e^{i(kz-\omega t)}\hat{\mathbf y}.
$$

Taking the real part,

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf E(z,t) &= E_0\cos(kz-\omega t+\delta)\,\hat{\mathbf x}, \\
\mathbf B(z,t) &= \frac{1}{c}E_0\cos(kz-\omega t+\delta)\,\hat{\mathbf y}.
\end{aligned}}
$$

This is the basic model of a monochromatic plane wave.

#### Polarization

By convention, polarization is specified by the direction of $\mathbf E$.  
So the wave above is polarized in the $x$ direction.

---

#### General propagation direction

Introduce the propagation vector $\mathbf k$, with magnitude $k$ and direction $\hat{\mathbf k}$.

Then

$$
\boxed{
\tilde{\mathbf E}(\mathbf r,t)=\tilde E_0 e^{i(\mathbf k\cdot \mathbf r-\omega t)}\hat{\mathbf n}}
$$

and

$$
\boxed{
\tilde{\mathbf B}(\mathbf r,t)=\frac{1}{c}\tilde E_0 e^{i(\mathbf k\cdot \mathbf r-\omega t)}(\hat{\mathbf k}\times \hat{\mathbf n})
=\frac{1}{c}\hat{\mathbf k}\times \tilde{\mathbf E}}.
$$

Here $\hat{\mathbf n}$ is the polarization vector, and because the wave is transverse,

$$
\boxed{\hat{\mathbf n}\cdot \hat{\mathbf k}=0}.
$$

The corresponding real fields are

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf E(\mathbf r,t) &= E_0\cos(\mathbf k\cdot \mathbf r-\omega t+\delta)\,\hat{\mathbf n}, \\
\mathbf B(\mathbf r,t) &= \frac{1}{c}E_0\cos(\mathbf k\cdot \mathbf r-\omega t+\delta)\,(\hat{\mathbf k}\times \hat{\mathbf n}).
\end{aligned}}
$$

### 9.2.3 Energy and momentum in electromagnetic waves

The fields do not just oscillate; they transport energy and momentum.

#### Energy density

From the general field-energy formula,

$$
u=\frac{1}{2}\left(\epsilon_0 E^2+\frac{1}{\mu_0}B^2\right).
$$

For a monochromatic plane wave,

$$
B^2=\frac{1}{c^2}E^2=\mu_0\epsilon_0 E^2,
$$

so the electric and magnetic parts are equal. Therefore,

$$
\boxed{u=\epsilon_0 E^2}
$$

and specifically,

$$
\boxed{u=\epsilon_0 E_0^2\cos^2(kz-\omega t+\delta)}.
$$

So in an EM plane wave, energy is equally shared between the electric and magnetic fields.

---

#### Energy flux: Poynting vector

The energy flux density is the Poynting vector

$$
\mathbf S=\frac{1}{\mu_0}(\mathbf E\times \mathbf B).
$$

For a monochromatic plane wave propagating in the $z$ direction,

$$
\boxed{\mathbf S=c\,u\,\hat{\mathbf z}}
$$

or explicitly,

$$
\boxed{\mathbf S=c\epsilon_0 E_0^2\cos^2(kz-\omega t+\delta)\,\hat{\mathbf z}}.
$$

So $\mathbf S$ points in the propagation direction, and its magnitude is energy density times wave speed.

---

#### Momentum density

From the field-momentum formula,

$$
\mathbf g=\frac{1}{c^2}\mathbf S.
$$

Hence for a monochromatic plane wave,

$$
\boxed{\mathbf g=\frac{1}{c}u\,\hat{\mathbf z}}
$$

or

$$
\boxed{\mathbf g=\frac{1}{c}\epsilon_0 E_0^2\cos^2(kz-\omega t+\delta)\,\hat{\mathbf z}}.
$$

So electromagnetic waves carry momentum in the same direction as energy flow.

---

### Time averages over a cycle

Because optical frequencies are extremely high, most measurements see cycle averages rather than the instantaneous oscillation.

Using
$$
\langle \cos^2(\cdots)\rangle=\frac{1}{2},
$$

we get

$$
\boxed{\langle u\rangle=\frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E_0^2}
$$

$$
\boxed{\langle \mathbf S\rangle=\frac{1}{2}c\epsilon_0 E_0^2\,\hat{\mathbf z}}
$$

$$
\boxed{\langle \mathbf g\rangle=\frac{1}{2c}\epsilon_0 E_0^2\,\hat{\mathbf z}}
$$

---

### Intensity

The average power per unit area carried by the wave is the **intensity**:

$$
\boxed{I\equiv \langle S\rangle=\frac{1}{2}c\epsilon_0 E_0^2}.
$$

This is one of the most useful formulas in applications.

---

### Radiation pressure

When light strikes a perfect absorber normally, it transfers momentum to the surface. The resulting average pressure is

$$
\boxed{P=\frac{I}{c}}.
$$

For a perfect reflector, the momentum reverses direction, so the pressure doubles:

$$
\boxed{P_{\text{reflector}}=\frac{2I}{c}}.
$$

### Summary sheet

#### Vacuum wave equations
$$
\boxed{
\nabla^2\mathbf E=\mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf E}{\partial t^2},
\qquad
\nabla^2\mathbf B=\mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf B}{\partial t^2}}
$$

#### Wave speed
$$
\boxed{c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon_0\mu_0}}}
$$

#### Plane-wave structure
$$
\boxed{
\mathbf E(\mathbf r,t)=E_0\cos(\mathbf k\cdot \mathbf r-\omega t+\delta)\,\hat{\mathbf n}}
$$

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf B(\mathbf r,t)=\frac{1}{c}E_0\cos(\mathbf k\cdot \mathbf r-\omega t+\delta)\,(\hat{\mathbf k}\times \hat{\mathbf n})}
$$

with
$$
\boxed{\hat{\mathbf n}\cdot \hat{\mathbf k}=0}
$$

and
$$
\boxed{B_0=\frac{E_0}{c}}.
$$

#### Energy and momentum
$$
\boxed{u=\epsilon_0 E^2}
$$

$$
\boxed{\mathbf S=\frac{1}{\mu_0}(\mathbf E\times \mathbf B)=c\,u\,\hat{\mathbf k}}
$$

$$
\boxed{\mathbf g=\frac{1}{c^2}\mathbf S=\frac{u}{c}\hat{\mathbf k}}
$$

#### Averages
$$
\boxed{\langle u\rangle=\frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E_0^2}
$$

$$
\boxed{\langle \mathbf S\rangle=\frac{1}{2}c\epsilon_0 E_0^2\,\hat{\mathbf k}}
$$

$$
\boxed{\langle \mathbf g\rangle=\frac{1}{2c}\epsilon_0 E_0^2\,\hat{\mathbf k}}
$$

#### Intensity and pressure
$$
\boxed{I=\frac{1}{2}c\epsilon_0 E_0^2}
$$

$$
\boxed{P_{\text{absorber}}=\frac{I}{c},\qquad P_{\text{reflector}}=\frac{2I}{c}}
$$
