## 4.1.1 Dielectircs

> most everyday objects belong to one of two large classes: **conductors** and **insulators** (dielectrics)

In **dielectrics**, unlike conductors that has unlimited supply of electrons to move around, *all charges are attached to specific atoms or molecules*.

The electrons can only:
$$
\begin{align}
  \text{ move a bit \textit{within} the atom}  & \to  \text{cumulative effects}
\end{align}
$$
which accounts for characteristics behaviors. 

## 4.1.2 Induced Dipoles for Neutral Atom

The electrons and the nucleus inside a *neutral* atom experience:
- electric field $\mathbf{E}$ pulling them apart
- mutual attraction (what force?) drawing them back
The resulted equilibrium leaves the atom **polarized**.

In particular, for dipoles for Atom, the slightly shifted charges:
$$
\qquad+\leftleftarrows\qquad \rightrightarrows -
$$
lead to a tiny dipole moment $\mathbf{p}$ in the **same direction as** $\mathbf{E}$. 

> [!claim|*]
> The induced dipole moment is approximately proportional to the field 
> $$
\mathbf{p}=\alpha \mathbf{E}
>$$
> where $\alpha = 4\pi \epsilon_{0}a^{3}=3\epsilon_{0}v$ is the **atomic polarizability**, 

Dipoles for Molecules
- more complicated than atom due to particular angles of polarization that occurs more readily than others.
- induced dipole moment may not be in the same direction as $\mathbf{E}$
- molecules may be asymmatrical (polarizability tensor $\alpha_{ij}$ is needed)

## 4.1.4 Polarization

Recall:

Consider external electric field on 
- atom
- molecule

The main question we ask is: 

> What happens to a piece of dielectric material when it is placed in an electric field?

> [!definition]
> The material becomes polarized if *a lot of little dipoles pointing along the direction of the field*, and we write polarization as
> $$
> \mathbf{P}\equiv \text{dipole moment / unit volume}
> $$
