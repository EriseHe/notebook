## 4.4.1 Susceptibility, Permittivity, Dielectric Constant

> We dealt with the *effect* of the polarization $\mathbf{P}$, but what about the *cause*?

This set of notes transitions between **Chapter 4 (Linear Dielectrics)** and **Chapter 5 (Magnetostatics)**. It covers how electric fields behave in matter and then introduces the fundamental laws governing magnetic fields from steady currents.

---

## 4.4 Linear Dielectrics (Electrostatics)

In many substances, the polarization $\mathbf{P}$ is directly proportional to the total electric field $\mathbf{E}$, provided the field is not too strong. These are called **linear dielectrics**.

### 1. Susceptibility and Permittivity
The proportionality is defined using the **electric susceptibility** ($\chi_e$):
$$\mathbf{P} = \epsilon_0 \chi_e \mathbf{E}$$

This allows us to write the **electric displacement** ($\mathbf{D}$) in terms of the field alone:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{D} &= \epsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P} \\
&= \epsilon_0(1 + \chi_e)\mathbf{E} \\
&= \epsilon \mathbf{E}
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!definition] Constants of Linear Media
> * **Permittivity** ($\epsilon$): $\epsilon \equiv \epsilon_0(1 + \chi_e)$
> * **Relative Permittivity** ($\epsilon_r$): $\epsilon_r \equiv 1 + \chi_e = \epsilon / \epsilon_0$ (also called the **dielectric constant**).

### 2. Physical Effects
* **Field Reduction**: In a homogeneous linear dielectric that fills all space, the electric field is reduced by a factor of $1/\epsilon_r$ compared to the vacuum field ($\mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_r} \mathbf{E}_{\text{vac}}$).
* **Shielding**: The polarization of the medium "shields" embedded charges by surrounding them with bound charge of the opposite sign.
* **Crystals**: In anisotropic materials like crystals, susceptibility is a **tensor**, meaning $\mathbf{P}$ might not point in the same direction as $\mathbf{E}$.


## 4.4.2 Boundary Value Problems with Linear Dielectrics

The introduction of linear media simplifies boundary value problems significantly. In a homogeneous linear dielectric, the bound charge density ($\rho_b$) is directly proportional to the free charge density ($\rho_f$).

### 1. Bound vs. Free Charge
Within a dielectric, the bound charge density is given by:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\rho_b &= -\nabla \cdot \mathbf{P} = -\nabla \cdot \left( \epsilon_0 \frac{\chi_e}{\epsilon} \mathbf{D} \right) \\
&= -\left( \frac{\chi_e}{1 + \chi_e} \right) \rho_f
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!important] The "Zero" Rule
> Unless free charge is actually embedded in the material ($\rho_f \neq 0$), the total volume charge density $\rho$ is zero. This means within the dielectric, the potential $V$ still obeys **Laplace's equation** ($\nabla^2 V = 0$).

### 2. Boundary Conditions
When solving for $V$ at the interface between two media, we use the following conditions:

| Variable | Boundary Condition | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Potential ($V$)** | $V_{\text{above}} = V_{\text{below}}$ | Potential is always continuous. |
| **Electric Field ($\mathbf{E}$)** | $\epsilon_{\text{above}} E_{\text{above}}^{\perp} - \epsilon_{\text{below}} E_{\text{below}}^{\perp} = \sigma_f$ | Accounts for free surface charge. |
| **In terms of $V$** | $\epsilon_{\text{above}} \frac{\partial V_{\text{above}}}{\partial n} - \epsilon_{\text{below}} \frac{\partial V_{\text{below}}}{\partial n} = -\sigma_f$ | Derivative w.r.t. the normal vector. |

## 4.4.3 Energy in Dielectric Systems

How much work does it take to charge up a system when dielectrics are present? The answer depends on what you mean by "energy."

### 1. The Capacitor Case
If a capacitor is filled with a linear dielectric, its capacitance increases:
$$C = \epsilon_r C_{\text{vac}}$$
Consequently, the work required to charge it to a specific voltage $V$ increases by the same factor:
$$W = \frac{1}{2} CV^2$$
This extra work is necessary because you must overcome the field produced by the **bound charges** that are trying to cancel out your efforts.

### 2. General Energy Formula
For a linear dielectric system, the total work done to assemble the free charge is:
$$\boxed{W = \frac{1}{2} \int \mathbf{D} \cdot \mathbf{E} d\tau}$$

### 3. Two Ways to Count Energy
Griffiths highlights a "subtle distinction" in what we call the "energy of a system":

1.  **Total Energy (Eq. 2.45):** $W = \frac{\epsilon_0}{2} \int E^2 d\tau$. This represents the work to assemble **every** charge (free and bound) piece by piece. It does *not* include the energy stored in the "molecular springs" (the stretching/twisting of atoms).
2.  **Free Charge Energy (Eq. 4.58):** $W = \frac{1}{2} \int \mathbf{D} \cdot \mathbf{E} d\tau$. This represents the work to bring in the **free charges only**, allowing the dielectric to polarize naturally. This *does* include the energy stored in the molecular springs.


> [!warning] Linearity Required
> Eq. 4.58 ($W = \frac{1}{2} \int \mathbf{D} \cdot \mathbf{E} d\tau$) is **only valid for linear dielectrics**. If the system is non-linear or dissipative (like an electret with "frozen-in" polarization), the work done depends on the path taken, and the simple integral fails.

---

### Summary Checklist
* **Boundary Conditions**: Use $D$ for free charge and $V$ for continuity.
* **Energy**: Use $\frac{1}{2} \mathbf{D} \cdot \mathbf{E}$ if you only care about the work done on free charges.
* **Homogeneity**: If $\rho_f = 0$ in the bulk, $\rho_b = 0$ in the bulk too! All charge resides on the surface.


# 4.4 Linear Dielectrics

In the previous sections, we analyzed the effects of polarization ($\mathbf{P}$) without committing to its cause. In this section, we assume the polarization is sustained by an applied electric field and is proportional to that field.

---

## 4.4.1 Susceptibility, Permittivity, and Dielectric Constant

For many substances, the polarization is proportional to the total field $\mathbf{E}$, provided the field is not too strong:

$$\mathbf{P} = \epsilon_0 \chi_e \mathbf{E}$$

> [!definition] Electric Susceptibility ($\chi_e$)
> $\chi_e$ is a dimensionless constant of proportionality that depends on the microscopic structure of the substance and external conditions like temperature.

### 1. The Displacement Field (D)
In linear media, the displacement field $\mathbf{D}$ is also proportional to $\mathbf{E}$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{D} &= \epsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P} \\
&= \epsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \epsilon_0 \chi_e \mathbf{E} \\
&= \epsilon_0(1 + \chi_e)\mathbf{E} \\
&= \epsilon \mathbf{E}
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!definition] Permittivity and Dielectric Constant
> * **Permittivity ($\epsilon$):** Defined as $\epsilon \equiv \epsilon_0(1 + \chi_e)$.
> * **Relative Permittivity ($\epsilon_r$):** Also called the **dielectric constant**, defined as $\epsilon_r \equiv 1 + \chi_e = \epsilon/\epsilon_0$.



### 2. Anisotropy and the Susceptibility Tensor
In crystals, it is often easier to polarize the material in certain directions. In such cases, the linear relation is replaced by a general tensor relation:
$$P_i = \epsilon_0 \sum_{j} \chi_{eij} E_j$$
The nine coefficients $\chi_{exx}, \chi_{exy}, \dots$ constitute the **susceptibility tensor**.

---

## 4.4.2 Boundary Value Problems with Linear Dielectrics

In a homogeneous isotropic linear dielectric, the bound charge density $\rho_b$ is proportional to the free charge density $\rho_f$:
$$\rho_b = -\nabla \cdot \mathbf{P} = -\nabla \cdot \left( \epsilon_0 \frac{\chi_e}{\epsilon} \mathbf{D} \right) = -\left( \frac{\chi_e}{1 + \chi_e} \right) \rho_f$$

**Key Implication:** Unless free charge is embedded in the material, $\rho_b = 0$ in the bulk. Any net charge must reside on the surface. Within the dielectric, the potential obeys **Laplace’s equation** ($\nabla^2 V = 0$).

### 1. Boundary Conditions
The conditions for solving the potential $V$ are expressed in terms of free charge:
* **Potential Continuity:** $V_{\text{above}} = V_{\text{below}}$
* **Field Jump (Normal):** $\epsilon_{\text{above}} E_{\text{above}}^{\perp} - \epsilon_{\text{below}} E_{\text{below}}^{\perp} = \sigma_f$
* **Potential Derivative:** $\epsilon_{\text{above}} \frac{\partial V_{\text{above}}}{\partial n} - \epsilon_{\text{below}} \frac{\partial V_{\text{below}}}{\partial n} = -\sigma_f$

---

## 4.4.3 Energy in Dielectric Systems

To calculate the work done in charging a system with dielectrics, we must account for the work done on the free charges while the dielectric responds.

### 1. General Derivation for Work
To increase the free charge by an amount $\Delta \rho_f$, the work done is:
$$\Delta W = \int (\Delta \rho_f)V d\tau$$
Since $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho_f$, then $\Delta \rho_f = \nabla \cdot (\Delta \mathbf{D})$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta W &= \int [\nabla \cdot (\Delta \mathbf{D})]V d\tau \\
&= \int \nabla \cdot [(\Delta \mathbf{D})V] d\tau + \int (\Delta \mathbf{D}) \cdot \mathbf{E} d\tau && \text{\scriptsize (Integration by parts)}
\end{aligned}
$$
The first term vanishes as a surface integral over all space. For **linear media**, $(\Delta \mathbf{D}) \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{2} \Delta (\mathbf{D} \cdot \mathbf{E})$.

> [!important] Total Stored Energy
> Integrating from zero to the final configuration:
> $$\boxed{W = \frac{1}{2} \int \mathbf{D} \cdot \mathbf{E} d\tau}$$

**Equation 2.45 vs 4.58:** Eq 2.45 ($W = \frac{\epsilon_0}{2} \int E^2 d\tau$) counts the work to assemble all charges (free and bound). Eq 4.58 includes the "spring energy" stored in the polarized molecules because it accounts for the work done on the free charges as they "stretch" those molecular springs.

---

## 4.4.4 Forces on Dielectrics

A dielectric is attracted into an electric field. In a parallel-plate capacitor, this is caused by the **fringing field**—the non-uniform field at the edges of the plates.



### 1. Force Calculation (Energy Method)
If the dielectric is pulled out by $dx$, the work done is $dW = F_{\text{me}} dx$. The electrical force is $F = -dW/dx$.
* **Constant Charge ($Q$):** $W = \frac{1}{2} \frac{Q^2}{C}$
  $$F = -\frac{dW}{dx} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{Q^2}{C^2} \frac{dC}{dx} = \frac{1}{2} V^2 \frac{dC}{dx}$$
* **Constant Potential ($V$):** $dW = F_{\text{me}} dx + V dQ$. This yields the same result for $F$ but requires accounting for the work done by the battery.

> [!important] Result for a Slab
> For a slab of width $w$ and thickness $d$ inserted a distance $x$:
> $$F = -\frac{\epsilon_0 \chi_e w}{2d} V^2$$
> The negative sign indicates the dielectric is pulled **into** the capacitor.
