## 6.3 The Auxiliary Field H

### 6.3.1 Ampère’s Law in Magnetized Materials
In magnetized materials, the total current density $\mathbf{J}$ is the sum of the **free current** $\mathbf{J}_f$ (the current flowing through wires or conductors that we hook up to a battery) and the **bound current** $\mathbf{J}_b$ (the current resulting from the alignment of atomic dipoles).

**Derivation of the H-field:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1}{\mu_0} (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) &= \mathbf{J} && \text{\scriptsize Ampère's law in terms of total current} \\
&= \mathbf{J}_f + \mathbf{J}_b && \text{\scriptsize total current is the sum of free and bound} \\
&= \mathbf{J}_f + (\nabla \times \mathbf{M}) && \text{\scriptsize substituting } \mathbf{J}_b = \nabla \times \mathbf{M} \\
\nabla \times \left( \frac{1}{\mu_0} \mathbf{B} - \mathbf{M} \right) &= \mathbf{J}_f && \text{\scriptsize collecting the curl operators to the left side}
\end{aligned}
$$

The quantity in the parentheses is designated by the letter $\mathbf{H}$.

> [!definition] The Auxiliary Field ($\mathbf{H}$)
> The field $\mathbf{H}$ is defined to isolate the effects of free currents:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{H} \equiv \frac{1}{\mu_0} \mathbf{B} - \mathbf{M}}$$
> In terms of $\mathbf{H}$, Ampère's law takes a much simpler form, involving only the current we can directly control and measure:
> * **Differential Form**: $\boxed{\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J}_f}$
> * **Integral Form**: $\boxed{\oint \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_{f, \text{enc}}}$


---

### 6.3.2 A Deceptive Parallel
It is tempting to think that $\mathbf{H}$ is just a "rescaled" version of $\mathbf{B}$, but Griffiths warns that this is a "deceptive parallel." The relationship between $\mathbf{B}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ is not as simple as $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{D}$.

#### 1. The Problem of Divergence
While the divergence of $\mathbf{B}$ is always zero ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$), the divergence of $\mathbf{H}$ generally is **not** zero.

**Derivation of the Divergence of H:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{H} &= \nabla \cdot \left( \frac{1}{\mu_0} \mathbf{B} - \mathbf{M} \right) && \text{\scriptsize applying the definition of } \mathbf{H} \\
&= \frac{1}{\mu_0} \underbrace{(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B})}_{0} - \nabla \cdot \mathbf{M} && \text{\scriptsize since there are no magnetic monopoles} \\
&= \boxed{-\nabla \cdot \mathbf{M}} && \text{\scriptsize (Eq. 6.23)}
\end{aligned}
$$
Because $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{H}$ is not necessarily zero, there is no "Biot-Savart" law for $\mathbf{H}$, and you cannot generally assume $\mathbf{H}$ is zero just because there is no free current.


#### 2. Practical vs. Theoretical Utility
* **Practical ($\mathbf{H}$)**: In the lab, $\mathbf{H}$ is often more "useful" because its source is the free current $I_f$—the thing you read on the dial of your power supply. 
* **Theoretical ($\mathbf{B}$)**: Theoretically, $\mathbf{B}$ remains the fundamental quantity. It is the field that appears in the Lorentz force law and is the one that is truly divergenceless.

> [!important] Solving Strategy
> When asked to find $\mathbf{B}$ or $\mathbf{H}$ in a problem with magnetic materials:
> 1. **Look for Symmetry**: If the problem has cylindrical, plane, solenoidal, or toroidal symmetry, find $\mathbf{H}$ directly from the free current using the integral form of Ampère's Law ($\oint \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_{f, \text{enc}}$).
> 2. **Calculate B**: Once you have $\mathbf{H}$, use the definition $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M})$ to find the total field.

---

### Summary Checklist for H
* $\mathbf{H}$ is to $\mathbf{B}$ what $\mathbf{D}$ is to $\mathbf{E}$.
* It "ignores" bound currents, focusing only on $\mathbf{J}_f$.
* **Warning**: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{H} \neq 0$ unless $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{M} = 0$. This means $\mathbf{H}$ lines can "start" and "end" where magnetization changes.
## 6.3.3 Boundary Conditions 

When crossing an interface between two different magnetic materials, use this table as your primary reference. Remember the golden rule: **B** handles the **normal** component, and **H** handles the **tangential** component.

### 1. The Core Comparison Table

| Component | Magnetic Field ($\mathbf{B}$) | Auxiliary Field ($\mathbf{H}$) | Exam Note |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Normal ($\perp$)** | $B_{\text{above}}^{\perp} = B_{\text{below}}^{\perp}$ | $H_{\text{above}}^{\perp} - H_{\text{below}}^{\perp} = -(M_{\text{above}}^{\perp} - M_{\text{below}}^{\perp})$ | **B is ALWAYS continuous.** |
| **Tangential ($\parallel$)** | $\mathbf{B}_{\text{above}}^{\parallel} - \mathbf{B}_{\text{below}}^{\parallel} = \mu_0 (\mathbf{K} \times \hat{\mathbf{n}})$ | $\mathbf{H}_{\text{above}}^{\parallel} - \mathbf{H}_{\text{below}}^{\parallel} = \mathbf{K}_f \times \hat{\mathbf{n}}$ | **H only sees FREE current.** |



### 2. Key Vector Equations (Must Memorize)

$$
\begin{aligned}
B_{\text{above}}^{\perp} - B_{\text{below}}^{\perp} &= 0 \\
\mathbf{H}_{\text{above}}^{\parallel} - \mathbf{H}_{\text{below}}^{\parallel} &= \mathbf{K}_f \times \hat{\mathbf{n}}
\end{aligned}
$$

### 3. Practical "Speed-Run" Tips

1.  **No Free Current ($K_f = 0$):** This is the most common exam scenario. If there is no free surface current, the **tangential component of H is continuous**.
    * *Strategy*: Calculate $\mathbf{H}$ using Ampère’s law, cross the boundary using tangential continuity, then convert back to $\mathbf{B}$ using $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M})$.
2.  **Normal Discontinuity:** If the magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ starts or stops at the boundary (like the end of a bar magnet), the normal component of $\mathbf{H}$ **will jump**.
3.  **The "A" Continuity:** Note that the vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ is **always continuous** across any boundary ($\mathbf{A}_{\text{above}} = \mathbf{A}_{\text{below}}$).
