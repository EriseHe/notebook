
## 6.2 The Field of a Magnetized Object

This section explores how to calculate the magnetic field produced by a magnetized material. The key theoretical breakthrough is showing that a magnetized object can be replaced by an equivalent distribution of "bound" currents.

### 6.2.1 Bound Currents
If the magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ (dipole moment per unit volume) of an object is known, we can find the total vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ by integrating the contributions from all infinitesimal dipoles $d\mathbf{m} = \mathbf{M} d\tau'$.



**Derivation of the Potential from Magnetization:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) &= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}') \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} d\tau' && \text{\scriptsize starting from the potential of a single dipole (Eq. 6.11)} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \left[ \mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}') \times \left( \nabla' \frac{1}{\boldsymbol{r}} \right) \right] d\tau' && \text{\scriptsize utilizing the identity } \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{\boldsymbol{r}^2} = \nabla' \frac{1}{\boldsymbol{r}} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \left\{ \int \frac{1}{\boldsymbol{r}} [\nabla' \times \mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}')] d\tau' - \int \nabla' \times \left[ \frac{\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}')}{\boldsymbol{r}} \right] d\tau' \right\} && \text{\scriptsize applying vector product rule 7} \\
&= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{1}{\boldsymbol{r}} [\nabla' \times \mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}')] d\tau' + \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \oint \frac{1}{\boldsymbol{r}} [\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}') \times d\mathbf{a}'] && \text{\scriptsize converting second volume integral to surface integral} \\
&= \underbrace{\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}_b(\mathbf{r}')}{\boldsymbol{r}} d\tau'}_{\text{potential of volume current}} + \underbrace{\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \oint \frac{\mathbf{K}_b(\mathbf{r}')}{\boldsymbol{r}} da'}_{\text{potential of surface current}} && \text{\scriptsize substituting bound current definitions (Eq. 6.15)}
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!definition] Bound Current Densities
> The field of a magnetized object is identical to the field produced by a specific distribution of "bound" currents:
> * **Bound Volume Current**: $\boxed{\mathbf{J}_b = \nabla \times \mathbf{M}}$
> * **Bound Surface Current**: $\boxed{\mathbf{K}_b = \mathbf{M} \times \hat{\mathbf{n}}}$

---

### 6.2.2 Physical Interpretation of Bound Currents
These currents are not "free" to move through the material; they represent the macroscopic average of billions of tiny atomic current loops.

#### 1. Surface Current ($\mathbf{K}_b$)
In a uniformly magnetized material, the internal current loops of adjacent atoms cancel one another out. However, at the surface, the loops are uncompensated, resulting in a net current flowing around the boundary.



**Relation to Magnetization:**
Consider a slab of thickness $t$. The dipole moment of a single loop is $m = I a$, where $a$ is the area. The total magnetization is $M = m / (at) = I/t$. Thus, the current per unit length is $K_b = I/t = M$. The vector form $\mathbf{K}_b = \mathbf{M} \times \hat{\mathbf{n}}$ accounts for the orientation relative to the surface normal.

#### 2. Volume Current ($\mathbf{J}_b$)
If the magnetization is non-uniform, the internal cancellation is no longer perfect. The "mismatch" between adjacent loops creates a net current in the bulk of the material, described by the curl of $\mathbf{M}$.



---

### 6.2.3 The Magnetic Field inside Matter
The microscopic magnetic field inside a material fluctuates violently at the atomic scale. When we talk about the "field in matter," we mean the **macroscopic field**: the average over a region large enough to contain many atoms.

> [!important] 
> Magnetization effects are typically much weaker than fields from free currents in ordinary conductors (like copper). We only consider $\mathbf{M}$ significant when dealing with magnetic materials (like iron) or permanent magnets.
