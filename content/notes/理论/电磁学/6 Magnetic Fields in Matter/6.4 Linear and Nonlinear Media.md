
# Section 6.4: Linear and Nonlinear Media

This section bridges the gap between theoretical definitions and material science, categorizing how substances respond to external magnetic fields.

---

## 6.4.1 Linear Media: Susceptibility and Permeability

In many substances (paramagnets and diamagnets), the magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ is **proportional** to the auxiliary field $\mathbf{H}$, provided the field is not too strong. These are called **linear media**.

### 1. Fundamental Definitions
> [!definition] Magnetic Susceptibility ($\chi_m$)
> The dimensionless constant of proportionality relating $\mathbf{M}$ and $\mathbf{H}$:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{M} = \chi_m \mathbf{H}}$$
> * **Paramagnets**: $\chi_m > 0$ (typically $\sim 10^{-5}$)
> * **Diamagnets**: $\chi_m < 0$ (typically $\sim -10^{-5}$)

### 2. Magnetic Permeability
Using the relationship $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M})$, we can express the total magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ solely in terms of $\mathbf{H}$:
$$\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \chi_m \mathbf{H}) = \mu_0(1 + \chi_m)\mathbf{H}$$

> [!definition] Permeability ($\mu$)
> The total permeability of a material is defined as:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{B} = \mu \mathbf{H}} \quad \text{where} \quad \boxed{\mu \equiv \mu_0(1 + \chi_m)}$$
> * **$\mu_0$**: Permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ N/A}^2$).
> * In a vacuum, $\chi_m = 0$, so $\mu = \mu_0$.

---

## 2. Bound Currents in Linear Media
In a **homogeneous** linear material (where $\chi_m$ is constant throughout the volume), the internal bound current density $\mathbf{J}_b$ has a special relationship with the free current density $\mathbf{J}_f$:

$$
\mathbf{J}_b = \nabla \times \mathbf{M} = \nabla \times (\chi_m \mathbf{H}) = \chi_m (\nabla \times \mathbf{H}) = \chi_m \mathbf{J}_f
$$

> [!important] The "Surface Current" Rule
> If no free current flows *through* the material ($\mathbf{J}_f = 0$), then $\mathbf{J}_b = 0$ as well. In this case, **all bound current resides strictly at the surface**.

---

## 3. The Divergence Headache
While $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ is a law of nature, $\mathbf{H}$ and $\mathbf{M}$ are not generally divergenceless.
* **At boundaries**: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{M}$ can be infinite where the material ends.
* **$\mathbf{H}$ Field**: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{H} = -\nabla \cdot \mathbf{M}$. 
* **Calculation Warning**: $\mathbf{H}$ is not divergenceless at points where the permeability $\mu$ is changing. This is why a Gaussian pillbox for $\mathbf{M}$ often yields a non-zero flux (Fig. 6.23).

## 6.4.2 Nonlinear Media: Ferromagnetism

Ferromagnets (like Iron, Nickel, and Cobalt) are **emphatically non-linear**. They do not follow $\mathbf{M} = \chi_m \mathbf{H}$.

### 1. Domains and Saturation
* **Magnetic Domains**: Alignment occurs in microscopic patches where dipoles line up parallel to one another due to quantum mechanical interactions.
* **Random Orientation**: In an unmagnetized wrench, these domains point in random directions, canceling each other out.
* **Saturation**: When an external field is strong enough, one domain eventually takes over the entire object. At this point, increasing $\mathbf{H}$ further has no effect on $\mathbf{M}$.

### 2. Hysteresis
Ferromagnets have "memory"—their current state depends on their past history. 

| Term | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Residual Magnetization** | The magnetization remaining when the external field is turned off. |
| **Coercive Field** | The reverse field needed to drive the magnetization back to zero. |
| **Hysteresis Loop** | The path traced on a $B$ vs $H$ plot as the external field is cycled. |

### 3. The Curie Point
Ferromagnetism is temperature-dependent. Random thermal motions compete with the dipole alignment.
> [!definition] Curie Point ($T_C$)
> A precise temperature above which a ferromagnetic material loses its "frozen-in" alignment and becomes abruptly **paramagnetic**. 
> * For Iron: $T_C \approx 770^\circ\text{C}$. This is a **phase transition**.

---

### Summary Checklist for Exam
1.  **Linear**: $\mathbf{B} = \mu \mathbf{H}$. Use this to simplify Ampère's Law problems.
2.  **Susceptibility**: $\chi_m$ is negative for diamagnets, positive for paramagnets.
3.  **$\mathbf{J}_b = \chi_m \mathbf{J}_f$**: Only valid for homogeneous linear media.
4.  **Ferromagnets**: Use the concept of domains and hysteresis; do not use $\mu$ or $\chi_m$ unless the problem specifies a "small linear region."
