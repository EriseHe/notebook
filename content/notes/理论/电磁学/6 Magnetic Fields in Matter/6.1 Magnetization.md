## 6.1.1 Diamagnets, Paramagnets, Ferromagnets

In classical electrodynamics, all magnetic phenomena are due to **electric charges in motion**. 

> [!definition] Magnetization
> Magnetization is the process of inducing a net alignment of magnetic dipoles within a medium.

If we examine magnetic materials at the atomic scale, we find "tiny currents" caused by two primary motions:

- **Orbital Motion:** Electrons orbiting around the nuclei.
- **Spin:** Electrons spinning about their own axes.

### Classification of Magnetic Materials 

Unlike electric polarization (which is in the same direction as the electric field $\mathbf{E}$), the response of a material to a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ depends on its internal structure. Materials are classified into three main categories:

| Material Type    | Response to Applied Field $\mathbf{B}$                   | Key Characteristics                                              |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Paramagnets**  | magnetization **parallel** to $\mathbf{B}$.              | caused by the torque of the external field.                      |
| **Diamagnets**   | magnetization **opposite** to $\mathbf{B}$.              | resulted by the changes in orbital speeds (Lenz's law analogy).  |
| **Ferromagnets** | retain magnetization even after $\mathbf{B}$ is removed. | defined by the “*history*” of the object; the most complex type. |

## 6.1.2 Torques and Forces on Magnetic Dipoles
### Torque on a Rectangular Loop

We use a rectangular loop of sides $a$ and $b$ carrying current $I$ to represent a simplified model for atom for mathematical convenience.

The torque $\mathbf{N}$ on , tilted at an angle $\theta$ in a uniform field $\mathbf{B} = B \hat{\mathbf{z}}$, is derived as:
$$\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{N} &= {a F \sin \theta}\hat{\mathbf{x}} && \text{\scriptsize lever arm $\times$ force} \\
&= a ({I b B}) \sin \theta \hat{\mathbf{x}} &&  \scriptsize F = IbB \text{ on vertical segments} \\
&= \underbrace{I a b}_{m} B \sin \theta \hat{\mathbf{x}} &&  \\
&= m B \sin \theta \hat{\mathbf{x}} && \text{\scriptsize applying the magnitude of the dipole moment} \\
&= \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B} && \text{\scriptsize vector cross product form (Eq. 6.1)}
\end{aligned}$$

> [!definition] Torque and Magnetic Dipole moment
> The torque $\mathbf{N}$ on , tilted at an angle $\theta$ in a uniform field $\mathbf{B} = B \hat{\mathbf{z}}$, is 
> $$ \boxed{\mathbf{N} = \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B}} \quad \text{where} \quad m = Iab$$
> is the magnetic dipole moment (or more generally, $m = I A$ where $A$ is the area).

2. Qualitative Origin of Paramagnetism

The torque $\mathbf{N}=\mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B}$ is what accounts for paramagnetism:

- **The Mechanism**: The torque acts in such a direction as to line the dipole up parallel to the field.
- **The Constraint**: While every electron is a magnetic dipole (a "tiny spinning charged sphere"), the Pauli exclusion principle usually locks electrons into pairs with opposing spins, neutralizing the torque.
- **The Result**: Paramagnetism occurs primarily in atoms with an odd number of electrons, where an "extra" unpaired member is subject to the magnetic torque.

### Forces in Non-Uniform Fields

In a non-uniform field, a net force $\mathbf{F}$ is exerted. For an infinitesimal loop with dipole moment $\mathbf{m}$:$$\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{F} &= \nabla (\underbrace{\mathbf{m} \cdot \mathbf{B}}_{U}) && \text{Force as the gradient of the interaction energy (Eq. 6.3)}
\end{aligned}$$For the specific case of a circular wire ring of radius $R$ in the "fringing" region of a solenoid:$$\begin{aligned}
F &= \underbrace{I (2\pi R)}_{I \times \text{circumference}} B \cos \theta && \text{Net downward force (Eq. 6.2)}
\end{aligned}$$
