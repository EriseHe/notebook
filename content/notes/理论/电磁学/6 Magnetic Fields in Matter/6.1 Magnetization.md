## 6.1.1 Diamagnets, Paramagnets, Ferromagnets

In classical electrodynamics, all magnetic phenomena are due to **electric charges in motion**. 

> [!definition] Magnetization
> Magnetization is the process of inducing a net alignment of magnetic dipoles within a medium.

If we examine magnetic materials at the atomic scale, we find "tiny currents" caused by two primary motions:

- **Orbital Motion:** Electrons orbiting around the nuclei.
- **Spin:** Electrons spinning about their own axes.

### Classification of Magnetic Materials 

Unlike electric polarization (which is in the same direction as the electric field $\mathbf{E}$), the response of a material to a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ depends on its internal structure. Materials are classified into three main categories:

| Material Type    | Response to Applied Field $\mathbf{B}$                   | Key Characteristics                                              |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Paramagnets**  | magnetization **parallel** to $\mathbf{B}$.              | caused by the torque of the external field.                      |
| **Diamagnets**   | magnetization **opposite** to $\mathbf{B}$.              | resulted by the changes in orbital speeds (Lenz's law analogy).  |
| **Ferromagnets** | retain magnetization even after $\mathbf{B}$ is removed. | defined by the “*history*” of the object; the most complex type. |

## 6.1.2 Torques and Forces on Magnetic Dipoles
### Torque on a Rectangular Loop

We use a rectangular loop of sides $a$ and $b$ carrying current $I$ to represent a simplified model for atom for mathematical convenience.

The torque $\mathbf{N}$ on , tilted at an angle $\theta$ in a uniform field $\mathbf{B} = B \hat{\mathbf{z}}$, is derived as:
$$\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{N} &= {a F \sin \theta}\hat{\mathbf{x}} && \text{\scriptsize lever arm $\times$ force} \\
&= a ({I b B}) \sin \theta \hat{\mathbf{x}} &&  \scriptsize F = IbB \text{ on vertical segments} \\
&= \underbrace{I a b}_{m} B \sin \theta \hat{\mathbf{x}} &&  \\
&= m B \sin \theta \hat{\mathbf{x}} && \text{\scriptsize applying the magnitude of the dipole moment} \\
&= \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B} && \text{\scriptsize vector cross product form (Eq. 6.1)}
\end{aligned}$$

> [!definition] Torque on a Dipole
> A magnetic dipole in a uniform magnetic field experiences a torque that tries to line it up **parallel** to the field:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{N} = \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B}}$$
> This is mathematically identical to the electrical analog: $\mathbf{N} = \mathbf{p} \times \mathbf{E}$.

### Qualitative Origin of Paramagnetism

The torque $\mathbf{N}=\mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B}$ is what accounts for paramagnetism:

- **The Mechanism**: The torque acts in such a direction as to line the dipole up parallel to the field.
- **The Constraint**: While every electron is a magnetic dipole (a "tiny spinning charged sphere"), the Pauli exclusion principle usually locks electrons into pairs with opposing spins, neutralizing the torque.
- **The Result**: Paramagnetism occurs primarily in atoms with an odd number of electrons, where an "extra" unpaired member is subject to the magnetic torque.

### 3. Force on a Magnetic Dipole

How a dipole moves linearly depends entirely on whether the external magnetic field is uniform or not.

#### A. In a Uniform Field
If the external field $\mathbf{B}$ is constant everywhere, the net force on any closed current loop is exactly zero.

**Proof:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{F} &= I \oint (d\mathbf{l} \times \mathbf{B}) && \text{\scriptsize integrating the magnetic force around the loop} \\
&= I \left( \oint d\mathbf{l} \right) \times \mathbf{B} && \text{\scriptsize pulling the constant } \mathbf{B} \text{ outside the integral} \\
&= \mathbf{0} && \text{\scriptsize the net displacement of any closed loop is zero}
\end{aligned}
$$

#### B. In a Nonuniform Field
If the external magnetic field is changing (like the fringing field at the end of a solenoid), the field lines have a radial component. This radial component exerts a net force on the loop, pulling it toward regions of stronger field.



**Example: The Fringing Field**
Suppose a circular wire ring of radius $R$, carrying a current $I$, is suspended above a short solenoid. Because $\mathbf{B}$ flares outward, there is a net downward force on the loop:
$$
\begin{aligned}
F &= \underbrace{I (2\pi R)}_{I \times \text{circumference}} B \cos \theta && \text{\scriptsize Net downward force (Eq. 6.2)}
\end{aligned}
$$

**The General Formula**
We can generalize this for any infinitesimal loop with dipole moment $\mathbf{m}$ in a non-uniform field $\mathbf{B}$. The exact force is the gradient of the dot product:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{F} &= \nabla (\underbrace{\mathbf{m} \cdot \mathbf{B}}_{-U}) && \text{\scriptsize Force as the negative gradient of interaction energy}
\end{aligned}
$$
> [!remark] 
> Just as in electrostatics where $\mathbf{F} = \nabla(\mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{E})$, the magnetic formula tells us that the dipole seeks out the region of lowest potential energy (where $\mathbf{m}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are parallel and $\mathbf{B}$ is strongest).


For an infinitesimal loop with dipole moment $\mathbf{m}$, the exact force is:
> [!definition] Force on a Dipole
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{F} = \nabla(\mathbf{m} \cdot \mathbf{B})}$$
> Again, this perfectly mirrors the electrical formula: $\mathbf{F} = \nabla(\mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{E})$.

### 4. The "Two Models" Warning: Gilbert vs. Ampère

Because the formulas for magnetic dipoles ($\mathbf{m}$) so perfectly match those for electric dipoles ($\mathbf{p}$), early physicists developed a model of magnetism that assumed it worked exactly like electricity.


| Model | The Physical Picture | Griffiths' Verdict |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **The Gilbert Model** | Magnetic dipoles are made of positive and negative "magnetic charges" (North and South poles) separated by a distance. | **Bad Physics, but Useful Math.** It yields the correct field far away and the correct torque, but fails completely up close because monopoles don't exist. |
| **The Ampère Model** | Magnetism is strictly due to moving electric charges (current loops). | **Physically Correct.** If you break a magnet, you just get two smaller current loops, not isolated poles. |

**Griffiths' Advice:** You can use the Gilbert model to get a quick intuitive "feel" for a problem by swapping $\mathbf{p} \to \mathbf{m}$, $1/\epsilon_0 \to \mu_0$, and $\mathbf{E} \to \mathbf{B}$. But **never rely on it for quantitative results** when dealing with the close-up features of a dipole, because the two models yield strikingly different answers at the origin.

## 6.1.3 Effect of a Magnetic Field on Atomic Orbits

While electrons have intrinsic **spin**, they also **revolve** around the nucleus. This orbital motion creates a second contribution to the magnetic properties of matter, known as **diamagnetism**.

### 1. The Orbital Dipole Moment
For an electron revolving in a circle of radius $R$ with speed $v$, the period is $T = 2\pi R / v$. This constitutes a steady current $I = -e/T$.

> [!definition] Orbital Dipole Moment
> The magnetic dipole moment associated with this orbit is:
> $$\mathbf{m} = I\mathbf{a} = \left( -\frac{ev}{2\pi R} \right) \pi R^2 \hat{\mathbf{z}} = \boxed{-\frac{1}{2}evR\hat{\mathbf{z}}}$$


### 2. The Mechanism of Diamagnetism
When an external magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ is turned on, it exerts an additional force $-e(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})$ on the electron. This force doesn't change the radius of the orbit (in this simplified classical model) but it **changes the speed** of the electron.

**Derivation of the change in speed ($\Delta v$):**
Suppose $\mathbf{B}$ is perpendicular to the plane of the orbit. The centripetal acceleration must now be sustained by both electrical and magnetic forces:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{e^2}{R^2} + e\bar{v}B &= m_e \frac{\bar{v}^2}{R} && \text{\scriptsize new force balance with magnetic field on} \\
\underbrace{m_e \frac{v^2}{R}}_{F_{elec}} + e\bar{v}B &= m_e \frac{\bar{v}^2}{R} && \text{\scriptsize substituting the original orbital condition} \\
e\bar{v}B &= \frac{m_e}{R}(\bar{v}^2 - v^2) && \text{\scriptsize rearranging to isolate the change} \\
e\bar{v}B &= \frac{m_e}{R}(\bar{v} + v)(\bar{v} - v) && \text{\scriptsize factoring the difference of squares} 
\end{aligned}
$$

Assuming the change $\Delta v = \bar{v} - v$ is small, we let $\bar{v} \approx v$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
e v B &\approx \frac{m_e}{R}(2v)\Delta v && \text{\scriptsize applying the small-change approximation} \\
\Delta v &= \frac{eRB}{2m_e} && \text{\scriptsize solving for the speed increment (Eq. 6.7)}
\end{aligned}
$$

### 3. The Induced Dipole Moment
The change in speed leads directly to a change in the dipole moment:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta \mathbf{m} &= -\frac{1}{2}e(\Delta v)R\hat{\mathbf{z}} && \text{\scriptsize from the definition of orbital moment} \\
&= -\frac{1}{2}e\left( \frac{eRB}{2m_e} \right)R\hat{\mathbf{z}} && \text{\scriptsize substituting } \Delta v \\
&= \boxed{-\frac{e^2 R^2}{4m_e} \mathbf{B}} && \text{\scriptsize (Eq. 6.8)}
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!important] The Core Principle of Diamagnetism
> The change in dipole moment $\Delta \mathbf{m}$ is always **opposite** to the direction of $\mathbf{B}$. This induced effect is universal to all atoms, but it is typically much weaker than paramagnetism and is only clearly observed in atoms with even numbers of electrons where paramagnetism is absent.

## 6.1.4 Magnetization

Now that we have analyzed the two microscopic mechanisms (spin alignment for paramagnetism and orbital speed changes for diamagnetism), we need a macroscopic variable to describe the state of magnetic polarization in a chunk of matter.

> [!definition] Magnetization ($\mathbf{M}$)
> Magnetization is defined as the magnetic dipole moment per unit volume:
> $$\boxed{\mathbf{M} \equiv \frac{d\mathbf{m}}{d\tau}}$$
> * **Units**: Amperes per meter (A/m).
> * **Role**: It is the magnetic analog to the polarization $\mathbf{P}$ in electrostatics.


### Summary of Magnetic Effects in Matter
| Effect | Physical Mechanism | Alignment | Strength |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Paramagnetism** | Torque on electron spins | Parallel to $\mathbf{B}$ | Weak |
| **Diamagnetism** | Change in orbital speeds | Opposite to $\mathbf{B}$ | Very Weak |
| **Ferromagnetism** | Quantum domain alignment | Parallel (very strong) | Extreme |

**Note on Strength**: In a non-uniform field, paramagnets are attracted into the field, while diamagnets are repelled. However, both forces are "pitifully weak" compared to ferromagnets like iron, which can be $10^4$ to $10^5$ times stronger. This is why we can often ignore magnetization when calculating fields for ordinary copper wires.
