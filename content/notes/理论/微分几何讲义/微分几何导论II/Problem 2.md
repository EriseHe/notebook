## Problem 2

**Statement.** Let $M$ be a smooth $n$-manifold, let $X$ be a vector field on $M$, and let $\varphi \in \Lambda(M)$. Prove Cartan's magic formula
$$
L_X \varphi = i_X d\varphi + d(i_X\varphi).
$$

### Big picture / idea

This identity says that the Lie derivative of a differential form can be split into two simpler operations:

1. first differentiate the form by $d$, then insert $X$ by $i_X$
2. or first insert $X$, then differentiate

and the sum of those two is exactly the infinitesimal change of the form along the flow of $X$.

So the geometric idea is:

- $L_X$ measures how the form changes as we move along the flow generated by $X$
- $i_X$ plugs the vector field $X$ into the form
- $d$ measures infinitesimal variation of the form

Cartan's formula says these three operations are not independent. They are tied together by a very rigid algebraic identity.

The cleanest proof is to define
$$
D := i_X d + d i_X
$$
and show that $D$ and $L_X$ are the **same derivation** on the whole exterior algebra.

---

### Step 1: define the operator

Set
$$
D := i_X d + d i_X.
$$

We will prove that
$$
D\varphi = L_X\varphi
$$
for every differential form $\varphi$.

---

### Step 2: show that $D$ is a derivation of degree $0$

Let $\alpha \in \Lambda^r(M)$ and $\beta \in \Lambda^s(M)$. Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
D(\alpha \wedge \beta)
&= i_X d(\alpha \wedge \beta) + d\bigl(i_X(\alpha \wedge \beta)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use the product rules for $d$ and $i_X$:
$$
d(\alpha \wedge \beta)=d\alpha \wedge \beta + (-1)^r \alpha \wedge d\beta
$$
and
$$
i_X(\alpha \wedge \beta)=i_X\alpha \wedge \beta + (-1)^r \alpha \wedge i_X\beta.
$$

Substitute these in:
$$
\begin{aligned}
D(\alpha \wedge \beta)
&= i_X\bigl(d\alpha \wedge \beta + (-1)^r \alpha \wedge d\beta\bigr)
   + d\bigl(i_X\alpha \wedge \beta + (-1)^r \alpha \wedge i_X\beta\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now expand the first term:
$$
\begin{aligned}
i_X\bigl(d\alpha \wedge \beta + (-1)^r \alpha \wedge d\beta\bigr)
&= i_X(d\alpha \wedge \beta) + (-1)^r i_X(\alpha \wedge d\beta) \\
&= i_X d\alpha \wedge \beta + (-1)^{r+1} d\alpha \wedge i_X\beta \\
&\qquad + (-1)^r i_X\alpha \wedge d\beta + \alpha \wedge i_X d\beta.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now expand the second term:
$$
\begin{aligned}
d\bigl(i_X\alpha \wedge \beta + (-1)^r \alpha \wedge i_X\beta\bigr)
&= d(i_X\alpha \wedge \beta) + (-1)^r d(\alpha \wedge i_X\beta) \\
&= d(i_X\alpha)\wedge \beta + (-1)^{r-1} i_X\alpha \wedge d\beta \\
&\qquad + (-1)^r d\alpha \wedge i_X\beta + \alpha \wedge d(i_X\beta).
\end{aligned}
$$

Add the two expansions:
$$
\begin{aligned}
D(\alpha \wedge \beta)
&= \bigl(i_X d\alpha + d(i_X\alpha)\bigr)\wedge \beta
   + \alpha \wedge \bigl(i_X d\beta + d(i_X\beta)\bigr) \\
&\qquad + \Bigl((-1)^{r+1}+(-1)^r\Bigr)d\alpha \wedge i_X\beta \\
&\qquad + \Bigl((-1)^r+(-1)^{r-1}\Bigr)i_X\alpha \wedge d\beta.
\end{aligned}
$$

But
$$
(-1)^{r+1}+(-1)^r = 0,
\qquad
(-1)^r+(-1)^{r-1}=0,
$$
so the mixed terms cancel. Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
D(\alpha \wedge \beta)
&= \bigl(i_X d\alpha + d(i_X\alpha)\bigr)\wedge \beta
   + \alpha \wedge \bigl(i_X d\beta + d(i_X\beta)\bigr) \\
&= D\alpha \wedge \beta + \alpha \wedge D\beta.
\end{aligned}
$$

So $D$ is a derivation of degree $0$.

---

### Step 3: recall that $L_X$ is also a derivation of degree $0$

For the Lie derivative of forms, we have
$$
L_X(\alpha \wedge \beta)=L_X\alpha \wedge \beta + \alpha \wedge L_X\beta.
$$

So $L_X$ is also a derivation of degree $0$.

This is the key structural point: both $D$ and $L_X$ are derivations on the exterior algebra. So to prove they are equal everywhere, it is enough to check them on generators.

---

### Step 4: check equality on functions

Let $f \in C^\infty(M)=\Lambda^0(M)$. Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
Df
&= i_X(df) + d(i_X f) \\
&= i_X(df) + d(0) \\
&= df(X) \\
&= Xf.
\end{aligned}
$$

But the Lie derivative of a function is just differentiation along $X$, so
$$
L_X f = Xf.
$$

Hence
$$
Df=L_Xf
$$
for all functions $f$.

---

### Step 5: check equality on $1$-forms

Let $\alpha$ be a $1$-form, and let $Y$ be any vector field. We compute $(D\alpha)(Y)$.

By definition,
$$
\begin{aligned}
(D\alpha)(Y)
&= \bigl(i_X d\alpha + d(i_X\alpha)\bigr)(Y) \\
&= (i_X d\alpha)(Y) + d(i_X\alpha)(Y) \\
&= d\alpha(X,Y) + Y\bigl(\alpha(X)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use the formula for $d\alpha$ on two vector fields:
$$
d\alpha(X,Y)=X(\alpha(Y)) - Y(\alpha(X)) - \alpha([X,Y]).
$$

Substitute:
$$
\begin{aligned}
(D\alpha)(Y)
&= X(\alpha(Y)) - Y(\alpha(X)) - \alpha([X,Y]) + Y(\alpha(X)) \\
&= X(\alpha(Y)) - \alpha([X,Y]).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now compute the Lie derivative of $\alpha$ on $Y$:
$$
(L_X\alpha)(Y)=X(\alpha(Y))-\alpha(L_XY).
$$

Since
$$
L_XY=[X,Y],
$$
we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
(L_X\alpha)(Y)
&= X(\alpha(Y))-\alpha([X,Y]).
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore
$$
(D\alpha)(Y)=(L_X\alpha)(Y)
$$
for every vector field $Y$, hence
$$
D\alpha=L_X\alpha
$$
for every $1$-form $\alpha$.

---

### Step 6: conclude for all forms

Every differential form is built from functions and $1$-forms using wedge products and addition. Locally, any $r$-form can be written as
$$
\varphi=\sum_{i_1<\cdots<i_r} \varphi_{i_1\cdots i_r}\, dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_r}.
$$

Since both $D$ and $L_X$ are degree-$0$ derivations, and since they agree on functions and on $1$-forms, they must agree on all differential forms.

Therefore
$$
\boxed{L_X\varphi = i_X d\varphi + d(i_X\varphi).}
$$

---

### Final comment on why this proof works

The point is not brute-force coordinate computation. The real idea is that the exterior algebra is generated by low-degree objects:

- $0$-forms = functions
- $1$-forms

Once two derivations agree on these generators, they agree on everything. So the proof reduces a statement about **all forms of all degrees** to a computation on just:

- functions
- $1$-forms

That is the conceptual core of Cartan's formula.
