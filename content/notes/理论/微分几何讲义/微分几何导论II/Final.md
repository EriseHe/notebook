## Problem 2

**Statement.** Let $M$ be a smooth manifold, let $X$ be a vector field on $M$, and let $\varphi \in \Lambda(M)$. Prove Cartan’s magic formula
$$
L_X \varphi = i_X d\varphi + d(i_X\varphi).
$$

### Idea

Define
$$
D := i_X d + d i_X.
$$
We will prove that $D=L_X$ on all differential forms.

The strategy is:

1. show $D$ is a derivation of degree $0$ on $\Lambda(M)$;
2. show $L_X$ is also a derivation of degree $0$;
3. check that $D$ and $L_X$ agree on functions and on $1$-forms;
4. conclude they agree on all forms, since differential forms are generated by functions and $1$-forms.

Boothby uses exactly the operators $L_X$, $i_X$, and $d$ in this differential-form setting. 

### Step 1: $D=i_Xd+d,i_X$ is a derivation of degree $0$

Let $\alpha \in \Lambda^r(M)$ and $\beta \in \Lambda^s(M)$. Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
D(\alpha \wedge \beta)
&= i_X d(\alpha \wedge \beta)+d\bigl(i_X(\alpha \wedge \beta)\bigr) \
&= i_X\bigl(d\alpha \wedge \beta+(-1)^r \alpha \wedge d\beta\bigr)
+d\bigl((i_X\alpha)\wedge \beta+(-1)^r \alpha \wedge i_X\beta\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now expand the two terms separately.

First,
$$
\begin{aligned}
i_X\bigl(d\alpha \wedge \beta+(-1)^r \alpha \wedge d\beta\bigr)
&= i_X(d\alpha \wedge \beta)+(-1)^r i_X(\alpha \wedge d\beta) \
&= (i_X d\alpha)\wedge \beta + (-1)^{r+1} d\alpha \wedge i_X\beta

* (-1)^r (i_X\alpha)\wedge d\beta + \alpha \wedge i_X d\beta.
  \end{aligned}
  $$

Second,
$$
\begin{aligned}
d\bigl((i_X\alpha)\wedge \beta+(-1)^r \alpha \wedge i_X\beta\bigr)
&= d\bigl((i_X\alpha)\wedge \beta\bigr)+(-1)^r d(\alpha \wedge i_X\beta) \
&= d(i_X\alpha)\wedge \beta + (-1)^{r-1}(i_X\alpha)\wedge d\beta \
&\qquad + (-1)^r d\alpha \wedge i_X\beta + \alpha \wedge d(i_X\beta).
\end{aligned}
$$

Add these:
$$
\begin{aligned}
D(\alpha \wedge \beta)
&= \bigl(i_X d\alpha + d(i_X\alpha)\bigr)\wedge \beta

* \alpha \wedge \bigl(i_X d\beta + d(i_X\beta)\bigr) \
  &\qquad + \Bigl((-1)^{r+1}+(-1)^r\Bigr)d\alpha \wedge i_X\beta
* \Bigl((-1)^r+(-1)^{r-1}\Bigr)(i_X\alpha)\wedge d\beta \
  &= D\alpha \wedge \beta + \alpha \wedge D\beta.
  \end{aligned}
  $$

So $D$ is a derivation of degree $0$.

### Step 2: $L_X$ is also a derivation of degree $0$

For differential forms, the Lie derivative satisfies
$$
L_X(\alpha \wedge \beta)=L_X\alpha \wedge \beta+\alpha \wedge L_X\beta.
$$
So $L_X$ is also a degree-$0$ derivation. This is one of the standard properties Boothby records for $L_X$. 

### Step 3: check equality on functions

Let $f \in C^\infty(M)=\Lambda^0(M)$. Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
Df
&= i_X(df)+d(i_X f) \
&= i_X(df)+d(0) \
&= df(X) \
&= Xf \
&= L_X f.
\end{aligned}
$$

So $D=L_X$ on $0$-forms.

### Step 4: check equality on $1$-forms

Let $\alpha$ be a $1$-form. We evaluate both sides on an arbitrary vector field $Y$.

First,
$$
\begin{aligned}
(D\alpha)(Y)
&= \bigl(i_X d\alpha + d(i_X\alpha)\bigr)(Y) \
&= d\alpha(X,Y)+Y\bigl(\alpha(X)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use the definition of $d\alpha$ on two vector fields:
$$
d\alpha(X,Y)=X(\alpha(Y))-Y(\alpha(X))-\alpha([X,Y]).
$$
Hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
(D\alpha)(Y)
&= X(\alpha(Y))-Y(\alpha(X))-\alpha([X,Y])+Y(\alpha(X)) \
&= X(\alpha(Y))-\alpha([X,Y]).
\end{aligned}
$$

On the other hand, the Lie derivative of a $1$-form satisfies
$$
(L_X\alpha)(Y)=X(\alpha(Y))-\alpha([X,Y]).
$$
Therefore
$$
(D\alpha)(Y)=(L_X\alpha)(Y)
\qquad \text{for every vector field }Y.
$$
So
$$
D\alpha=L_X\alpha
$$
for every $1$-form $\alpha$.

### Step 5: conclude for all forms

Every differential form is locally a finite sum of terms of the form
$$
f, dx^{i_1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_r},
$$
so the exterior algebra is generated by functions and $1$-forms. Since both $D$ and $L_X$ are degree-$0$ derivations and they agree on functions and $1$-forms, they agree on all of $\Lambda(M)$.

Therefore,
$$
\boxed{L_X\varphi=i_Xd\varphi+d(i_X\varphi)}
$$
for every $\varphi \in \Lambda(M)$.

## Problem 3

**Statement.** Let $M$ be a manifold with smooth boundary $\partial M$. Let $g$ be a Riemannian metric smooth on $M$ such that $\partial M$ is strictly convex. Prove that for $(x,v)\in \partial_+SM\setminus \partial_0SM$, the geodesic $\gamma_{x,v}(t)$ stays in $M$ for sufficiently small $t>0$.

### Idea

Near a boundary point, Boothby models $M$ on the half-space
$$
H^n={(x^1,\dots,x^n)\in \mathbb{R}^n : x^n\ge 0},
$$
with the boundary corresponding to $x^n=0$. So the last coordinate plays the role of a boundary defining function. If the initial velocity points strictly inward and is not tangent to the boundary, then the last coordinate has positive derivative at $t=0$. That alone forces the geodesic to move into the interior for small positive time. The final problem statement is exactly this claim.  

### Step 1: choose a boundary chart

Since $M$ is a smooth manifold with boundary, there exists a coordinate neighborhood $(U,\varphi)$ of $x$ such that
$$
\varphi(U)\subset H^n,
\qquad
\varphi(x)=0,
$$
and
$$
\varphi(U\cap \partial M)=\varphi(U)\cap {x^n=0}.
$$
Thus, in these local coordinates, points of $M$ correspond to points with $x^n\ge 0$, boundary points correspond to $x^n=0$, and interior points correspond to $x^n>0$. This is exactly Boothby’s local model for a manifold with boundary. 

Define the local boundary defining function
$$
\rho := x^n \circ \varphi.
$$
Then, on $U$,
$$
U\cap M = {\rho \ge 0},
\qquad
U\cap \partial M = {\rho = 0},
$$
and $d\rho \neq 0$ on $\partial M$.

### Step 2: look at the boundary defining function along the geodesic

Let
$$
\gamma(t):=\gamma_{x,v}(t)
$$
be the geodesic with
$$
\gamma(0)=x,
\qquad
\dot\gamma(0)=v.
$$
Since geodesics are smooth, the function
$$
h(t):=\rho(\gamma(t))
$$
is smooth for $|t|$ small.

At $t=0$ we have
$$
h(0)=\rho(x)=0.
$$

Differentiate:
$$
\begin{aligned}
h'(0)
&= \frac{d}{dt}\Big|_{t=0}\rho(\gamma(t)) \
&= d\rho_x(\dot\gamma(0)) \
&= d\rho_x(v).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now $(x,v)\in \partial_+SM\setminus \partial_0SM$ means that $x\in \partial M$, the vector $v$ is not tangent to $\partial M$, and it points strictly inward. In the coordinates above, this is exactly the statement
$$
d\rho_x(v)>0.
$$
Equivalently,
$$
h'(0)>0.
$$

### Step 3: conclude positivity for small positive time

Since $h'$ is continuous and $h'(0)>0$, there exists $\varepsilon>0$ such that
$$
h'(t)>\frac{1}{2}h'(0)>0
\qquad \text{for }0\le t\le \varepsilon.
$$

Therefore, for every $t\in (0,\varepsilon]$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
h(t)-h(0)
&=\int_0^t h'(s),ds \
&> \int_0^t \frac{1}{2}h'(0),ds \
&= \frac{t}{2}h'(0) \
&> 0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $h(0)=0$, this gives
$$
h(t)>0
\qquad \text{for }0<t\le \varepsilon.
$$
That is,
$$
\rho(\gamma(t))>0
\qquad \text{for }0<t\le \varepsilon.
$$

But $\rho>0$ means the point lies in the interior of $M$. Hence
$$
\gamma_{x,v}(t)\in \operatorname{Int}(M)\subset M
\qquad \text{for all sufficiently small }t>0.
$$

Therefore,
$$
\boxed{\gamma_{x,v}(t)\text{ stays in }M\text{ for sufficiently small }t>0.}
$$

### Remark on strict convexity

For this particular statement, the strict convexity hypothesis is actually stronger than necessary. Since $(x,v)\notin \partial_0SM$, the first derivative $h'(0)$ already tells us that the geodesic immediately enters the interior.

Strict convexity becomes important in the **tangent** case $(x,v)\in \partial_0SM$, where one studies the second derivative of $h(t)$ at $t=0$ instead of the first.
