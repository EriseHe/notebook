# 1 Smooth functions on a manifold

Let $M$ be a smooth manifold. A map $f:M\to\mathbb R$ is **smooth** if for any coordinate map
$$
\sigma:U\subset\mathbb R^{n}\to M
$$
the composition
$$
f\circ\sigma:U\to\mathbb R
$$
is a $C^\infty$ function. We write
$$
C^\infty(M)=\{\text{smooth functions }M\to\mathbb R\}
$$
(Smoothness for maps between manifolds is defined the same way, in local coordinates.)


# 2 Tangent vectors

**Definition.**  
A tangent vector $X\in T_pM$ is a directional derivative
$$
X:C^\infty(M)\to\mathbb R
$$
such that
$$
X(fg)=f(p)\,Xg+g(p)\,Xf
$$
for all $f,g\in C^\infty(M)$. As in the surface case, $T_pM$ is an $n$–dimensional vector space. In local coordinates $(x^1,\dots,x^n)$, the coordinate basis for $T_pM$ is given by
$$
\partial_i:=\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i},
\qquad i=1,\dots,n
$$
For a curve $\gamma:I\to M$, the velocity is the tangent vector
$$
\dot\gamma:f\longmapsto\frac{d}{dt}\big(f\circ\gamma\big)
$$
at the point $\gamma(t)$.


## 2.1 Tangent bundle and vector bundles

The space
$$
TM=\bigcup_{p\in M}T_pM
$$
has the structure of a manifold. Given a coordinate patch
$$
\sigma:U\to M
$$
we define
$$
\widetilde\sigma:U\times\mathbb R^n\longrightarrow TM
$$
by
$$
\widetilde\sigma(x,v)=v^j\,\partial_j\big|_{\sigma(x)}\in T_{\sigma(x)}M
$$

$TM$ is called the **tangent bundle** of $M$.

## 2.2 Vector bundles

**Definition.**  
A vector bundle $E$ of rank $k$ over $M$ is a manifold $E$ equipped with a smooth map
$$
\pi:E\to M
$$
such that

1. The fiber
   $$
   E_p:=\pi^{-1}(p)
   $$
   is a $k$–dimensional vector space for each $p\in M$

2. For each $p\in M$, there is a neighborhood $V\ni p$ and a diffeomorphism
   $$
   \psi:\pi^{-1}(V)\longrightarrow V\times\mathbb R^k
   $$

A **section** of $E$ is a smooth map
$$
f:M\to E
$$
such that $f(p)\in E_p$ for each $p$ (i.e. $\pi\circ f=\operatorname{id}_M$).

A section of $TM$ is called a **vector field**.


# 3 Cotangent vectors and the cotangent bundle

For a finite–dimensional vector space $V$, the dual space $V^*$ is the set of linear maps
$$
V\to\mathbb R
$$

The **cotangent space**
$$
T_p^*M=(T_pM)^*
$$
is the dual space of $T_pM$. Given coordinates $x^i$ near $p$, we have the coordinate basis $\partial_j$ for $T_pM$. The **dual basis** $dx^i$ is defined by
$$
dx^i(\partial_j)=\delta^i{}_j
$$
This allows us to define coordinate patches for
$$
T^*M=\bigcup_{p\in M}T_p^*M
$$
the **cotangent bundle** of $M$.


# 4 Differential of a smooth function

For $f\in C^\infty(M)$, the **differential** of $f$ is the section $df\in\Gamma(T^*M)$, defined by
$$
df(X)=Xf
$$
for $X\in T_pM$. 

>This is consistent with the cotangent basis notation
>$$
dx^i(\partial_k)
:=\frac{\partial}{\partial x^k}(x^i)
=\delta^i{}_k
>$$

# 5 Riemannian metrics

A **metric** on $M$ is a smooth family of inner products
$$
g(\,\cdot\,,\,\cdot\,):T_pM\times T_pM\to\mathbb R
$$
In local coordinates, this means that
$$
g_{ij}(x):=g(\partial_i,\partial_j)
$$
are smooth functions of $(x^1,\dots,x^n)$ and the matrix $(g_{ij})$ is symmetric and positive definite at each point. The metric defines the length of curves $\gamma:I\to M$ by
$$
\mathcal{l}(\gamma)=\int_I\lvert\dot\gamma\rvert\,dt
$$
where $\lvert\dot\gamma\rvert^2=g(\dot\gamma,\dot\gamma)$.

# 6 Metric as an isomorphism $TM\leftrightarrow T^*M$

The metric defines a map
$$
T_pM\longrightarrow T_p^*M,
\qquad
X\longmapsto g(X,\cdot)
$$

In coordinates,
$$
\partial_i\longmapsto g(\partial_i,\cdot)=g_{ij}\,dx^j
$$

Similarly, we can use the inverse matrix
$$
[g^{ij}]=[g_{ij}]^{-1}
$$
to define a map
$$
g^{-1}:T_p^*M\longrightarrow T_pM
$$

# 7 Gradient vector field

**Example.**  
The gradient vector field of $f$ is defined by
$$
\nabla f:=g^{-1}(df)
$$
which means
$$
g(\nabla f,\cdot)=df(\cdot)
$$

In coordinates,
$$
\begin{aligned}
df &= (\partial_j f)\,dx^j \\[4pt]
\nabla f &= g^{ij}\,(\partial_j f)\,\partial_i
\end{aligned}
$$

# 8 Tensors

In linear algebra, the tensor product of vector spaces $V,W$ is a vector space
$$
V\otimes W
$$
consisting of elements $v\otimes w$ with the identifications
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda(v\otimes w) &= (\lambda v)\otimes w
                  = v\otimes(\lambda w) \quad &&\text{for }\lambda\in\mathbb R \\[4pt]
(v_1+v_2)\otimes w &= v_1\otimes w+v_2\otimes w \\[2pt]
v\otimes(w_1+w_2) &= v\otimes w_1+v\otimes w_2
\end{aligned}
$$

Note that
$$
\dim(V\otimes W)=\dim V\cdot\dim W
$$

**Example**
$$
V^*\otimes V^*=\{\text{bilinear forms }V\times V\to\mathbb R\}
$$

Given vector bundles $E,F$ over $M$, we can take the tensor products of fibers to define
$$
E\otimes F:=\bigcup_{p\in M}\big(E_p\otimes F_p\big)
$$

**Definition.**  
A tensor on $M$ is a section of the bundle
$$
T^k_{\ \ell}M:=\underbrace{T^*M\otimes\cdots\otimes T^*M}_{k\text{ copies}}
\otimes
\underbrace{TM\otimes\cdots\otimes TM}_{\ell\text{ copies}}
$$

**Example**:
$$
g\ \text{is a symmetric }(2,0)\text{ tensor}
$$
