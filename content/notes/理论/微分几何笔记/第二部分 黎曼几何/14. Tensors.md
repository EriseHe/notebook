# 1 Tensors

In linear algebra, the **tensor product** of vector spaces $V,W$ is a vector space $V\otimes W$ consisting of elements $v\otimes w$ with identification rules
$$
\begin{aligned}
(\lambda v)\otimes w &= \lambda(v\otimes w)\\&=v\otimes(\lambda w) \quad &&\text{for }\lambda\in\mathbb R\\[4pt]
(v_1+v_2)\otimes w &= v_1\otimes w+v_2\otimes w\\[4pt]
v\otimes(w_1+w_2) &= v\otimes w_1+v\otimes w_2
\end{aligned}
$$
Note that
$$
\dim(V\otimes W)=\dim V\cdot\dim W
$$

Example
$$
V^*\otimes V^*=\{\text{bilinear forms }V\times V\to\mathbb R\}
$$

Given vector bundles $E,F$ over $M$, we can take the tensor products of fibers to define
$$
E\otimes F:=\bigcup_{p\in M}(E_p\otimes F_p)
$$

**Definition.** A **tensor on $M$** is a section of the bundle
$$
T^k_{\ \ell}M:=\underbrace{T^*M\otimes\cdots\otimes T^*M}_{k\ \text{copies}}
\otimes
\underbrace{TM\otimes\cdots\otimes TM}_{\ell\ \text{copies}}
$$

Example: the metric $g$ is a symmetric $(2,0)$–tensor.

We can convert types using the maps $g$ and $g^{-1}$ between $TM$ and $T^*M$. With respect to the coordinate basis, this works by raising and lowering indices with $g_{ij}$ or $g^{ij}$. For instance
$$
a^i\partial_i
\;\longmapsto\;
g_{ij}a^i\,dx^j
$$
takes a tangent vector to a cotangent vector.

## 1.1 Volume

From calculus, changing variables $x^1,\dots,x^n$ to $u^1,\dots,u^n$ with Jacobian matrix
$$
J=\left[\frac{\partial u^i}{\partial x^j}\right]
$$
gives
$$
dx^1\cdots dx^n
=
|\det J|\,du^1\cdots du^n
$$

Let
$$
g_{ij}=g(\partial_{x^i},\partial_{x^j}),
\qquad
\tilde g_{ij}=g(\partial_{u^i},\partial_{u^j})
$$

As a tensor
$$
\begin{aligned}
g
&=g_{ij}\,dx^i\otimes dx^j
=\tilde g_{ij}\,du^i\otimes du^j
\end{aligned}
$$
Since $du^i=\frac{\partial u^i}{\partial x^k}\,dx^k$, we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_{ij}
&=\tilde g_{kl}\,
\frac{\partial u^k}{\partial x^i}\,
\frac{\partial u^l}{\partial x^j}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Taking determinants,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\det[g_{ij}]
&=\det[\tilde g_{ij}]\,(\det J)^2
\end{aligned}
$$
and hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sqrt{\det[g_{ij}]}\,dx^1\cdots dx^n
&=
\underbrace{\sqrt{\det[\tilde g_{ij}]}\,|\det J|\,du^1\cdots du^n}_{\text{change of variables}}
=
\sqrt{\det[\tilde g_{ij}]}\,du^1\cdots du^n.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the **volume density**
$$
dV=\sqrt{\det g}\,dx^1\cdots dx^n
$$
is independent of coordinates. Here $g$ denotes the tensor (invariant of coordinates) while $\det g$ means $\det[g_{ij}]$ in the coordinates $x^1,\dots,x^n$. (To make this more confusing, $dV$ is often written as $dg$.)


## 1.2 Connections

Consider a vector field $Y$ on a surface $S\subset\mathbb R^3$. We view $Y$ as a map $Y:S\to TS$, but also as $Y:S\to\mathbb R^3$. Given $X\in T_pS$, choose a curve $\gamma$ in $S$ with $\gamma(0)=p$ and $\dot\gamma(0)=X$. Then
$$
Y\circ\gamma:I\to\mathbb R^3
$$
is a map which can be differentiated, so
$$
\left.\frac{d}{dt}Y(\gamma(t))\right|_{t=0}
$$
depends only on $X$, but is not necessarily tangent to $S$. Hence the “derivative” $XY$ does not make sense as a tangent vector.

To correct this, define
$$
\nabla_XY:=\text{orthogonal projection of }
\left.\frac{d}{dt}Y(\gamma(t))\right|_{t=0}
\text{ into }T_pS.
$$
If we write
$$
\mathcal T:=\{\text{sections of }TS\},
$$
then $\nabla$ defines a map
$$
\nabla:\mathcal T\times\mathcal T\to\mathcal T.
$$

This idea can be generalized to differentiating sections of a vector bundle. Let $E$ be a vector bundle over a manifold $M$, and set
$$
\mathcal E:=\{\text{smooth sections of }E\},
\qquad
\mathcal T:=\{\text{vector fields on }M\}.
$$

**Definition.** A **connection** on $E$ is a map
$$
\nabla:\mathcal T\times\mathcal E\to\mathcal E,
\qquad
(X,\phi)\longmapsto\nabla_X\phi
$$
(“derivative of $\phi$ in direction $X$”) satisfying:

1. $\nabla_{(\cdot)}\phi$ is $C^\infty(M)$–linear:
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   \nabla_{X_1+X_2}\phi &= \nabla_{X_1}\phi+\nabla_{X_2}\phi,\\[2pt]
   \nabla_{fX}\phi &= f\,\nabla_X\phi\qquad f\in C^\infty(M).
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. $\nabla_X(\cdot)$ is $\mathbb R$–linear.  
3. $\nabla_X(\cdot)$ is a derivation, meaning for $f\in C^\infty(M)$
   $$
   \nabla_X(f\phi)=(Xf)\,\phi+f\,\nabla_X\phi.
   $$

One checks that our definition for $S\subset\mathbb R^3$ by projection qualifies as a connection on $TS$.

## 1.3 Lie bracket of vector fields

Let $X,Y\in\mathcal T$ and $f\in C^\infty(M)$. Then $Yf\in C^\infty(M)$, so $X(Yf)$ is defined. However, $XY$ takes two derivatives and is not a vector field. In the **Lie bracket**
$$
[X,Y]:=XY-YX
$$
the second derivatives cancel.

In coordinates, let
$$
X=a^j\partial_j,\qquad Y=b^k\partial_k.
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
X(Yf)
&=a^j\partial_j(b^k\partial_k f)
=
\underbrace{a^j(\partial_j b^k)\partial_k f}_{\text{first derivatives}}
+
\underbrace{a^j b^k\partial_j\partial_k f}_{\text{second derivatives}},\\[4pt]
Y(Xf)
&=b^k\partial_k(a^j\partial_j f)
=
b^k(\partial_k a^j)\partial_j f
+
b^k a^j\partial_k\partial_j f.
\end{aligned}
$$
Since $\partial_j\partial_k f=\partial_k\partial_j f$, the second derivative terms cancel, so
$$
\begin{aligned}
[X,Y]f
&=(XY-YX)f \\[4pt]
&=\big(a^j\partial_j b^k - b^j\partial_j a^k\big)\partial_k f,
\end{aligned}
$$
and hence
$$
\boxed{
[X,Y]
=
\big(a^j\partial_j b^k - b^j\partial_j a^k\big)\partial_k.
}
$$

## 1.4 Riemannian connection (Levi–Civita connection)

**Theorem.** On a Riemannian manifold $M$ there is a unique connection $\nabla$ on $TM$ such that

1. **Symmetry**
   $$
   \nabla_XY-\nabla_YX=[X,Y].
   $$
2. **Compatibility with $g$**
   $$
   X\,g(Y,Z)=g(\nabla_XY,Z)+g(Y,\nabla_XZ).
   $$

This is called the **Riemannian connection**.
