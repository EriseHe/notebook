## 1. Riemannian Connection on a Riemannian Manifold

Let $(M,g)$ be a Riemannian manifold.

> [!theorem|1.1] Existence and uniqueness of the Riemannian connection  
> On a Riemannian manifold $M$, there is a unique connection $\nabla$ on $TM$ such that  
> 1. **Symmetry**
>    $$
>    \nabla_X Y - \nabla_Y X = [X,Y]
>    $$
> 2. **Compatibility with $g$**
>    $$
>    X\,g(Y,Z) = g(\nabla_X Y,Z) + g(Y,\nabla_X Z)
>    $$
> This connection is called the **Riemannian connection** (or Levi-Civita connection).

### 1.1 Sketch of the proof

Use (1) and (2) to combine the three expressions
$$
X\,g(Y,Z),\quad Y\,g(X,Z),\quad Z\,g(X,Y)
$$
into a symmetric combination:
$$
X\,g(Y,Z) + Y\,g(X,Z) - Z\,g(X,Y)
$$
Using symmetry and compatibility one can rewrite this as
$$
\begin{aligned}
X\,g(Y,Z) + Y\,g(X,Z) - Z\,g(X,Y)
&= 2\,g(\nabla_X Y,Z)
  + \text{terms involving only }[\,\cdot,\cdot\,]
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence we can solve for $g(\nabla_X Y,Z)$ purely in terms of $g$ and the Lie bracket $[\,\cdot,\cdot\,]$.  
This determines $\nabla_X Y$ uniquely, and one checks that the resulting $\nabla$ satisfies (1) and (2).

## 2. Riemannian Connection in Local Coordinates

Let $(x^1,\dots,x^n)$ be local coordinates, and write $\partial_i := \dfrac{\partial}{\partial x^i}$.

### 2.1 Christoffel symbols

> [!definition|2.1] Christoffel symbols  
> The **Christoffel symbols** $\Gamma_{ij}^k$ are the coefficients defined by
> $$
> \nabla_{\partial_i}\partial_j = \Gamma_{ij}^k\,\partial_k
> $$
> (Here the $\Gamma_{ij}^k$ depend on the chosen coordinates and do **not** form a tensor.)

Let $g_{jh} = g(\partial_j,\partial_h)$. By compatibility,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\partial_i g_{jh}
&= \partial_i g(\partial_j,\partial_h) \\[4pt]
&= g(\nabla_{\partial_i}\partial_j,\partial_h)
   + g(\partial_j,\nabla_{\partial_i}\partial_h) \\[4pt]
&= \Gamma_{ij}^k g_{kh} + \Gamma_{ih}^k g_{jk}
\end{aligned}
$$

Now write down the three versions obtained by cyclic permutation of $(i,j,h)$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\partial_i g_{jh} &= \Gamma_{ij}^k g_{kh} + \Gamma_{ih}^k g_{jk} \\[4pt]
\partial_j g_{ih} &= \Gamma_{ji}^k g_{kh} + \Gamma_{jh}^k g_{ik} \\[4pt]
\partial_h g_{ij} &= \Gamma_{hi}^k g_{kj} + \Gamma_{hj}^k g_{ik}
\end{aligned}
$$

Using symmetry $\Gamma_{ij}^k = \Gamma_{ji}^k$ and combining these three equations, one solves for $\Gamma_{ij}^k$. In matrix form this gives
$$
\boxed{\Gamma_{ij}^k
= \tfrac12\,g^{k\ell}
   \Big(
      \partial_i g_{j\ell}
      + \partial_j g_{i\ell}
      - \partial_\ell g_{ij}
   \Big)}
$$
where $(g^{k\ell})$ is the inverse matrix of $(g_{k\ell})$. This is the standard coordinate formula for the Riemannian connection.

## 3. Example: The Cone in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Consider the cone
$$
\Sigma = \{(x,y,z)\in\mathbb{R}^3 \mid z = a r\}
$$
parametrized by
$$
\sigma(z,\theta) = (a z\cos\theta,\; a z\sin\theta,\; z)
$$

We use coordinates $(z,\theta)$ on $\Sigma$. Write
$$
\partial_z := \frac{\partial}{\partial z},\qquad
\partial_\theta := \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}
$$

We will compute $\nabla_{\partial_\theta}\partial_\theta$ in two ways.

### 3.1 Geometric computation

First derivatives:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma_z &= \partial_z\sigma = (a\cos\theta,\; a\sin\theta,\; 1) \\[4pt]
\sigma_\theta &= \partial_\theta\sigma
= (-a z\sin\theta,\; a z\cos\theta,\; 0)
\end{aligned}
$$

Second derivative in the $\theta$–direction:
$$
\sigma_{\theta\theta}
= \partial_\theta\sigma_\theta
= (-a z\cos\theta,\; -a z\sin\theta,\; 0)
$$

This vector $\sigma_{\theta\theta}$ is not tangent to the cone (it points “towards” the $z$–axis).  
Let
$$
e_1 := \frac{\sigma_z}{\|\sigma_z\|}
= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+a^2}}\,(a\cos\theta,\; a\sin\theta,\; 1)
$$
be a unit tangent vector along the $z$–direction.

The Riemannian connection on $\Sigma$ is the orthogonal projection of the ambient Euclidean derivative onto $T\Sigma$, so
$$
\nabla_{\partial_\theta}\partial_\theta
= \text{projection of }\sigma_{\theta\theta}\text{ onto }T\Sigma
= \langle\sigma_{\theta\theta},e_1\rangle\,e_1
$$

A short computation gives
$$
\langle\sigma_{\theta\theta},e_1\rangle
= -\frac{a^2 z}{1+a^2}
$$
so
$$
\nabla_{\partial_\theta}\partial_\theta
= -\frac{a^2 z}{1+a^2}\,e_1
$$

### 3.2 Christoffel symbols on the cone

First compute the induced metric:
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_{zz} &= \langle\sigma_z,\sigma_z\rangle = 1+a^2 \\[4pt]
g_{z\theta} &= \langle\sigma_z,\sigma_\theta\rangle = 0 \\[4pt]
g_{\theta\theta} &= \langle\sigma_\theta,\sigma_\theta\rangle = a^2 z^2
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, in the coordinates $(z,\theta)$,
$$
[g_{ij}] =
\begin{pmatrix}
1+a^2 & 0 \\
0 & a^2 z^2
\end{pmatrix}
$$

We write $\partial_1 = \partial_z$, $\partial_2 = \partial_\theta$.  
Then
$$
\nabla_{\partial_\theta}\partial_\theta
= \Gamma_{22}^i\,\partial_i
$$

Using the coordinate formula for the Christoffel symbols,
$$
\Gamma_{22}^1
= \tfrac12\,g^{1j}
\big(2\,\partial_2 g_{2j} - \partial_j g_{22}\big)
$$

Since $g_{22} = a^2 z^2$ and $g_{21}=g_{12}=0$, the only non–zero contribution comes from $j=1$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Gamma_{22}^1
&= \tfrac12\,g^{11}\big(-\partial_1 g_{22}\big) \\[4pt]
&= \tfrac12\,\frac{1}{1+a^2}
     \big(-\partial_z(a^2 z^2)\big) \\[4pt]
&= \tfrac12\,\frac{1}{1+a^2}
     \big(-2 a^2 z\big) \\[4pt]
&= -\frac{a^2 z}{1+a^2}
\end{aligned}
$$

Moreover,
$$
\Gamma_{22}^2 = 0
$$

Therefore,
$$
\nabla_{\partial_\theta}\partial_\theta
= \Gamma_{22}^1\,\partial_z
= -\frac{a^2 z}{1+a^2}\,\partial_z
$$

This agrees with the geometric computation, once we rewrite $e_1$ in terms of $\partial_z$.

> [!theorem|3.1] Submanifold connection  
> If $M\subset\mathbb{R}^N$ and $g$ is the restriction of the Euclidean metric, then the Riemannian connection on $M$ is obtained by **orthogonal projection** of the ambient Euclidean derivative onto the tangent spaces of $M$.

## 4. Covariant Differentiation Along a Curve

### 4.1 Covariant derivative along a curve

Let $\gamma:I\to M$ be a smooth curve and $X$ a vector field **along** $\gamma$, meaning
$$
X(t)\in T_{\gamma(t)}M
$$
and $X(t)$ varies smoothly with $t$.

> [!definition|4.1] Covariant derivative along $\gamma$  
> The **covariant derivative** of $X$ along $\gamma$ is
> $$
> D_t X := \nabla_{\dot\gamma}X(t)\in T_{\gamma(t)}M
> $$

### 4.2 Coordinate expression

In local coordinates, write
$$
\gamma(t) = (x^1(t),\dots,x^n(t)),\qquad
X(t) = a^i(t)\,\partial_i
$$
and
$$
\dot\gamma = \dot x^i\,\partial_i
$$

Compute:
$$
\begin{aligned}
D_t X
&= \nabla_{\dot\gamma}(a^k\partial_k) \\[4pt]
&= \underbrace{\dot a^k\partial_k}_{\text{derivative of coefficients}}
   + a^k\nabla_{\dot\gamma}\partial_k \\[4pt]
&= \dot a^k\partial_k
   + a^k \dot x^j \nabla_{\partial_j}\partial_k \\[4pt]
&= \dot a^k\partial_k
   + a^k \dot x^j \Gamma_{jk}^i \partial_i \\[4pt]
&= \big(\dot a^i + \dot x^j a^k \Gamma_{jk}^i\big)\partial_i
\end{aligned}
$$

So the coordinate form is
$$
D_t X = \big(\dot a^i + \dot x^j a^k \Gamma_{jk}^i\big)\partial_i
$$

### 4.3 Geodesics

> [!definition|4.2] Geodesic  
> A curve $\gamma$ is a **geodesic** if its covariant acceleration vanishes:
> $$
> D_t\dot\gamma = 0
> $$

In coordinates, $\dot\gamma = \dot x^i\partial_i$, so using the formula for $D_t$ we get
$$
D_t\dot\gamma
= \big(\ddot x^i + \dot x^j\dot x^k\Gamma_{jk}^i\big)\partial_i
$$
Thus $\gamma$ is a geodesic iff
$$
\ddot x^i + \dot x^j\dot x^k\Gamma_{jk}^i = 0
\quad\text{for }i=1,\dots,n
$$
This is a linear system of second–order ODEs for the coordinates $x^i(t)$.

> [!theorem|4.3] Existence and uniqueness of geodesics  
> For any point $\gamma(0)\in M$ and initial velocity $\dot\gamma(0)\in T_{\gamma(0)}M$, there exists a unique geodesic $\gamma(t)$ solving the geodesic equation with these initial conditions.

## 5. Parallel Transport

### 5.1 Parallel vector fields

> [!definition|5.1] Parallel vector field along a curve  
> A vector field $X$ along a curve $\gamma$ is **parallel** if
> $$
> D_t X = 0
> $$

In coordinates, if $X = a^i\partial_i$, then
$$
D_t X = 0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\dot a^i + a^k\dot x^j\Gamma_{jk}^i = 0
\quad\text{for }i=1,\dots,n
$$
This is a linear first–order system of ODEs for the coefficient functions $a^i(t)$.

> [!theorem|5.2] Existence and uniqueness of parallel transport  
> Given a curve $\gamma$ and an initial vector $X(0)\in T_{\gamma(0)}M$, there exists a unique parallel vector field $X(t)$ along $\gamma$ with $X(0)$ as initial value.

Geometrically, parallel transport along $\gamma$ gives a map between tangent spaces:
$$
T_{\gamma(t_0)}M \longrightarrow T_{\gamma(t_1)}M
$$
It “connects’’ the tangent spaces along the curve $\gamma$ by sliding vectors so that they remain parallel in the sense $D_t X = 0$.
