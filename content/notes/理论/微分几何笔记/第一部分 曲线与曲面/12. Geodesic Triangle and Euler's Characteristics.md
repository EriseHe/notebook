# 1 Geodesic triangles

Consider a triangle $T$ with all sides geodesic.

We will assume $T$ fits in a geodesic polar patch centered at $A$.

Parametrize the non-radial side by a unit–speed geodesic
$$
\gamma(t) = (r(t),\theta(t))
$$

With
$$
ds^{2} = dr^{2} + \phi^{2}\,d\theta^{2},
$$
the geodesic equations become
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dt}(\dot r) &= \phi\,(\partial_{r}\phi)\,\dot\theta^{2}\\[4pt]
\frac{d}{dt}(\phi^{2}\dot\theta) &= \phi\,(\partial_{\theta}\phi)\,\dot\theta^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

Define an orthonormal frame
$$
e_{1} = \partial_{r},\qquad
e_{2} = \frac{1}{\phi}\,\partial_{\theta}.
$$

Let $\psi$ be the angle from $e_{1}$ to $\dot\gamma$ ($>0$ since $\gamma$ is not radial). Since $\dot\gamma$ is a unit vector,
$$
\dot\gamma = \cos\psi\,e_{1} + \sin\psi\,e_{2}
$$
Compare this to the chain rule
$$
\dot\gamma = \dot r\,\partial_{r} + \dot\theta\,\partial_{\theta},
$$
so
$$
\dot r = \cos\psi\qquad
\dot\theta = \frac{\sin\psi}{\phi}
$$
Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dt}(\cos\psi)
&= \ddot r \\[4pt]
-\sin\psi\,\dot\psi
&=
\underbrace{\phi\,\dot\theta^{2}\,\partial_{r}\phi}_{\text{geodesic eq.}} \\[4pt]
&= \dot\theta\,(\phi\dot\theta)\,\partial_{r}\phi
= \dot\theta\,\sin\psi\,\partial_{r}\phi,
\end{aligned}
$$
so (since $\sin\psi\neq 0$ along the non-radial side)
$$
\dot\psi = -\,\dot\theta\,\partial_{r}\phi.
$$

**Conclusion:** along $\gamma$ we have
$$
\boxed{\displaystyle
\frac{d\psi}{d\theta} = -\,\partial_{r}\phi}
$$


# 2 Gauss–Bonnet Theorem (triangle version)

For a geodesic triangle $T$ within a geodesic coordinate patch,
with interior angles $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ at the vertices,
$$
\boxed{\displaystyle
\int_{T} K\,dA = (\alpha + \beta + \gamma) - \pi.}
$$

## 2.1 Proof (in a geodesic polar patch)

Write $T$ in geodesic polar coordinates $(r,\theta)$ centered at one vertex as
$$
0 \le \theta \le \alpha,\qquad 0 \le r \le r(\theta),
$$
where $r(\theta)$ describes the non-radial side $\gamma$. In these coordinates,
$$
K = -\,\frac{\partial_{r}^{2}\phi}{\phi},\qquad
dA = \phi\,dr\,d\theta
$$
so
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{T} K\,dA
&=
\int_{0}^{\alpha}\!\!\int_{0}^{r(\theta)}
\underbrace{\left(-\frac{\partial_{r}^{2}\phi}{\phi}\right)}_{K}\,
\underbrace{\phi\,dr\,d\theta}_{dA} \\[4pt]
&=
\int_{0}^{\alpha}\!\!\int_{0}^{r(\theta)}
-\partial_{r}^{2}\phi\,dr\,d\theta \\[4pt]
&=
\int_{0}^{\alpha}
\big[-\partial_{r}\phi\big]_{r=0}^{r=r(\theta)}\,d\theta \\[4pt]
&=
\int_{0}^{\alpha}
\big(1 - \partial_{r}\phi(r(\theta),\theta)\big)\,d\theta
\end{aligned}
$$

Along the side $\gamma$ we have
$$
\frac{d\psi}{d\theta} = -\,\partial_{r}\phi
$$
so
$$
1 - \partial_{r}\phi
=
1 + \frac{d\psi}{d\theta}
$$
and therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{T} K\,dA
&=
\int_{0}^{\alpha}\left(1 + \frac{d\psi}{d\theta}\right)\!d\theta \\[4pt]
&=
\alpha + \psi(\alpha) - \psi(0)
\end{aligned}
$$

A little angle–chasing at the two non-center vertices shows
$$
\psi(\alpha) - \psi(0)
=
\beta + \gamma - \pi
$$
so
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{T} K\,dA
&= \alpha + \psi(\alpha) - \psi(0) \\[4pt]
&= \alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi
\end{aligned}
$$


# 3 Global version

Let $S$ be a compact surface without boundary.

By covering $S$ with geodesic patches, we can subdivide $S$ into
geodesic triangles:
$$
S = \bigcup_j T_j
$$
On each triangle,
$$
\int_{T_j} K\,dA = (\alpha_j + \beta_j + \gamma_j) - \pi
$$

For the triangulation $\{T_j\}$ let
$$
v = \#\text{ vertices},\quad
e = \#\text{ edges},\quad
f = \#\text{ faces}.
$$

No boundary implies
$$
3f = 2e
$$
(each of the $f$ triangles has $3$ edges, and each edge is shared by $2$
triangles).

Summing over faces gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{S} K\,dA
&= \sum_{j=1}^{f}(\alpha_j + \beta_j + \gamma_j - \pi) \\[4pt]
&= \underbrace{\sum_{j=1}^{f}(\alpha_j + \beta_j + \gamma_j)}_{\text{sum of all angles at vertices}}
    - \pi f \\[4pt]
&= 2\pi v - \pi f \\[4pt]
&= 2\pi\,(v - e + f)
= 2\pi\,\underbrace{(v - e + f)}_{\chi(S)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the integral of curvature depends only on the Euler characteristic $\chi(S)$, which is independent of the chosen triangulation.

## 3.1 Gauss–Bonnet Theorem (compact surface)

For a compact surface $S$,
$$
\boxed{\displaystyle
\int_{S} K\,dA = 2\pi\,\chi(S).}
$$

For a closed, orientable surface of genus $g$ (number of “holes”),
$$
\chi(S) = 2 - 2g.
$$

## 3.2 Full version (with boundary)

On an oriented surface $S$, let $R$ be a compact region with piecewise
smooth boundary $\partial R$. Then
$$
\boxed{\displaystyle
\int_{R} K\,dA
\;+\;
\int_{\partial R} k_{g}\,ds
\;=\;
2\pi\,\chi(R)
\;-\;
\sum (\text{exterior angles}).}
$$


# 4 Smooth manifolds

A smooth manifold $M$ is a **second countable Hausdorff** space
equipped with a **smooth atlas**.

- **Second countable:**  
  The topology of $M$ is generated by a countable collection of open sets.

- **Hausdorff:**  
  Any two points $p,q\in M$ are contained in disjoint open sets.

- **Smooth atlas:**  
  $M$ is covered by open sets $U_j$ with homeomorphisms
  $$
  \phi_j : V_j \subset \mathbb{R}^{n} \longrightarrow U_j,
  $$
  such that the transition maps
  $$
  \phi_i^{-1}\circ \phi_j : \phi_j^{-1}(U_i\cap U_j) \to \phi_i^{-1}(U_i\cap U_j)
  $$
  are smooth on overlaps.

(Whitney Embedding Theorem: without loss of generality, we can assume $M \subset \mathbb{R}^{2n}$)
