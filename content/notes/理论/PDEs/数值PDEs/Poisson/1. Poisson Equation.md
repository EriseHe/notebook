# 2  Poisson Equation

## 2.0  Poisson Equation

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
-\Delta u &= f \qquad \text{and some BCs} \\
\text{OR:}\qquad
-\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x_1^2}
-\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x_2^2}
-\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x_3^2}
&= f .
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

Boundary Conditions:

- **Dirichlet** $u(\partial\Omega)=g$.

  With lifting function, we can turn Dirichlet into a homogeneous condition: Let $\mathcal{L}_g\in\Omega$ s.t. $\mathcal{L}_g(\partial\Omega)=g$. Then,

  $$
  u=\mathring{u}+\mathcal{L}_g
  \Longrightarrow
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
  -\Delta \mathring{u} &= f+\Delta \mathcal{L}_g \\
  \mathring{u}(\partial\Omega) &= 0
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
  $$

  So, only homogeneous Dirichlet BC will be discussed.

- **Neumann:** $\nabla u\cdot n=g$.

  Poisson equations with Neumann BCs are not WP. The solutions are not unique by adding constants.

- **Robin:** $\nabla u\cdot n+\alpha u(\partial\Omega)=g$.

## 2.1  Poisson Equation as a Minimization Problem

Consider the energy:

$$
J(u)=\frac{1}{2}\int_{\Omega}|\nabla u|^2-\int_{\Omega}fu
$$

with boundary condition $u(\partial\Omega)=0$.

**Goal:** Find the configuration $(u)$ that minimizes the energy. i.e.,

$$
\min J(u).
$$

Solve $\nabla J=0$. Let $v$ be a function such that $v(\partial\Omega)=0$. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\nabla J
&=\lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\frac{1}{\varepsilon}\big(J(u+\varepsilon v)-J(u)\big) \\
&=\lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\frac{1}{\varepsilon}\left[
\frac{1}{2}\int_{\Omega}\big(|\nabla u|^2+\varepsilon^2|\nabla v|^2+2\varepsilon\nabla u\cdot\nabla v\big)
-\int_{\Omega}fu-\varepsilon\int_{\Omega}fv
-\frac{1}{2}|\nabla u|^2+\int_{\Omega}fu
\right] \\
&=\lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\left[
\frac{1}{2}\int_{\Omega}\varepsilon|\nabla v|^2+2\nabla u\cdot\nabla v
-\int_{\Omega}fv
\right] \\
&=\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\cdot\nabla v-\int_{\Omega}fv
=0
\qquad
\forall\, v\ \text{s.t.}\ v(\partial\Omega)=0.
\end{aligned}
$$

By Greens’ Theorem and Gauss Theorem,

$$
\int_{\partial\Omega} v\cdot n=\int_{\Omega}\nabla\cdot (v)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\int_{\Omega}\nabla\cdot (\beta\rho)
=
\int_{\Omega}\nabla\rho\cdot\beta
+
\int_{\Omega}\nabla\cdot\beta\cdot\rho .
$$

So,

$$
\int_{\Omega}\nabla\rho\cdot\beta
=
\int_{\partial\Omega}\rho\beta\cdot n
-
\int_{\Omega}(\nabla\cdot\beta)\cdot\rho .
$$

Substitute $\rho=v$ and $\beta=\nabla u$:

$$
\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\cdot\nabla v
=
\underbrace{\int_{\partial\Omega} v\nabla u\cdot n}_{=0\ \text{b/c}\ v(\partial v)=0}
-\int_{\Omega}(\Delta u)v.
$$

Hence, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
-\int_{\Omega}(\Delta u)v-\int_{\Omega}fv&=0 \\
\int_{\Omega}(-\Delta u-f)v&=0 \\
-\Delta u&=f .
\end{aligned}
$$

> [!remark]
> **Remark.**
>
> - Strong formulation:
>
>   $$
>   -\Delta u=f
>   $$
>
>   requires second derivatives (Laplace operator)
>
> - Week formulation:
>
>   $$
>   \int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v-\int_{\Omega}fv=0
>   \qquad
>   \forall\, v\ \text{s.t.}\ v(\partial\Omega)=0
>   $$
>
>   only needs first-order derivatives (Gradient).

If $u$ solves

$$
\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v-\int_{\Omega}fv=0
\qquad
\forall\, v\ \text{s.t.}\ v(\partial\Omega)=0,
$$

then $J(u)\le J(u+w)$.

## 2.2  Finite Differences

Two mathematically equivalent strong formulations may be different numerically:

$$
-\Delta u=f.
$$

On the other hand, we can write

$$
-\nabla\cdot(\nabla u)=f
\Longrightarrow
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\nabla u &= \sigma \\
\nabla\cdot\sigma &= f.
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

## 2.2.1  Poisson Equation in 1D

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
-u''&=f,\qquad x\in[0,1] \\
u(0)&=u(1)=0
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

- Max-Min Principle

- Discretization:

  $$
  -u''(x_j)=f(x_j).
  $$

  Collocation/Mesh. $|h|$ is the step size.

- **Claim**

  $$
  (u')'\approx \frac{u(x_{j+1})-2u(x_j)+u(x_{j-1})}{h^2}.
  $$

**Proof 1.** Denote $u(x_{j+1})=u_{j+1}$. By Taylor expansion:

$$
\begin{aligned}
u_{j+1}
&=u_j+u'(x_j)h+\frac{1}{2}u''(x_j)h^2+\frac{1}{3!}u'''(x_j)h^3+\frac{1}{4!}u^{(4)}(x_j)h^4+O(h^5) \\
u_{j-1}
&=u_j-u'(x_j)h+\frac{1}{2}u''(x_j)h^2-\frac{1}{3!}u'''(x_j)h^3+\frac{1}{4!}u^{(4)}(x_j)h^4+O(h^5) \\
u_{j+1}+u_{j-1}
&=2u_j+u''(x_j)h^2+\frac{2}{4!}u^{(4)}h^4+O(h^6) \\
u''(x_j)
&=\frac{u_{j+1}+u_{j-1}-2u_j}{h^2}
+\underbrace{\frac{1}{12}u^{(4)}(x_j)h^2}_{\tau(h),\ \text{consistency error}}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, when we make the mesh finer $(h\to \frac{1}{2}h)$, the consistency error decrease by 4 times (order of 2).  
Q.E.D. $\blacksquare$

- Form the numerical problem: Except for the endpoints, we can write

  $$
  -\frac{u_{j+1}-2u_j+u_{j-1}}{h^2}=f_j.
  $$

Denote

$$
u=
\begin{bmatrix}
u_1\\
u_2\\
\vdots\\
u_{N-1}
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
f=
\begin{bmatrix}
f_1\\
f_2\\
\vdots\\
f_{N-1}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Define

$$
A=\frac{1}{h^2}
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 & 0 \\
-1 & \ddots & \ddots \\
0 & \ddots & \ddots & -1 \\
& & -1 & 2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then, we get $Au=f$.

> [!proposition]
> **Proposition**  $A$ is SPD (symmetric positive definite) $\Longrightarrow$ all eigenvalues of $A$ are positive.
>
> **Proof 2.** We will show $A$ is symmetric, and $A$ is positive definite.
>
> 1. Symmetry is trivial.
>
> 2. Positivity: $[\mathrm{WTS}:\ \forall\, x\ne 0,\ x^\top Ax>0.]$
>
>    $$
>    \begin{aligned}
>    x^\top Ax
>    &=\frac{1}{h^2}\big(2x_1^2-2x_1x_2+x_2^2+\cdots\big) \\
>    &=\frac{1}{h^2}\big(x_1^2+(x_1-x_2)^2+\cdots+(x_j-x_{j+1})^2+\cdots+x_{N-1}^2\big) \\
>    &\ge 0.
>    \end{aligned}
>    $$
>
>    If we have a constant vector: $x=\begin{bmatrix}x_* \cdots x_*\end{bmatrix}^\top$. Then,
>
>    $$
>    x^\top Ax=\frac{1}{2}(x_*^2+x_*^2)=0 \Longleftrightarrow x_*=0.
>    $$
>
>    Hence, $\forall\, x\ne 0,\ x^\top Ax=0$.
>
> So, $A$ is SPD.  
> Q.E.D. $\blacksquare$

- **Corollary**  $A$ is non-singular, and the numerical problem is well-posed.

- Error Analysis:

$$
\begin{aligned}
u_{\mathrm{num}}&=A^{-1}f \ \Longrightarrow\ \|u_{\mathrm{num}}\|\le \|A^{-1}\|\,\|f\| \\
Au_{\mathrm{ex}}&=f+\tau,
\qquad
\begin{bmatrix}\tau_j\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
c\,f''(x_j)h^2
\end{bmatrix} \\
e&=u_{\mathrm{ex}}-u_{\mathrm{num}}=A^{-1}\tau \\
\|e\|&\le \|A^{-1}\|\,\underbrace{\|\tau\|}_{O(h^2)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

But this is not enough: $A^{-1}$ also depends on $h$.

> [!proposition]
> **Proposition**  $\|A^{-1}\|_2\ \perp\!\!\!\perp\ h$.
>
> **Proof 3.** Recall: $\|A\|_2=\rho(A)=\max_i|\lambda_i|$, the spectral radius. Then,
>
> $$
> \|A^{-1}\|_2=\frac{1}{\min_i|\lambda_i|}=\rho(A^{-1}).
> $$
>
> $[\mathrm{WTS}:\ \text{eigenvalues of }A\ \text{(or }A^{-1}\text{) are independent of }h].$
>
> $$
> A=\frac{1}{h^2}\,\mathrm{trid}(-1,2,-1),\qquad T=\mathrm{trid}(-1,2,-1).
> $$
>
> Solve $Tx=\lambda x$:
>
> $$
> (T-\lambda I)x=0
> $$
>
> $$
> x_{j-1}-(2-\lambda)x_j+x_{j+1}=0.
> $$
>
> Characteristic equation:
>
> $$
> 1-(2-\lambda)\rho^2+\rho^2=0
> $$
>
> $$
> x_j=a\rho_1^j+b\rho_2^j
> \qquad\text{or}\qquad
> x_j=a\rho^j+jb\rho^j.
> $$
>
> Imposing $\Delta\ge 0$, we get $\lambda\ge 4$. Imposing BC: $x_0=x_N=0$, we get $a=b=0$, and thus $x=0$. Trivial.
>
> Hence, we don’t have eigenvalues $\ge 4$, and we should only consider complex solutions.
>
> $$
> \rho_{1,2}=\frac{2-\lambda\pm i\sqrt{2}\sqrt{4-\lambda}}{2}
> =\cos(\theta)\pm i\sin(\theta)
> \qquad
> [\text{Enforcing }|\rho|=1]
> $$
>
> So,
>
> $$
> x_j=a\rho_1^j+b\rho_2^j.
> $$
>
> Since $x_0=x_N=0$:
>
> $$
> \left\{
> \begin{aligned}
> a+b&=0 \\
> a\rho_1^N+B\rho_2^N&=0
> \end{aligned}
> \right.
> \Longrightarrow
> \rho_1^N-\bar{\rho}_1^N=0.
> $$
>
> $$
> \cos(N\theta)+i\sin(N\theta)-\cos(N\theta)+i\sin(N\theta)=0
> \Longrightarrow
> \theta=\frac{j\pi}{N},\qquad j=1,\ldots,N-1.
> $$
>
> Going back, we have
>
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> \lambda_j
> &=-\frac{1}{\rho}+\rho+2
> =2\left(1-\cos\left(\frac{j\pi}{N}\right)\right) \\
> \cos(2\alpha)&=1-2\sin^2(\alpha) \\
> 2\sin^2(\alpha)&=1-\cos(2\alpha)
> \Longrightarrow
> \lambda_j=4\sin^2\left(\frac{j\pi}{2N}\right).
> \end{aligned}
> $$
>
> Hence, eigenvalues of $A$:
>
> $$
> \lambda=\frac{4}{h^2}\sin^2\left(\frac{\pi}{2N}\right)
> =\frac{4}{h^2}\sin^2\left(\frac{\pi}{2}h\right)
> \qquad
> [h=\frac{1}{N}]
> $$
>
> When $h\to 0$, $\sin^2\left(\frac{\pi}{2}h\right)\to \left(\frac{\pi}{2}h\right)^2$. So,
>
> $$
> \lambda=\frac{4}{h^2}\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^2h^2=\pi^2\ \perp\!\!\!\perp\ h.
> $$
>
> Q.E.D. $\blacksquare$

> [!corollary]
> **Corollary**  Our numerical method is convergent.
>
> $$
> e=A^{-1}\tau.
> $$
>
> When $h\to 0,\ \tau\to 0,\ \|A^{-1}\|_2\to \pi^2$. Then,
>
> $$
> \|e\|_2\le \|A^{-1}\|_2\|\tau\|_2=\pi^2\|\tau\|_2.
> $$
>
> $$
> \|\tau\|_2^2=\sum_{i=1}^{N-1}\tau_i^2=\sum_{i=1}^{N-1}c^2\big(f''(x_j)\big)^2h^4.
> $$
>
> If $f''$ is bounded, $\|f''\|\le \tilde{c}$. Then, combing the constants and the sum, we have
>
> $$
> \|\tau\|_2^2\le \tilde{c}(N-1)h^4.
> $$
>
> Recall that $N=\frac{1}{h}$. Then, $\|\tau\|_2^2\le \tilde{c}h^3 \Longrightarrow \|\tau\|_2\sim O(h^{3/2}).$ So,
>
> $$
> \|e\|_2\sim O(h^{3/2}).
> $$

- Practical Notes:

  When constructing $A$, we first assemble the full matrix, and then replace the boundaries with actual BCs.

- Another perspective of FD Convergence.

> [!definition]
> **Definition 2.2.1 ($h$-Norm).** Let $v_h$ be a grid function.
>
> $$
> \|v_h\|_h^2=
> \left(\sum_{j=1}^{N-1} v_j^2+\frac{1}{2}v_0^2+\frac{1}{2}v_N^2\right)h,
> $$
>
> and
>
> $$
> L_h v_h=\frac{v_{j+1}-2v_j+v_{j-1}}{h^2}.
> $$

Some properties include:

1. $$
   (L_h v_h,w_h)=(v_h,L_h w_h)
   $$

2. $$
   (L_h v_h,w_h)=\sum_{j=1}^{N-1}\frac{v_{j+1}-v_j}{h}\cdot\frac{w_{j+1}-w_j}{h}.
   $$

3. $h(L_h v_h,v_h)$ is a norm. Denote this norm as $|||\cdot|||_h$.

   **Proof 4.**

   $$
   h(L_h v_h,v_h)=h\sum\left(\frac{v_{j+1}-v_j}{h}\right)^2.
   $$

   Non-negativity and other properties can be easily shown.  
   Q.E.D. $\blacksquare$

4. $c\|v_h\|_h^2\le |||v_h|||_h^2$.

5. Problem: $L_h u_h=f_h$.

   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   c\|u_h\|_h^2
   &\le h(L_h u_h,u_h)\le \|f_h\|_h\|u_h\|_h
   \Longrightarrow \|u_h\|\le c\|f_h\|_h \\
   c\|e_h\|_h^2
   &\le h(L_h e_h,e_h)\le \|\tau\|_h\|e_h\|_h
   \Longrightarrow \|e_h\|_h\le \frac{1}{c}\|\tau\|_h
   \end{aligned}
   $$

   $$

   \end{aligned}
   $$

   so

   $$
   \|\tau\|_h\le h^2\sqrt{\max_{j=1,\ldots,N}c_j}\sim O(h^2).
   $$

## 2.3  Galerkin Methods

**Set-Up**

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
-\Delta u&=f \\
u(\partial\Omega)&=0
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

Weak formulation: Find $u$ s.t.

$$
\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v\,dx=\int_{\Omega}fv\,dx
\qquad
\forall\, v\ \text{s.t.}\ v(\partial\Omega)=0.
$$

**Requirements** $\nabla u\in L^2,\ \nabla v\in L^2\Longrightarrow u,v\in H_0^1,$ and $f\in H^{-1}$ (dual of $H_0^1$, functional applied to $H_0^1$.)

> [!lemma]
> **Lemma 2.1 Max-Milgram Lemma (Sufficient Condition)**  Consider the problem $a(u,v)=F(v)$, where $u,v\in X$. If
>
> - $a(\cdot,\cdot)$ is a bilinear form,
>
> - bounded: $\exists\, M>0$ s.t. $|a(u,v)|\le M\|u\|_X\|v\|_X$,
>
> - coercivity: $a(u,u)\ge \alpha\|u\|_X^2\ \forall\, u\in X$, and
>
> - $F(\cdot)$ is a continuous linear functional: $\|F(v)\|_X\le \gamma\|v\|_X\ \forall\, v\in X$,
>
> then, the problem is well-posed. i.e., ①②③④ $\Longrightarrow$ well-posedness (sufficient but not necessary).

> [!theorem]
> **Theorem 2.3.2**
>
> Poisson equation is well-posed.


>**Proof 1.**
>
> $$
> \left\{
> \begin{aligned}
> -\Delta u&=f \\
> u(\partial\Omega)&=0
> \end{aligned}
> \right.
> \Longrightarrow
> \forall\, v\in H_0^1,\ \text{find }u\in H_0^1\ \text{s.t.}\ 
> \underbrace{\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v}_{a(u,v)}
> -
> \underbrace{\int_{\Omega}fv}_{F(v)}
> =0.
> $$
>
> - $\|u\|_{H^1}^2=\|u\|_{L^2}^2+\|\nabla u\|_{L^2}^2$
>
> - $$
>   \left|\int_{\Omega}fv\right|\le \|f\|_{L^2}\|v\|_{L^2}\le \|f\|_{H^1}\|v\|_{H^1}
>   $$
>
> - $\forall\, u\in X=H_0^1$,
>
>   $$
>   \int_{\Omega}|\nabla u|^2=a(u,u)\ge \alpha\|u\|_X^2.
>   $$
>
> By Poincaré Inequality,
>
> $$
> C_p\int_{\Omega}u^2\le \int_{\Omega}|\nabla u|^2
> $$
>
> $$
> C_p\|u\|_{L^2}^2\le \|\nabla u\|_{L^2}^2=\underbrace{|u|_{H^1}^2}_{semi\text{-}norm}
> $$
>
> $$
> \|u\|_{H^1}^2=\int_{\Omega}u^2+\int_{\Omega}\nabla u^2\le \left(\frac{1}{C_p}+1\right)\int_{\Omega}|\nabla u|^2
> $$
>
> So, we have coercivity.
>
> By Lax-Milgram Lemma, Poisson equation is WP.  
> Q.E.D. $\blacksquare$

> [!remark]
> **Remark.**  $a(u,v)=\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v$ is a scalar product in $H_0^1$:
>
> - symmetric, and
>
> - non-negativity (by coercivity).
>
> The norm induced by $a(u,u)=\|u\|_a^2$ is equivalent to $\|u\|_{H^1}^2$:
>
> $$
> C\|u\|_{H^1}^2\le a(u,u)\le \|u\|_{H^1}^2.
> $$

If we change the problem and the space:

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
-\Delta u&=f \\
\nabla u\cdot n&=0 \qquad \text{on }\partial\Omega\ \ (\text{Neumann})
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

$$
\Longrightarrow\ \ \text{Find }u\in H^1\ \text{s.t.}\ \ \forall\, v\in H^1:
\qquad
\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v=\int_{\Omega}fv.
$$

Now, we don’t have Poincaré Inequality anymore, and $a(u,v)$ is not coercive. We cannot apply LM Lemma. In fact, the problem is not WP. Any $(u+\text{constant})$ will be a solution.

Hw to make the problem WP?

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
-\Delta u+\sigma u&=f,\qquad \sigma>0 \\
\nabla u\cdot n&=0 \qquad \text{on }\partial\Omega
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

$$
\Longrightarrow\ \ \int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v+\sigma\int_{\Omega}uv=\int_{\Omega}fv
\qquad
\underbrace{}_{a(u,v)}
$$

$$
|a(u,v)|\le \max(\sigma,1)\|u\|_{H^1}\|v\|_{H^1},
\qquad
\min(1,\sigma)\|u\|_{H^1}^2\le a(u,v).
$$

$\Longrightarrow$ $a(u,v)$ is coercive, and the problem is WP.

Now, let’s consider mixed boundary conditions (i.e., Robin conditions).

$$
\Omega,\ \Gamma_D,\ \Gamma_N
$$

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
-\Delta u&=f \\
u(\Gamma_D)&=0 \\
\nabla u\cdot n|_{\Gamma_N}&=0,
\end{aligned}
\right.
\qquad
\text{where }\Gamma_D\cup\Gamma_N=\partial\Omega,\ \text{and measure}(\Gamma_D\cap\Gamma_N)=0.
$$

$$
-\int_{\Omega}\Delta u\cdot v
=
-\underbrace{\int_{\Gamma_N}\nabla u\cdot n\,v}_{=0}
+\underbrace{\int_{\Gamma_D}\nabla u\cdot n\,v}_{=0}
+\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v.
$$

Problem: Find $u\in H^1_{\Gamma_D}$ s.t. $\forall\, v\in H^1_{\Gamma_D}$ s.t.

$$
\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v=\int_{\Omega}fv.
$$

This problem is WP because we have Poicaré on $H^1_{\Gamma_D}$.

Now, consider the true problem: Find $u\in X=H_0^1$ s.t.

$$
\int_{\Omega}\nabla u\nabla v=\int_{\Omega}fv
\qquad
\forall\, v\in X
\Longrightarrow
a(u,v)=F(v).
$$

**Assumption**  $a(u,v)=a(v,u)$ (symmetry) and all LM assumptions.

The Galerkin Methods solve the numerical problem in $X_N\subset X$: Find $u_N\in X_N$ s.t.

$$
a(u_N,v_N)=F(v_N),
\qquad
\forall\, v_N\in X_N.
$$

This problem is WP, which can be trivially proven by LM assumptions.

> [!proposition]
> **Proposition 2.3**  *This problem is strongly consistent (consistency error is 0).*
>
> **Proof 2.** Note that $a(u,v)=F(v)$ and $a(u_N,v_N)=F(v_N)$. Then,
>
> $$
> a(u,v_N)=F(v_N)
> \qquad
> \text{because }X_N\subset X.
> $$
>
> The consistence error is 0. So, the problem is strongly consistent. We could also show
>
> $$
> a(u-u_N,v_N)=0
> \qquad
> \forall\, v_N\in X_N.
> $$
>
> Q.E.D. $\blacksquare$

> [!proposition]
> **Proposition 2.4**  *This problem is stable.*
>
> **Proof 3.**
>
> $$
> \alpha\|u_N\|_{H^1}^2\le a(u_N,u_N)\le \|f\|\cdot \|u_N\|_{H^1}.
> $$
>
> So, it is clearly stable.  
> Q.E.D. $\blacksquare$

> [!lemma]
> **Lemma 2.5 Cèa Lemma**
>
> $$
> \|u-u_N\|_{H^1}\le K\inf_{w_N\in X_N}\|u-w_N\|_{H^1}.
> $$
>
> **Proof 4.**
>
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> \alpha\|u-u_N\|_{H^1}^2
> &\le a(u-u_N,u-u_N)
> =a(u-u_N,u-w_N)+a(u-u_N,w_N-u_N)
> \qquad[\text{Bilinearity, } w_N\in X_N] \\
> \alpha\|u-u_N\|_{H^1}^2
> &\le a(u-u_N,u-w_N)
> \le M\|u-u_N\|_{H^1}\cdot \|u-w_N\|_{H^1}
> \qquad[\text{Continuity}] \\
> \|u-u_N\|_{H^1}
> &\le \frac{M}{\alpha}\|u-w_N\|_{H^1}.
> \end{aligned}
> $$
>
> $$
> \|u-u_N\|_{H^1}\le K\|u-w_N\|_{H^1}
> \quad \forall\, w_N\in X_N
> \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
> \|u-u_N\|_{H^1}\le K\inf_{w_N\in X_N}\|u-w_N\|_{H^1}.
> $$
>
> Q.E.D. $\blacksquare$

This Lemma implies that Galerkin solution is not the best one, but its $H^1$ norm error goes to 0 as quick as the best one. i.e., it has the same rate of convergence as the best approximation in $X_N$. When

$$
N\to\infty,\ X_n\to X,\ \text{and}\ \inf_{w_N\in X_n}\|u-w_N\|_{H^1}\to 0.
$$

> [!claim]
> **Claim 2.6**  $P_N:$ Find $u_N\in X_N,\ a(u_N,v_n)=F(v_N)\ \ \forall\, v_N\in X_N$ is WP.
>
> **Proof 5.** We can write $u_N=\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}c_j\varphi_j(x)$, where $\varphi_j(\cdot)$ are basis functions. Then, $X_n=\mathrm{span}\{\varphi_j\}_{j=0}^{N-1}$.
>
> So,
>
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> a(u_N,v_N)
> &=a\left(\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}c_j\varphi_j(x),v_N\right)=F(v_N) \\
> \sum_{j=0}^{N-1}c_j a(\varphi_j,v_N)
> &=F(v_N)
> \qquad[\text{Bilinearity}]
> \end{aligned}
> $$
>
> Instead of testing on $v_N\in X_N$, as $X_N=\mathrm{span}\{\varphi_j\}_{j=0}^{N-1}$, we just need to prove it works for all $\varphi_i$’s.
>
> $$
> \sum_{j=0}^{N-1}c_j a(\varphi_j,\varphi_i)=F(\varphi_i)
> \qquad[\text{Take }v_N=\varphi_i]
> $$
>
> Define
>
> $$
> A:=\big[a(\varphi_j,\varphi_i)\big]_{i,j},
> \qquad
> c=\big[c_j\big]_j,
> \qquad
> b=\big[F(\varphi_i)\big]_i.
> $$
>
> So, $P_N$ becomes
>
> $$
> Ac=b.
> $$

> [!claim]
> **Claim 2.7**  $A$ is non-singular
>
> - $A$ is symmetric: $a(\varphi_j,\varphi_i)=a(\varphi_i,\varphi_j)\qquad[\text{symmetry}]$
>
> - $A$ is positive-definite: Assume $c\ne 0$, then
>
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> c^\top Ac
> &=\sum_{i,j} c_jA_{ij}c_i
> =\sum_{i,j} c_j a(\varphi_j,\varphi_i)c_i \\
> &=\sum_{i,j} a(c_j\varphi_j,c_i\varphi_i)
> \qquad[\text{bilinearity}] \\
> &=a\left(\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}c_j\varphi_j,\sum_{i=0}^{N-1}c_i\varphi_i\right)
> \qquad[\text{bilinearity}] \\
> &=a(u_N,u_N)
> \qquad\left[u_N=\sum_{i=0}^{N-1}c_i\varphi_i\right] \\
> &\ge \alpha\|u_N\|_{H^1}^2\ge 0
> \qquad[\text{coercivity}]
> \end{aligned}
> $$
>
> So, $A$ is SPD, and thus $A$ is non-singular. $\Longrightarrow\ P_N$ is WP.  
> Q.E.D. $\blacksquare$
