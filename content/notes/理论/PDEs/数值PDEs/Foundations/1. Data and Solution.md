## 0.1 Basic Concept

We think of a **physical problem** (the actual physics in a perfect world) as a map that takes **data** $\to$ **solution**, and a **numerical problem** as an approximation of that *map* (and of the data).


![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/snip/images/fKRfBMUfZXvuDbVZEPbx1Q8ZVMMroL1g3XxvwELQNkA.original.fullsize.png)

## 0.1.1 Three Mappings
A *physical map $P$* (exact, continuous, infinite-dimensional reality) is formulated as

$$
\underbrace{d}_{\text{data}}
\;\longmapsto\;
\underbrace{u}_{\text{solution}}
=
P(d)
$$
- data $d$: initial conditions, boundary conditions, or source terms
- solution $u$: the *true*, continuous function that solves the PDE.

>On computer, we must discretize the problem so the continuous map can be handled by the algorithm. 

A *numerical map* $N$ is formulated as:

$$\underbrace{ d_h }_{\text{approx. data} } \longmapsto \underbrace{ u_h }_{\text{numerical sol.} } = N(d_h)$$
- **mesh step** $h$: discretization size parameter/step size
- **approximated data** $d_{h}$: the projection or restriction of the continuous data $d$ onto a finite-dimensional discrete space
- **numerical solution** $u_h$: the exact solution to the discrete algebraic system of equations (e.g., $A_h u_h = d_h$)

A *perturbed numerical map* $N_{\text{perturb}}$, by definition, is formulated as

$$
\underbrace{d_h + \delta_h}_{\text{perturbed data}}
\;\longmapsto\;
\underbrace{u_h + \varepsilon_h}_{\text{perturbed numerical sol.}}
$$

So the diagram is comparing:
$$
\begin{align}
\text{data space: }  & d, \;d_h,\;d_h+\delta_h \\
\text{solution space: } &  u,\; u_{u},\; u_{h}+\epsilon_{h}
\end{align}
$$

with the mappings $P$, $N$, and $N_{\text{perturb}}$ connecting them.

## 0.1.2 Three Fundamental Properties

To evaluate the relationship between these maps, or bridge the gap between the physical reality ($P$) and the numerical execution ($N$), we ask 3 most important questions for numerical PDEs: 

### 0.1.2.1 Convergence

> Does $u_h \to u$ when $h \to 0$?

**Convergence** measures the "distance" (error) between the *true physics* and our *numerical approximation*. By definition, a method is **convergent** if, as the mesh is refined ($h \to 0$), the numerical solution approaches the true physical solution ($u_{h}\to u$).

### 0.1.2.2 Consistency

> Does $N \to P$ when $h \to 0$?

**Consistency** checks the "local accuracy" of our discrete equations, the map $N$, in relations to $P$. In other words, "do our discrete equations accurately mimic the continuous differential equations?".

We test this by plugging the exact solution $u$ into the discrete operator $A_h$. The resulting residual is the *Truncation Error* $\tau_h$.
### 0.1.2.3 Stability

> Does $\varepsilon_h \to 0$ when $\delta_h \to 0$?

**Stability** is about sensitivity to perturbations. If you slightly nudge the data ($d_h + \delta_h$), does the solution ripple slightly ($\varepsilon_h$) or explode? 

The operator $N$ must be *uniformly bounded* with respect to $h$. In linear algebra terms, we use condition number of matrix $A_h$ to measure that behavior. 


## 0.1.3 The Fundamental link

For well-posed linear problems, you don't need to prove convergence directly (which is hard because we usually don't know $u$). Instead, we use the Lax Equivalence Theorem:


> [!theorem|] Lax Equivalence Theorem
> For linear, well-posed problems, we the relationship below:
> $$
> \text{Consistency + Stability}  \iff \text{Convergence}
> $$
