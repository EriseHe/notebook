### 3.4.1 Reaction-Dominated Problem

Consider the boundary value problem
$$
\begin{aligned}
-\mu u'' + \sigma u &= 0, \qquad x\in (0,1),\\
u(0)&=0,\\
u(1)&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$
Let
$$
0=x_0<x_1<\cdots<x_N=1,
\qquad
h=x_i-x_{i-1}=\frac1N,
$$
and let $\{\psi_i\}$ be the standard piecewise linear hat basis functions. For an interior node $x_i$, $i=1,\dots,N-1$, we have
$$
\psi_i(x)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{x-x_{i-1}}{h}, & x\in [x_{i-1},x_i],\\[1ex]
\dfrac{x_{i+1}-x}{h}, & x\in [x_i,x_{i+1}],\\[1ex]
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

#### 3.4.1.1 Weak Form

The weak form is: find $u_h\in V_h$ such that for all $v_h\in V_h^0$,
$$
\mu \int_0^1 u_h' v_h'\,dx
+\sigma \int_0^1 u_h v_h\,dx
=0.
$$
Taking $v_h=\psi_i$ gives, for each interior node $i$,
$$
\mu \int_0^1 u_h' \psi_i'\,dx
+\sigma \int_0^1 u_h \psi_i\,dx
=0.
$$


### 3.4.2 Standard $P_1$ Finite Element Discretization

Using the usual stiffness and mass matrices
$$
\underbrace{
\frac{\mu}{h}\left[\begin{array}{cccc}
2 & -1 & & \\
-1 & 2 & -1 & \\
& \ddots & \ddots & \ddots \\
& & -1 & 2
\end{array}\right]}_{\text{stiffness}}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\underbrace{
\frac{\sigma h}{6}\left[\begin{array}{cccc}
4 & 1 & & \\
1 & 4 & 1 & \\
& \ddots & \ddots & \ddots \\
& & 1 & 4
\end{array}\right]}_{\text{consistent mass}}
$$
the nodal equation at an interior node is
$$
\begin{align}
\frac{\mu}{h}\,(-u_{i-1}+2u_i-u_{i+1})
+\frac{\sigma h}{6}(u_{i-1}+4u_i+u_{i+1}) & =0\\
-\mu\left(\frac{u_{i+1}-2u_i+u_{i-1}}{h^2}\right)
+\frac{\sigma}{6}\left(u_{i-1}+4u_i+u_{i+1}\right) & =0 \\
\end{align}
$$
and equivalently,
$$
\left(-\frac{\mu}{h^2}+\frac{\sigma}{6}\right)u_{i-1}
+\left(\frac{2\mu}{h^2}+\frac{4\sigma}{6}\right)u_i
+\left(-\frac{\mu}{h^2}+\frac{\sigma}{6}\right)u_{i+1}
 =0
$$
To keep the off-diagonal entries nonpositive, we require
$$
\begin{align}
-\frac{\mu}{h^2}+\frac{\sigma}{6}&\le 0\\
\mathbb{P}_{e}=\frac{\sigma h^2}{6\mu}&\le 1&&
\end{align}
\Longrightarrow\qquad
\boxed{h\le \sqrt{\frac{6\mu}{\sigma}}}
$$

for mesh restriction.
## Reaction-Dominated Problem

Consider
$$
\begin{aligned}
-\mu u'' + \sigma u &= 0, \qquad x\in (0,1),\\
u(0)&=0,\\
u(1)&=1.
\end{aligned}
$$

Let
$$
0=x_0<x_1<\cdots<x_N=1,
\qquad
h=x_i-x_{i-1}=\frac{1}{N},
$$
and let $\{\varphi_i\}$ be the standard piecewise linear hat basis functions.

---

## Weak Form

The weak form is: find $u_h$ such that
$$
\mu \int_0^1 u_h' v_h'\,dx+\sigma \int_0^1 u_h v_h\,dx=0
$$
for all test functions $v_h$.

Taking $v_h=\varphi_i$, we obtain
$$
\mu \int_0^1 u_h' \varphi_i'\,dx+\sigma \int_0^1 u_h \varphi_i\,dx=0.
$$

---

## Standard Finite Element Equation

Using the usual stiffness and consistent mass matrices, the nodal equation at an interior node $x_i$ is
$$
\frac{\mu}{h}(-u_{i-1}+2u_i-u_{i+1})
+\frac{\sigma h}{6}(u_{i-1}+4u_i+u_{i+1})=0.
$$

Dividing by $h$, this becomes
$$
-\mu\left(\frac{u_{i+1}-2u_i+u_{i-1}}{h^2}\right)
+\frac{\sigma}{6}(u_{i-1}+4u_i+u_{i+1})=0.
$$

Equivalently,
$$
\left(-\frac{\mu}{h^2}+\frac{\sigma}{6}\right)u_{i-1}
+\left(\frac{2\mu}{h^2}+\frac{4\sigma}{6}\right)u_i
+\left(-\frac{\mu}{h^2}+\frac{\sigma}{6}\right)u_{i+1}=0.
$$

To keep the off-diagonal coefficients nonpositive, we require
$$
-\frac{\mu}{h^2}+\frac{\sigma}{6}\le 0,
$$
that is,
$$
\frac{\sigma h^2}{6\mu}\le 1.
$$

Hence,
$$
h\le \sqrt{\frac{6\mu}{\sigma}}.
$$

---

## Mass Lumping

We now approximate the mass term by the trapezoidal rule, written in your notation as $\sigma(T)$.

### Off-diagonal term

For $j\neq i$, only the neighboring cases $j=i-1$ and $j=i+1$ need to be checked, since otherwise the supports do not overlap and the integral is automatically $0$. For example,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma \int_0^1 \varphi_i\varphi_j
&\approx \sigma(T)\int_0^1 \varphi_i\varphi_j \\
&= \sigma(T)\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_{i+1}} \varphi_i\varphi_j \\
&= \sigma(T)\left(
\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i}\varphi_i\varphi_{i-1}
+\int_{x_i}^{x_{i+1}}\varphi_i\varphi_{i-1}
+\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i}\varphi_i\varphi_{i+1}
+\int_{x_i}^{x_{i+1}}\varphi_i\varphi_{i+1}
\right) \\
&= \sigma(T)\left(
\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i}\varphi_i\varphi_{i-1}
+0
+0
+\int_{x_i}^{x_{i+1}}\varphi_i\varphi_{i+1}
\right),
\end{aligned}
$$
since
$$
\int_{x_i}^{x_{i+1}}\varphi_i\varphi_{i-1}=0,
\qquad
\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i}\varphi_i\varphi_{i+1}=0,
$$
because the supports do not overlap on those intervals.

Applying the trapezoidal rule on each remaining interval,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma \int_0^1 \varphi_i\varphi_j
&\approx
\sigma(T)\left(
\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i}\varphi_i\varphi_{i-1}
+
\int_{x_i}^{x_{i+1}}\varphi_i\varphi_{i+1}
\right) \\
&=
\frac{\sigma h}{2}\Big(\varphi_i\varphi_{i-1}(x_{i-1})+\varphi_i\varphi_{i-1}(x_i)\Big)
+
\frac{\sigma h}{2}\Big(\varphi_i\varphi_{i+1}(x_i)+\varphi_i\varphi_{i+1}(x_{i+1})\Big) \\
&= 0+0 \\
&= 0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore, the off-diagonal terms vanish:
$$
\sigma(T)\int_0^1 \varphi_i\varphi_j = 0,
\qquad j\neq i.
$$
### Diagonal term

For the diagonal term,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma \int_0^1 \varphi_i^2
&\approx \sigma(T)\int_0^1 \varphi_i^2 \\
&= \sigma(T)\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i}\varphi_i^2
+\sigma(T)\int_{x_i}^{x_{i+1}}\varphi_i^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Applying the trapezoidal rule,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma(T)\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i}\varphi_i^2
&=
\frac{\sigma h}{2}\Big(\varphi_i^2(x_{i-1})+\varphi_i^2(x_i)\Big),
\\
\sigma(T)\int_{x_i}^{x_{i+1}}\varphi_i^2
&=
\frac{\sigma h}{2}\Big(\varphi_i^2(x_i)+\varphi_i^2(x_{i+1})\Big).
\end{aligned}
$$

Since
$$
\varphi_i(x_{i-1})=0,\qquad
\varphi_i(x_i)=1,\qquad
\varphi_i(x_{i+1})=0,
$$
we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma(T)\int_0^1 \varphi_i^2
&=
\frac{\sigma h}{2}(0+1)+\frac{\sigma h}{2}(1+0) \\
&= \sigma h.
\end{aligned}
$$

So the lumped mass matrix is diagonal, with diagonal entry $\sigma h$.

---

## Lumped Scheme

Therefore, after mass lumping, the finite element equation becomes
$$
\frac{\mu}{h}(-u_{i-1}+2u_i-u_{i+1})+\sigma h\,u_i=0.
$$

Dividing by $h$ gives
$$
-\mu\left(\frac{u_{i+1}-2u_i+u_{i-1}}{h^2}\right)+\sigma u_i=0.
$$

Thus the lumped finite element scheme agrees with the finite difference scheme.

---

## Bilinear Form

For this reaction--diffusion problem, the bilinear form is
$$
a(\varphi_j,\varphi_i)
=
\mu\int_0^1 \varphi_j'\varphi_i'\,dx
+\sigma\int_0^1 \varphi_j\varphi_i\,dx.
$$





















### 3.4.3 Mass Lumping

To lump the mass matrix, we approximate the mass integral by nodal quadrature (equivalently, the trapezoidal rule on each element). For the $i$-th test function,
$$
\begin{align}
\int_0^1 u \psi_i\,dx
= & 
\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i} u\psi_i\,dx
+
\int_{x_i}^{x_{i+1}} u\psi_i\,dx
 \\
= & 
\frac{h}{2}\Big[\cancel{ u\underbrace{ \psi_i(x_{i-1})  }_{ 0 }}+u\underbrace{ \psi_i(x_i) }_{ 1 }\Big]  + 
\frac{h}{2}\Big[u\underbrace{ \psi_i(x_i) }_{ 1 }+u\cancel{ \underbrace{ \psi_i(x_{i+1}) }_{ 0 } }\Big]  &  & \text{trapezoidal rule}\\
=  &  \frac{h}{2}u_{i}+ \frac{h}{2}u_i = hu_{i}
\end{align} 
$$
So the lumped mass contribution is
$$
\boxed{\sigma \int_0^1 u\psi_i\,dx
\approx
\sigma h\,u_i}
$$

## Lumped-Mass Finite Element Scheme

Replacing the consistent mass term by the lumped one gives
$$
\begin{align}
\frac{\mu}{h}(-u_{i-1}+2u_i-u_{i+1})+\sigma h\,u_i & =0 \\
-\mu\left(\frac{u_{i+1}-2u_i+u_{i-1}}{h^2}\right)+\sigma u_i&=0
\end{align}
$$
This is exactly the standard finite difference discretization:
$$
\boxed{
\text{FD}=\text{FE}+\text{mass lumping}.
}
$$

### Bilinear Form for Reaction-Diffusion

For the reaction--diffusion problem, the bilinear form is
$$
a(\psi_j,\psi_i)
=
\mu\int_0^1 \psi_j'\psi_i'\,dx
+\sigma\int_0^1 \psi_j\psi_i\,dx.
$$

To record the finite element bilinear form:
$$
\boxed{
a(\psi_j,\psi_i)
=
\mu\int_0^1 \psi_j'\psi_i'\,dx
+\sigma\int_0^1 \psi_j\psi_i\,dx.
}
$$
### Lumped Bilinear Form

The **consistent** finite element bilinear form for the reaction--diffusion problem is
$$
a(w,v)
=
\mu\int_0^1 w'v'\,dx
+
\sigma\int_0^1 wv\,dx.
$$

After mass lumping, we replace the exact mass integral by a nodal quadrature rule:
$$
\int_0^1 wv\,dx
\;\approx\;
Q_h(wv).
$$
Thus the **lumped** bilinear form becomes
$$
\boxed{
a_h(w,v)
=
\mu\int_0^1 w'v'\,dx
+
\sigma Q_h(wv).
}
$$

For piecewise linear basis functions $\{\psi_i\}$, the nodal quadrature gives
$$
Q_h(\psi_j\psi_i)=h\,\delta_{ij},
$$
so
$$
\boxed{
a_h(\psi_j,\psi_i)
=
\mu\int_0^1 \psi_j'\psi_i'\,dx
+
\sigma h\,\delta_{ij}.
}
$$

Hence the mass-lumped finite element matrix is
$$
A_h = K + \sigma M_{\mathrm{lump}},
$$
where $K$ is the usual stiffness matrix and
$$
M_{\mathrm{lump}} = hI.
$$

So in 1D with $P_1$ elements,
$$
\boxed{
M_{\mathrm{lump}}
=
hI,
\qquad
K
=
\frac{\mu}{h}
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 & & \\
-1 & 2 & -1 & \\
& \ddots & \ddots & \ddots \\
& & -1 & 2
\end{bmatrix}.
}
$$

### Why Strang's Lemma Appears

The original finite element method uses the exact bilinear form $a(\cdot,\cdot)$, but mass lumping replaces it by the approximate form $a_h(\cdot,\cdot)$.  So this is **not exactly the same Galerkin method anymore**: it is a **perturbed variational problem**.

That is why **Strang's Lemma** is the right tool. It says that the total error is controlled by

1. the usual finite element approximation error, and  
2. the additional consistency error coming from replacing $a$ by $a_h$.

In this setting, if $u$ is the exact solution and $u_h$ is the lumped-mass finite element solution, then schematically
$$
\|u-u_h\|
\;\le\;
C\left(
\underbrace{\inf_{v_h\in V_h}\|u-v_h\|}_{\text{approximation error}}
+
\underbrace{\sup_{w_h\in V_h\setminus\{0\}}
\frac{|a(v_h,w_h)-a_h(v_h,w_h)|}{\|w_h\|}}_{\text{quadrature / lumping error}}
\right).
$$

So the full picture is:
$$
\text{mass lumping} = \text{exact mass bilinear form $\to$ quadrature-based diagonal approximation}
$$

And Strang's Lemma tells us that this is **acceptable** as long as the added quadrature error is small enough.

### Summary

- The **consistent** bilinear form is
  $$
  a(w,v)=\mu\int_0^1 w'v'\,dx+\sigma\int_0^1 wv\,dx.
  $$
- The **lumped** bilinear form is
  $$
  a_h(w,v)=\mu\int_0^1 w'v'\,dx+\sigma Q_h(wv).
  $$
- For $P_1$ elements in 1D,
  $$
  Q_h(\psi_j\psi_i)=h\delta_{ij},
  $$
  so the mass matrix becomes diagonal.
- Therefore, the standard finite difference scheme can be interpreted as
  $$
  \boxed{\text{FD}=\text{FE}+\text{mass lumping}.}
  $$




### 4. Strang's First Lemma

Let $u$ be the exact solution and $\tilde u_h$ the discrete solution defined by the approximate bilinear form $a_h$.  
Then, under the usual continuity and stability assumptions,
$$
\|u-\tilde u_h\|_V
\le C
\left(
\underbrace{\inf_{v_h\in V_h}\|u-v_h\|_V}_{\text{best approximation}}
+
\underbrace{\sup_{w_h\in V_h\setminus\{0\}}
\frac{|a(v_h,w_h)-a_h(v_h,w_h)|}{\|w_h\|_V}}_{\text{bilinear form consistency error}}
+
\underbrace{\sup_{w_h\in V_h\setminus\{0\}}
\frac{|F(w_h)-F_h(w_h)|}{\|w_h\|_V}}_{\text{load consistency error}}
\right).
$$

This is the full picture:

$$
\text{total error}
=
\text{approximation error}
+
\text{quadrature / mass-lumping error}.
$$

### 5. Interpretation for mass lumping

Mass lumping changes the discrete problem by replacing the exact mass/reaction inner product with a simpler quadrature rule, often producing a diagonal matrix.

So Strang's Lemma tells us:

- if $V_h$ approximates well, and
- if the quadrature error in $a_h$ and $F_h$ is small,

then the total error remains controlled.

In other words,
$$
\boxed{
\text{mass lumping is acceptable if the added consistency error is of higher order or comparable to the FE approximation error.}
}
$$
