# Linearization, ODE Stability, Semi-Discretization, and the $\Theta$-Method

## 1. Linearizing a nonlinear problem

### Nonlinear diffusion example

Consider the nonlinear PDE
$$
-\nabla \cdot \bigl(\mu(u)\nabla u\bigr)=f.
$$

If we have a nonlinear problem, we want to construct an iterative sequence
$$
u^{(k)} \to u,
$$
where each iterate produces the next one, $u^{(k)} \mapsto u^{(k+1)}$.
### Example: Navier--Stokes

For instance, for Navier--Stokes,
$$
\begin{aligned}
(u\cdot \nabla)u - \mu \Delta u + \nabla p &= 0,\\
\nabla \cdot u &= 0.
\end{aligned}
$$

### Picard iteration

Then we have what we call **Picard iteration**:
$$
\boxed{
(u^{(k)}\cdot \nabla)u^{(k+1)} - \mu \Delta u^{(k+1)} + \nabla p^{(k+1)} = 0
}
$$

That is, we freeze one factor of the nonlinear term using the previous iterate.

### Newton's method

For a scalar nonlinear equation $f(x)=0$, Newton's method is
$$
x^{(k+1)} = x^{(k)} - \frac{f(x^{(k)})}{f'(x^{(k)})}.
$$

In operator form, this becomes
$$
f'(x^{(k)})(x^{(k+1)}-x^{(k)})=-f(x^{(k)}).
$$

### Linearization of the convection term

If
$$
F(u)=(u\cdot \nabla)u-\mu \Delta u,
$$
then its directional derivative in the direction $v$ is
$$
\lim_{\delta\to 0}\frac{1}{\delta}\Bigl(((u+\delta v)\cdot \nabla)(u+\delta v)-(u\cdot \nabla)u\Bigr).
$$

This gives the usual linearization
$$
F'(u)[v]=(u\cdot \nabla)v+(v\cdot \nabla)u-\mu \Delta v.
$$


## 2. ODE stability

### General ODE

Consider
$$
\frac{dy}{dt}=f(t,y).
$$

A standard model problem is
$$
\frac{dy}{dt}=\lambda y,
\qquad \lambda<0.
$$

Then the exact solution satisfies
$$
y(t)\to 0 \quad \text{as } t\to \infty.
$$

The question is whether the numerical solution also decays.

### Forward Euler on the test equation

Applying Forward Euler,
$$
\frac{y^{n+1}-y^n}{\Delta t}=\lambda y^n,
$$
so
$$
y^{n+1}=(1+\lambda \Delta t)y^n.
$$

For stability, we need
$$
|1+\lambda \Delta t|<1.
$$

Since $\lambda<0$, write $\lambda=-|\lambda|$. Then
$$
|1-|\lambda|\Delta t|<1,
$$
which implies
$$
-1<1-|\lambda|\Delta t<1.
$$
Hence
$$
\Delta t<\frac{2}{|\lambda|}.
$$

### Orders of FE and BE

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{FE} &\quad O(\Delta t),\\
\text{BE} &\quad O(\Delta t).
\end{aligned}
$$

### Forward Euler and Backward Euler

Forward Euler:
$$
y^{n+1}=y^n+\Delta t\,f(y^n).
$$

Backward Euler:
$$
y^{n+1}=y^n+\Delta t\,f(y^{n+1}).
$$

For the scalar test equation, Backward Euler is unconditionally stable, while Forward Euler requires
$$
\Delta t<\frac{2}{|\lambda|}.
$$

### Linear systems

Now consider
$$
\frac{dy}{dt}=Ay+b.
$$

Suppose $A$ is diagonalizable:
$$
TAT^{-1}=D.
$$

Let
$$
\lambda = \operatorname{eig}(A),
\qquad \Re(\lambda)<0.
$$

After diagonalization, the system decouples into scalar modes. Each mode behaves like
$$
w_i(t)=Ce^{\lambda_i t}.
$$

If $\lambda_i=-\beta_i$ with $\beta_i>0$, then
$$
w_i(t)=Ce^{-\beta_i t}.
$$

So the transient decays exponentially.

The slowest decaying mode governs the long-time behavior.

## 3. Semi-discretization in space

Now we can write the weak problem as
$$
\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t},v\right)+a(u,v)=F(v).
$$

Assume, for example,
$$
u\in L^2(0,T;H_0^1(\Omega))\cap H^1(0,T;L^2(\Omega)).
$$

Let $V_h\subset H_0^1(\Omega)$, we approximate
$$
u_h(x,t)=\sum_j u_j(t)\psi_j(x),
$$
with test functions $v_h=\psi_i(x).$ By comparison, a spectral-type expansion would look like
$$
u(x,t)=\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} T_j(t)X_j(x).
$$

Then the semi-discrete problem is
$$
\left(\frac{\partial u_h}{\partial t},v_h\right)+a(u_h,v_h)=F(v_h).
$$

Substituting the finite element expansion,
$$
\left(\sum_j \frac{d u_j}{dt}\psi_j,\psi_i\right)
+\sum_j u_j(t)\,a(\psi_j,\psi_i)
=F(\psi_i).
$$

In matrix form this is
$$
M\frac{dU}{dt}+AU=F,
$$
or equivalently
$$
\frac{dU}{dt}=-M^{-1}AU+M^{-1}F.
$$

Here $M$ is the mass matrix and $A$ is the stiffness matrix.

## 4. Error analysis

Let
$$
e_h=u-u_h.
$$
Taking the difference between the continuous and discrete equations and testing with $v_h$, we obtain
$$
\left(\frac{\partial e_h}{\partial t},v_h\right)+a(e_h,v_h)=0.
$$

Now introduce an intermediate $w_h\in V_h$. Then
$$
e_h=(u-w_h)+(w_h-u_h).
$$

Using coercivity,
$$
\alpha \|e_h\|_{H^1}^2 \le a(e_h,e_h).
$$

Split the bilinear form:
$$
\begin{aligned}
a(e_h,e_h)
&=a(e_h,u-w_h)+a(e_h,w_h-u_h).
\end{aligned}
$$

For the first term,
$$
\begin{aligned}
a(e_h,u-w_h)
&\le \gamma \|e_h\|_{H^1}\|u-w_h\|_{H^1}\\
&\le \frac{\alpha}{2}\|e_h\|_{H^1}^2 + C\|u-w_h\|_{H^1}^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

For the second term,
$$
a(e_h,w_h-u_h)
=
-\left(\frac{\partial e_h}{\partial t},w_h-u_h\right).
$$

Then
$$
\begin{aligned}
-\left(\frac{\partial e_h}{\partial t},w_h-u_h\right)
&=
-\left(\frac{\partial e_h}{\partial t},w_h-u\right)
-\left(\frac{\partial e_h}{\partial t},u-u_h\right)\\
&=
-\left(\frac{\partial e_h}{\partial t},w_h-u\right)
-\frac{1}{2}\frac{d}{dt}\|e_h\|_{L^2}^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore,
$$
\frac{1}{2}\frac{d}{dt}\|e_h\|_{L^2}^2
+\frac{\alpha}{2}\|e_h\|_{H^1}^2
\le
C\|u-w_h\|_{H^1}^2
-\left(\frac{\partial e_h}{\partial t},w_h-u\right).
$$

This is the standard structure of the semi-discrete energy estimate.

## 5. Time discretization and the $\Theta$-method

Starting from
$$
\frac{du}{dt}=-M^{-1}Au+M^{-1}F,
$$
integrate in time. One popular choice is the **$\Theta$-method**:
$$
u^{n+1}
=
u^n
+
\Delta t\Bigl[
\Theta\,\mathrm{rhs}(t^{n+1},u^{n+1})
+
(1-\Theta)\,\mathrm{rhs}(t^n,u^n)
\Bigr],
\qquad \Theta\in[0,1].
$$

If $\Theta=\tfrac12$, this is the **Crank--Nicolson** method. A trapezoidal-type quadrature formula is
$$
\int_{t^n}^{t^{n+1}} f(t)\,dt
\approx
\frac{f(t^n)+f(t^{n+1})}{2}\,\Delta t.
$$

### Applying the $\Theta$-method to the linear system

For
$$
\frac{dU}{dt}=-M^{-1}AU,
$$
the method becomes
$$
U^{n+1}
=
U^n
+
\Theta \Delta t(-M^{-1}A)U^{n+1}
+
(1-\Theta)\Delta t(-M^{-1}A)U^n.
$$

If we diagonalize mode-by-mode, then for each eigenvalue $\lambda_i>0$ of $M^{-1}A$,
$$
(1+\Theta \Delta t\,\lambda_i)w_i^{n+1}
=
\bigl(1-(1-\Theta)\Delta t\,\lambda_i\bigr)w_i^n.
$$

So the amplification factor is
$$
g_i
=
\frac{1-(1-\Theta)\Delta t\,\lambda_i}{1+\Theta \Delta t\,\lambda_i}.
$$

### Stability

For the parameter $\Theta$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Theta \ge \frac12
&& \text{unconditionally stable},\\[4pt]
\Theta < \frac12
&& \text{conditionally stable}
\quad\underbrace{\text{with } \Delta t < \frac{2}{(1-2\Theta)\lambda_{\max}}}_{\text{scalar/mode stability condition}}.
\end{aligned}
$$
