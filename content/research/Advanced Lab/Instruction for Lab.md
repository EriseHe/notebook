The purpose of this course is to broaden and refine your laboratory and analytical skills, to further your mastery of several fields of physics, and to communicate your results in journal-paper-style written reports and a conference-style talk. 


LEARNING GOALS

Broaden and refine laboratory skills: You'll learn and practice skills such as wiring op amps, aligning lasers, calibrating a spectrometer, and using sophisticated instruments such as oscilloscopes and single-photon detectors.
Broaden and refine analytical skills: You'll practice skills such as solving differential equations, using Dirac notation, performing celestial-coordinate conversions, and curve-fitting.
Explore advanced physics: Through experiment, you'll explore advanced physics topics such as hyperfine structure, Bell inequalities, and quantum cryptography.
Communicate to learn. You'll use communication as a form of inquiry, invention, and reflection. (This language is from Emory's continuing communication GER, but it's true: When you prepare to communicate technical information, through writing or oral presentations, you really do learn a lot.)
Communicate flexibly. You'll communicate effectively for specified audiences in more than one mode, such as written, oral, and graphical modes.
You will write lab reports for the first three of your four modules. The lab reports will be written in the style of a journal paper. The target audience is a hypothetical editor or reviewer. For the fourth module, you will give a brief (5-10 minute) presentation, similar to a talk at a physics conference.

Guidelines for Writing Lab Reports (New for 2026!)

The goal is to write your lab reports as if they were journal papers. The target audience is a hypothetical journal editor or reviewer who has never seen our lab or read my lab manuals. You know more than your target audience; your goal is to teach the reader something they don't already know. Your goal is to guide the reader easily and effortlessly from the familiar (basic physics) to the unfamiliar (the theoretical and experimental details of your module). Every sentence should be part of a logical, coherent narrative. You're telling a story, with the goal of teaching; you're not just listing facts.

How to decide what to include: You may want to think about your results, including any figures and tables. What does the reader need to be taught in advance about the theory and the procedure, in order to understand your results? (Also include anything the manual specifically asks for. If the manual skips steps in derivations, fill them in. If the manual gives complete derivations, you may skip steps, explaining the missing steps in words.)

How much detail to include about the procedure: You want the reader to be able to reproduce your results, but you don't need to include every detail. You can assume the reader knows how to use an oscilloscope, for example. You don't need to explain how it works, or state that you plugged it in and turned it on. Please do not use the imperative ("align the laser"); instead, state what you did ("we aligned the laser"). You're not writing a manual; do not tell the reader what to do. It is acceptable and preferable to use first person (I/we) because this clarifies what you did, as opposed to what was done by others in the past.

Tenses: You may use both past and present tense, even in the same sentence ("we plugged in the oscilloscope because it doesn't do anything if it's not plugged in"), but don't randomly switch between past and present for no reason ("we turned on the laser, and we align the lenses"). Please avoid the word "would" unless you're 100% sure there's no other way to say what you mean.

Sections of the report: The titles of your sections can be anything you want, like chapter titles in a book. Your sections don't have to be Introduction, Procedure, etc., although they may. The only section that almost all journal papers have is the abstract.

The abstract: This is a single opening paragraph advertising your achievements, typically including specific, quantitative results. The abstract is often the only free section of a journal paper; the rest of the paper may be behind a paywall. Therefore, the abstract should function as a standalone document.

Clarity: Poets want every word to mean as many things as possible; science writers want exactly the opposite.  Every single sentence in your report should have only one possible interpretation. 

Significant digits: If you have three digits in your raw data, you should have no more than three digits in your final results. Ideally, you would use error analysis to determine the number of significant digits. 

Error analysis:  This is a sophisticated subject.  A good introduction is http://user.physics.unc.edu/~deardorf/uncertainty/UNCguide.htmlLinks to an external site., which also explains how the uncertainty determines the number of significant digits to use in the measured result.  A more advanced treatment is http://www.physics.umd.edu/courses/Phys261/F06/ErrorPropagation.pdfLinks to an external site..

Captions:  Figure captions go below the figure.  Table captions go above the table.  (This is an arbitrary but nearly universal rule; check any journal.)  Only the first letter of each sentence is capitalized.  The first sentence in a caption is typically a fragment, as in “Figure 1.  The apparatus.”  Subsequent sentences, if there are any, are complete sentences.  

Figure axes and table headers: These should be labeled with the name or symbol of the variable. The units should be in parentheses.

Equations:  Even sentences containing equations should be punctuated normally.  Avoid sentence fragments.  The format for equations is as follows:

 

We now will study the beloved Pythagorean theorem,

                        a2 + b2 = c2.                                                            (1)

Equation (1) shows....  (Notice the period in the previous line!)

 

Comma splices: These are common in informal writing, but I avoid them in formal scientific writing.

            INFORMAL (please avoid):  I love writing lab reports, I weep at the beauty of my work.

            FORMAL:  I love writing lab reports; I weep at the beauty of my work.

            FORMAL:  I love writing lab reports, and I weep at the beauty of my work.

            FORMAL:  I love writing lab reports. I weep at the beauty of my work.

Accommodate your reader:  Good scientific papers accommodate both the "lazy reader" and the "thorough reader":

The "lazy reader" reads only the abstract and the figure captions.  All the evidence that you've accomplished something interesting should appear in the abstract, figures, and figure captions.  The abstract should stand on its own and make sense to someone who hasn't read the rest of the paper.  Each figure with its caption should similarly make sense to someone who hasn't read the rest of the paper.
The "thorough reader" (possibly a "lazy reader" impressed by your abstract and figures) wants to read a continuous narrative from start to finish.  This reader will ignore your figures and tables until the text refers to them; you should guide your reader to each figure and table whenever you want your reader to look there. Authors cannot control where figures and tables appear in journal papers, so please do not write "as shown in the figure below." (Instead: "as shown in Fig. 1.")
Capitalization of terms: I believe the most common convention in physics journals is, i.e., Maxwell's equations, Newton's laws, etc. (Don't capitalize equations, laws, etc.)

Plagiarism: You are expected to write your laboratory reports on your own.  Any unique phrases lifted directly from any source, including the internet, must be enclosed in quotation marks with the source clearly identified. All sources that you studied to gain understanding while preparing to write your laboratory report, including links to the internet, should be listed at the end of your report under References. If you are ever in doubt, identify the source of your material. In particular, be sure to cite the source of any graphics you download into your report.  

I don't like to be bossy, but please avoid these forms of plagiarism:

Please do not copy entire paragraphs from any source.  In physics papers, we almost never quote exact text.
Please do not do a sentence-by-sentence paraphrase of any document. I know it's hard to come up with a unique explanation of a technical topic, but the idea is to understand it well enough to pull the sentences "out of your head" (or your notes).
Please do not use anyone else's data in lieu of data you're supposed to record yourself. You may, however, cite anyone else's data for purposes of discussion.
