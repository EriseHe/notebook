Object Reflection Paper

My archival object is a conference document titled “RECOMMENDATIONS TO THE CHINA PLANNING COMMITTEE AND THE COMMITTEE ON EAST ASIA,” produced as part of the China Planning Committee of the Committee on East Asia, “Conference on Medical and Health Work in China,” held March 21–22, 1945, in New York City. It is currently housed at Pitts Theology Library, Special Collections and Archives, within the collection American Missionary Aid to Post-WWII Reconstruction in East Asia (MSS 423), which is composed of documents connected to the Foreign Missions Conference of North America and spans summer 1944 to 1947.

Within the collection, the document is physically located inside Volume 1, the thickest of the three bound volumes in Box 1. My chosen object is a continuous block of typed pages (pp. 5-8) within the “Recommendation” section of the March 1945 conference materials. The object consists of typewriter-like text on lightly yellowed paper that is visibly aged over time: the ink looks unevenly faded in places, and some letters are partially missing on the page and require closer attention to read.

The text on these pages is dense, but the content is instantly identifiable by its centered or left-aligned section titles and consistently underlined headings. As a governance document, it is organized explicitly into capitalized alphabetical subsections (A, B, C…), followed by numbered lists and smaller alphabetical subpoints, which produce a hierarchical format that visually reads like official administrative prose. Some of the numbered items lead with the capitalized underlined word “RESOLVED.”

This document does not look like any personal account or humanitarian report at first glance. I notice how administrative and state-like the document feels visually, which distinguishes it from mere religious communication, such as charity appeals, testimony, or narrative. The format - especially the repeated “RESOLVED” - and the quiet anxiety in its phrasing, such as “we pledge” and “we strongly recommend”, made me feel like this is a blueprint for governance rather than a set of “recommendations”.

My subsequent impression was about the geography of authority. The document is “about China,” but it was produced in New York and addressed to committees embedded in U.S. institutional space. Given the distance between these places, I was surprised by the certainty of its language to presume China to be a place that needed coordination, redistribution, and standardization. The idea of post-war care was more like post-war administrative power. One thing that was unexpected to me was that they never mentioned anything directly related to gender. Nursing and midwifery were highly gendered domains during that time, but in the documents, they are elaborated in technical “standards” and “training” terms. This reminded me of Foucault’s argument that modern power is often exercised through “neutral” professional language in ways that can mask its political and gendered nature.

This object was created as part of the paperwork of a formal planning meeting, as previously mentioned, “Conference on Medical and Health Work in China”. The format

and the content of the object suggest that it may have been drafted by committee members or a designated secretary after discussion. It was possibly typed up for circulation as an official conference product in New York City. The intended recipients were named in the title: the China Planning Committee and the Committee on East Asia, as well as mission boards and affiliated agencies that are expected to implement these recommendations.

In 1945, a document like this would have been designed to be copied and mailed and likely circulated to justify decisions about U.S. missionary priorities. The original donor of this particular volume seems to be missing (only Volume 2 has the name of the donor on the back of the front page), and the librarian who processed this collection at Emory a decade ago was unsure about the creator. While it is currently located at Pitts Theology Library, it appears to have originally been housed in another private library that later closed, after which the materials were transferred to Emory’s library.

Here are some brainstormed questions for this object:

1. How did the U.S. missionary medical guidelines in Asia function as an extension of the U.S. settler-colonial model? (In what ways do U.S. missionary medical guideline in Asia and domestic settler colonialism share a common strategy or logic to eliminate indigenous cultures?)

2. How did the U.S. missionary medical planners define “modern” health work through standards? To what extent did such efforts reshape what counted as legitimate medical knowledge after World War II?

3. How does the conference’s call for trained midwives suggest a shift toward standardized medical authority over childbirth and maternal care?

4. How did missionary medical planners use nursing and midwifery training to reshape women’s roles and public health practices in post-war China?

5. What is the relationship between U.S. missionary medical planners’ efforts in post-war China and the institutionalization of preventive medicine in China?

6. How did, if at all, U.S. missionary medical planners help build public-health infrastructure in China that later might have supported state efforts to manage population health and reproduction?

Among these research questions, Question 6 really fascinates me, but the causal bridge might be too long or outside the scope of this class. Question 4 has the most explicit relation to the “doing gender” perspective we discussed in class. The selected conference recommendations directly demonstrate how gender is produced at the policy level. The political shift in institutional interaction within nursing and midwifery, highly feminized occupations during this period, redefines norms of accountability; that is, what kinds of feminized labor count as legitimate caregivers under what institutional

purposes (e.g., minimum of senior middle school education in Section C.2). The recommendations to make nursing schools "institutions separate from the hospitals" with independent budgets and governing bodies (Section C.1.a) act as an institutional enclosure to ensure that feminized labor is specifically “accountable” to Western medical standards. More literally, the organizational settings where women must “do gender” have been reconfigured across missions, schools, and hospitals in a West & Zimmerman sense.

This is precisely one site where childbirth is medicalized and becomes a legible instrument of public health administration instead of a traditionally informal, household-based practice. On one hand, this research question offers a primary example of how gender can be made institutional without being explicitly named. It also serves as a case study for us to observe the practical impact of missionary work as “soft” colonial governance through expertise rather than overt rules. On the other hand, by moving nursing and midwifery into formal training hierarchies, we can see U.S. missionary medical planning as an early form of biopower that normalizes an administrative approach to health and reproduction, compatible with later state population governance. It hence has genealogical significance for the history of Chinese biopolitics. In this context, Question 4, by its core inquiry, would ask us to re-articulate U.S. missionary activity as an implicit colonial installation of the gendered infrastructures that allow health and reproduction to become governable.
